# Properties of Relations (Section 9.1.4)

---

When analyzing a relation $R$ on a single set $A$, specific structural patterns—**Reflexivity, Symmetry, Antisymmetry, and Transitivity**—tell us how the elements within that set interact with one another.

---

### 1. Reflexive Relations

A relation is **reflexive** if every element in the set relates to itself.

> **Definition 3:** A relation $R$ on a set $A$ is called **reflexive** if $(a, a) \in R$ for every element $a \in A$.

* **Directed Graph (Digraph) Check:** A reflexive relation has a **loop at every single vertex** in its graph.

---

### 2. Symmetric and Antisymmetric Relations

These properties describe how pairs relate in reverse.

> **Definition 4 (Symmetric):** $R$ is **symmetric** if $(b, a) \in R$ whenever $(a, b) \in R$ for all $a, b \in A$.
> *(If $a$ is related to $b$, then $b$ must be related to $a$)*.

> **Definition 5 (Antisymmetric):** $R$ is **antisymmetric** if the only way both $(a, b) \in R$ and $(b, a) \in R$ can be true is if $a = b$.
> *(If $(a, b) \in R$ and $a \neq b$, then $(b, a) \notin R$)*.

* **⚠️ Warning Note:** A relation can be *neither* symmetric nor antisymmetric, or it can satisfy both (such as the empty relation, or a relation containing only loops). Do not assume symmetric and antisymmetric are direct opposites.

---

### 3. Transitive Relations

Transitivity captures the "chaining" logic of a relation.

> **Definition 6:** $R$ is **transitive** if whenever $(a, b) \in R$ and $(b, c) \in R$, then $(a, c) \in R$.

* **Directed Graph (Digraph) Check:** If there is a directed path of arrows from $a$ to $b$ and $b$ to $c$, there must also be a direct arrow pointing from $a$ to $c$.

---

### 4. Textbook Analysis: Examples

Let's evaluate a relation on the set $A = \{1, 2, 3, 4\}$:
$$R = \{(1, 1), (1, 2), (2, 2), (3, 3), (4, 4)\}$$

* **Reflexive?** **Yes**. It contains all loops: $(1, 1)$, $(2, 2)$, $(3, 3)$, and $(4, 4)$.
* **Symmetric?** **No**. The pair $(1, 2)$ belongs to $R$, but its reverse $(2, 1)$ is missing.
* **Antisymmetric?** **Yes**. The only pairs $(a, b)$ and $(b, a)$ present are the loops $(a, a)$, meaning $a = b$ is always satisfied when both directions exist.
* **Transitive?** **Yes**. All chaining sequences are satisfied (for example, $(1, 1) \in R$ and $(1, 2) \in R \implies (1, 2) \in R$).

---

### 🧠 Quick Check: Try it Yourself!

For the set $A = \{1, 2, 3\}$, consider the relation:
$$R = \{(1, 1), (1, 2), (2, 1), (2, 2), (3, 3)\}$$

1. Is this relation **reflexive**?
2. Is this relation **symmetric**?
3. Is this relation **transitive**?

---

### 💡 Solutions & Explanation

> [!NOTE]
> Here are the step-by-step verification answers for the check above:
> 
> 1. **Is the relation reflexive?** **Yes**.
>    * *Proof:* The set is $A = \{1, 2, 3\}$. The relation contains $(1, 1)$, $(2, 2)$, and $(3, 3)$. Since every element $a \in A$ has its corresponding loop $(a, a)$ in $R$, the relation is reflexive.
> 2. **Is the relation symmetric?** **Yes**.
>    * *Proof:* The non-loop pairs in $R$ are $(1, 2)$ and $(2, 1)$. Since both $(1, 2) \in R \implies (2, 1) \in R$ and $(2, 1) \in R \implies (1, 2) \in R$ are true, the relation is symmetric.
> 3. **Is the relation transitive?** **Yes**.
>    * *Proof:* We check all chaining paths of length two $(a, b) \in R$ and $(b, c) \in R$ to verify if $(a, c) \in R$:
>      * For path $(1, 2)$ and $(2, 1)$: Since $(1, 1) \in R$ is true, the chain holds.
>      * For path $(2, 1)$ and $(1, 2)$: Since $(2, 2) \in R$ is true, the chain holds.
>      * Any chain involving loops (such as $(1, 1)$ and $(1, 2)$, which yields $(1, 2)$) is trivially satisfied.
>      Because all chains successfully resolve, the relation is transitive.
