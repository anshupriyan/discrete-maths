# Geometric and Arithmetic Progressions (Section 2.4.2)

---

### 1. Geometric Progressions

A **geometric progression** is a sequence where each term after the first is obtained by multiplying the previous term by a fixed, non-zero number called the **common ratio**.

> **Definition 2:** A geometric progression is a sequence of the form:
> $$a, ar, ar^2, \dots, ar^n, \dots$$
> where the initial term $a$ and the common ratio $r$ are real numbers.

* **The Functional Analogy:** A geometric progression is the discrete equivalent of an exponential function $f(x) = a \cdot r^x$, where the exponent $n$ increases by $1$ at each step.

#### **Textbook Example 2:**
Here are three geometric progressions, all evaluated starting at index $n = 0$:

1. **Sequence $\{b_n\}$ where $b_n = (-1)^n$:**
   * **Initial term ($a$):** $1$
   * **Common ratio ($r$):** $-1$
   * **Terms ($b_0, b_1, b_2, \dots$):** **$1, -1, 1, -1, 1, \dots$**

2. **Sequence $\{c_n\}$ where $c_n = 2 \cdot 5^n$:**
   * **Initial term ($a$):** $2$
   * **Common ratio ($r$):** $5$
   * **Terms ($c_0, c_1, c_2, \dots$):** **$2, 10, 50, 250, 1250, \dots$**

3. **Sequence $\{d_n\}$ where $d_n = 6 \cdot (1/3)^n$:**
   * **Initial term ($a$):** $6$
   * **Common ratio ($r$):** $1/3$
   * **Terms ($d_0, d_1, d_2, \dots$):** **$6, 2, \frac{2}{3}, \frac{2}{9}, \frac{2}{27}, \dots$**

---

### 2. Arithmetic Progressions

An **arithmetic progression** is a sequence where each term after the first is obtained by adding a fixed real number called the **common difference** to the previous term.

> **Definition 3:** An arithmetic progression is a sequence of the form:
> $$a, a + d, a + 2d, \dots, a + nd, \dots$$
> where the initial term $a$ and the common difference $d$ are real numbers.

* **The Functional Analogy:** An arithmetic progression is the discrete equivalent of a linear function $f(x) = dx + a$.

#### **Textbook Example 3:**
Here are two arithmetic progressions, starting at index $n = 0$:

1. **Sequence $\{s_n\}$ where $s_n = -1 + 4n$:**
   * **Initial term ($a$):** $-1$
   * **Common difference ($d$):** $4$
   * **Terms ($s_0, s_1, s_2, \dots$):** **$-1, 3, 7, 11, \dots$**

2. **Sequence $\{t_n\}$ where $t_n = 7 - 3n$:**
   * **Initial term ($a$):** $7$
   * **Common difference ($d$):** $-3$
   * **Terms ($t_0, t_1, t_2, \dots$):** **$7, 4, 1, -2, \dots$**

### đź§  Quick Check: Try it Yourself!

Identify whether each sequence is a **geometric progression**, an **arithmetic progression**, or **neither**. Find its matching parameter ($r$ or $d$):
1. $3, 6, 12, 24, 48, \dots$
2. $15, 10, 5, 0, -5, \dots$

---

### đź’ˇ Solutions & Explanation

> [!NOTE]
> Here are the step-by-step verification answers for the check above:
> 
> 1. **$3, 6, 12, 24, 48, \dots$:** **Geometric Progression** with common ratio **$r = 2$**.
>    * *Proof:* The quotient of consecutive terms is constant:
>      $$\frac{6}{3} = 2, \quad \frac{12}{6} = 2, \quad \frac{24}{12} = 2, \quad \frac{48}{24} = 2$$
>      Since each term is multiplied by a fixed value of $2$ to get the next term, it is geometric with $a = 3$ and $r = 2$.
> 2. **$15, 10, 5, 0, -5, \dots$:** **Arithmetic Progression** with common difference **$d = -5$**.
>    * *Proof:* The difference between consecutive terms is constant:
>      $$10 - 15 = -5, \quad 5 - 10 = -5, \quad 0 - 5 = -5, \quad -5 - 0 = -5$$
>      Since we add a fixed value of $-5$ to each term to get the next term, it is arithmetic with $a = 15$ and $d = -5$.
