# Multisets (Section 2.2.5)

---

### 1. What is a Multiset?

In an ordinary set, order does not matter and elements cannot be repeated—listing an item more than once changes nothing. However, in many real-world computer science and mathematical contexts, the number of times an element occurs is significant.

> **Definition:** A **multiset** (short for *multiple-membership set*) is an unordered collection of elements where an element can occur as a member more than once.

The term *multiset* was coined by Nicolaas Govert de Bruijn in the 1970s, but the concept dates back to the 12th-century work of the Indian mathematician **Bhaskaracharya**.

---

### 2. Notations and Multiplicity

There are two primary ways to write out a multiset:

#### **A. Roster Notation**
List out duplicated elements explicitly inside standard braces:
* **Example:** $\{a, a, a, b, b\}$ is a multiset containing the element $a$ three times and the element $b$ twice.
* *Note:* You must state explicitly that you are working with multisets, or else an ordinary set reader would simplify this to $\{a, b\}$.

#### **B. Multiplicity Notation**
To avoid ambiguity, a more formal notation represents a multiset as:
$$\{m_1 \cdot a_1, m_2 \cdot a_2, \dots, m_r \cdot a_r\}$$
Where each $a_i$ is an element, and each $m_i$ is its **multiplicity** (the number of times it occurs in the multiset).

* Using this notation, $\{a, a, a, b, b\}$ becomes: **$\{3 \cdot a, 2 \cdot b\}$**.
* Any element not explicitly featured in the multiset is considered to have a multiplicity of $0$.

#### **Cardinality of a Multiset**
The cardinality of a multiset is defined as the **sum of the multiplicities** of its elements.
* For $\{3 \cdot a, 2 \cdot b\}$, the cardinality is $3 + 2 = 5$.

### 3. Multiset Operations

When combining two multisets $P$ and $Q$, we use rules based on their elements' multiplicities:

1. **Union ($P \cup Q$):** Take the **maximum** multiplicity of each element.
2. **Intersection ($P \cap Q$):** Take the **minimum** multiplicity of each element.
3. **Difference ($P - Q$):** Subtract the multiplicity in $Q$ from the multiplicity in $P$. If the result is negative, cap the multiplicity at **0**.
4. **Sum ($P + Q$):** **Add** the multiplicities of the elements together. *(Note: Ordinary sets do not have a sum operation, making this unique to multisets).*

#### **Textbook Example 21:**
Suppose that $P$ and $Q$ are the multisets:
* $P = \{4 \cdot a, 1 \cdot b, 3 \cdot c\}$
* $Q = \{3 \cdot a, 4 \cdot b, 2 \cdot d\}$

Find $P \cup Q$, $P \cap Q$, $P - Q$, and $P + Q$.

* **Solutions:**
  * **Union ($P \cup Q$):** Check the highest count for each element:
    * For $a$: $\max(4, 3) = 4$
    * For $b$: $\max(1, 4) = 4$
    * For $c$: $\max(3, 0) = 3$
    * For $d$: $\max(0, 2) = 2$
    $$\mathbf{P \cup Q = \{4 \cdot a, 4 \cdot b, 3 \cdot c, 2 \cdot d\}}$$

  * **Intersection ($P \cap Q$):** Check the lowest shared count for each element:
    * For $a$: $\min(4, 3) = 3$
    * For $b$: $\min(1, 4) = 1$
    * For $c$: $\min(3, 0) = 0$
    * For $d$: $\min(0, 2) = 0$
    $$\mathbf{P \cap Q = \{3 \cdot a, 1 \cdot b\}}$$

  * **Difference ($P - Q$):** Subtract $Q$'s counts from $P$'s counts:
    * For $a$: $4 - 3 = 1$
    * For $b$: $1 - 4 = -3 \rightarrow \text{capped at } 0$
    * For $c$: $3 - 0 = 3$
    * For $d$: $0 - 2 = -2 \rightarrow \text{capped at } 0$
    $$\mathbf{P - Q = \{1 \cdot a, 3 \cdot c\}}$$

  * **Sum ($P + Q$):** Add the multiplicities directly together:
    * For $a$: $4 + 3 = 7$
    * For $b$: $1 + 4 = 5$
    * For $c$: $3 + 0 = 3$
    * For $d$: $0 + 2 = 2$
    $$\mathbf{P + Q = \{7 \cdot a, 5 \cdot b, 3 \cdot c, 2 \cdot d\}}$$

### 🧠 Quick Check: Try it Yourself!

Let $A$ and $B$ be the multisets $\{3 \cdot a, 2 \cdot b, 1 \cdot c\}$ and $\{2 \cdot a, 3 \cdot b, 4 \cdot d\}$, respectively.
Find:
1. $A \cap B$
2. $A - B$

---

### 💡 Solutions & Explanation

> [!NOTE]
> Here are the step-by-step verification answers for the check above:
> 
> 1. **$A \cap B$:** $\{2 \cdot a, 2 \cdot b\}$.
>    * *Why?* Intersection takes the minimum multiplicity for each element:
>      * For $a$: $\min(3, 2) = 2$
>      * For $b$: $\min(2, 3) = 2$
>      * For $c$: $\min(1, 0) = 0$ (not in intersection)
>      * For $d$: $\min(0, 4) = 0$ (not in intersection)
> 2. **$A - B$:** $\{1 \cdot a, 1 \cdot c\}$.
>    * *Why?* Difference subtracts $B$'s multiplicities from $A$'s and caps negative results at $0$:
>      * For $a$: $3 - 2 = 1$
>      * For $b$: $2 - 3 = -1 \rightarrow \text{capped at } 0$
>      * For $c$: $1 - 0 = 1$
>      * For $d$: $0 - 4 = -4 \rightarrow \text{capped at } 0$
