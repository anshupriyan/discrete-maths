# Cartesian Products (Section 2.1.6)

---

### Why do we need Cartesian Products?

Standard sets are completely unordered. However, in many mathematical and computer science applications, the order of elements is absolutely critical. To represent ordered collections, discrete mathematics introduces a structure called **ordered $n$-tuples**.

---

### 1. Ordered $n$-tuples and Ordered Pairs

> **Definition 7:** The **ordered $n$-tuple** $(a_1, a_2, \dots, a_n)$ is the ordered collection that has $a_1$ as its first element, $a_2$ as its second element, $\dots$, and $a_n$ as its $n$th element.

* **Equality Rule:** Two ordered $n$-tuples are mathematically equal if and only if every single corresponding pair of their elements is identical. Formally:
  $$(a_1, a_2, \dots, a_n) = (b_1, b_2, \dots, b_n) \iff a_i = b_i \text{ for } i = 1, 2, \dots, n$$

* **Ordered Pairs:** An ordered 2-tuple is simply called an **ordered pair**. Written as $(a, b)$, it behaves such that $(a, b)$ and $(b, a)$ are **never equal** unless $a = b$.

---

### 2. Defining the Cartesian Product

Now that we have a way to write ordered pairs, we can define a method to cross-multiply two sets together.

> **Definition 8:** Let $A$ and $B$ be sets. The **Cartesian product** of $A$ and $B$, denoted by **$A \times B$**, is the set of all ordered pairs $(a, b)$, where $a \in A$ and $b \in B$.

Using set-builder notation, the Cartesian product is defined as:
$$A \times B = \{(a, b) \mid a \in A \land b \in B\}$$

---

### 3. Core Textbook Examples

#### Example 17: Computing a Finite Product
What is the Cartesian product of $A = \{1, 2\}$ and $B = \{a, b, c\}$?

* **Solution:** Pair every element in $A$ with every element in $B$ in an ordered manner:
  $$A \times B = \{(1, a), (1, b), (1, c), (2, a), (2, b), (2, c)\}$$

#### Example 18: Order Matters!
Show that the Cartesian product $B \times A$ is not equal to $A \times B$ using the sets above.

* **Solution:** If we compute $B \times A$, the elements from $B$ must come first:
  $$B \times A = \{(a, 1), (a, 2), (b, 1), (b, 2), (c, 1), (c, 2)\}$$
* Because the ordered pair $(1, a) \neq (a, 1)$, the resulting sets are entirely different.
* **⚠️ The Commutative Law Rule:** $A \times B$ and $B \times A$ are **never equal** unless $A = \emptyset$, $B = \emptyset$ (making the product $\emptyset$), or $A = B$.

---

### 4. Products of Multiple Sets

The Cartesian product easily extends beyond just two sets.

> **Definition 9:** The Cartesian product of the sets $A_1, A_2, \dots, A_n$, denoted by $A_1 \times A_2 \times \dots \times A_n$, is the set of ordered $n$-tuples $(a_1, a_2, \dots, a_n)$, where $a_i \in A_i$ for $i = 1, 2, \dots, n$.

#### Exponents with Sets
When multiplying a set by itself, the book introduces shorthand power notation:
* $A^2 = A \times A$
* $A^3 = A \times A \times A$

**Example 20 from the book:** If $A = \{1, 2\}$, then:
$$A^2 = \{(1, 1), (1, 2), (2, 1), (2, 2)\}$$
$$A^3 = \{(1, 1, 1), (1, 1, 2), (1, 2, 1), (1, 2, 2), (2, 1, 1), (2, 1, 2), (2, 2, 1), (2, 2, 2)\}$$

---

### 5. Connecting Products to "Relations" (Preview to Ch 9)

The book notes that Cartesian products are the exact foundation for **Relations**.

* Any **subset** $R$ of a Cartesian product $A \times B$ is formally called a **binary relation** from $A$ to $B$. You will use this heavily as you transition further into chapter sections and Chapter 9!

### 🧠 Quick Study Question

Suppose you have two sets:
* $M = \{5\}$
* $N = \{6, 7\}$

1. What is $M \times N$?
2. What is $N \times M$?
3. If a set $X$ has $m$ elements and set $Y$ has $n$ elements, what is the total size (cardinality) of $|X \times Y|$?

---

### 💡 Solutions & Explanation

> [!NOTE]
> Here are the step-by-step verification answers for the check above:
> 
> 1. **$M \times N$:** $\{(5, 6), (5, 7)\}$. Elements from $M$ are paired as the first coordinate, and elements from $N$ are paired as the second coordinate.
> 2. **$N \times M$:** $\{(6, 5), (7, 5)\}$. Elements from $N$ act as the first coordinate, and elements from $M$ act as the second coordinate.
> 3. **Cardinality rule of Cartesian Products ($|X \times Y|$):** $m \cdot n$. The total size of a Cartesian product is the product of the individual cardinalities of the sets ($|X| \cdot |Y|$). For instance, in Example 17, $|A| \cdot |B| = 2 \cdot 3 = 6$.
