# Chapter 5: Induction and Recursion (Overview)

Before we dive into the math, let's look at the big picture. Why do computer scientists combine **induction** and **recursion** into a single chapter?

They are actually two sides of the exact same coin:
* **Recursion (Top-Down):** Splitting a massive computational problem into smaller, identical versions of itself until it hits a small baseline.
* **Induction (Bottom-Up):** Starting at a small baseline truth, and mathematically scaling up to prove that a rule holds for an infinite sequence of cases.

---

## 5.1 Mathematical Induction

### 5.1.1 Introduction

Suppose we want to prove a statement that asserts a property holds true for **all positive integers**. For example:
* *“The sum of the first $n$ positive integers is $n(n + 1) / 2$.”*
* *“A computer algorithm processing a list of $n$ elements will terminate in at most $n^2$ operations.”*

Up to this point, we have used *Direct Proofs*, *Contraposition*, and *Contradiction* (from Section 1.7). While those tools are great, they can struggle when a proposition explicitly changes dynamically with an infinite counter variable $n$.

To solve this, we use **Mathematical Induction**.

> **The Conceptual Analogy: Infinite Dominoes**
> Imagine an infinite line of dominoes standing on end, labeled $1, 2, 3, \dots, n, \dots$.
> How can you guarantee that **every single domino will fall** without physically tipping over each one individually?
> 1. You must tip over the **very first domino** (Domino 1).
> 2. You must ensure that the dominoes are spaced perfectly such that **if any arbitrary domino falls (Domino $k$), it is guaranteed to knock over its immediate neighbor (Domino $k + 1$)**.
> 
> If both conditions are met, a chain reaction triggers, and every domino in the infinite line falls down.

---

### 5.1.2 Mathematical Induction (The Formal Rule)

Let's translate this domino chain reaction into a formal, rigorous mathematical blueprint.

To prove that a propositional function $P(n)$ is true for all positive integers $n$ (where our domain of discourse is typically $\{1, 2, 3, \dots\}$), a proof by mathematical induction consists of **two explicit, mandatory steps**:

> ### The Induction Blueprint
> 1. **BASIS STEP:** Show that the proposition $P(1)$ is true. (Knocking over the first domino).
> 2. **INDUCTIVE STEP:** Show that the conditional statement **$P(k) \rightarrow P(k + 1)$** is true for every positive integer $k$. (The bridging mechanism).

Written out as a formal rule of inference using our quantified predicate logic notation, it looks like this:
$$[P(1) \dots \forall k (P(k) \rightarrow P(k + 1))] \rightarrow \forall n P(n)$$

---

### The Secret Engine: The Inductive Hypothesis

When executing the **Inductive Step**, your goal is to prove the implication $P(k) \rightarrow P(k + 1)$.

How do we prove an implication? We use a direct approach: we **assume the hypothesis is true**.
* **The Inductive Hypothesis:** The explicit assumption that $P(k)$ is true for an arbitrary positive integer $k$.

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **Common Student Confusion:** Students often look at this and say, *"Wait! Isn't assuming $P(k)$ is true circular reasoning? Aren't we assuming what we are trying to prove?"*
> **No!** We are *not* assuming $P(n)$ is true for all numbers. We are merely proving a conditional bridge: *“**If** it happens to be true for some mystery number $k$, then it forces the next number $k+1$ to be true.”* Once you successfully build that conditional bridge, the **Basis Step** acts as the spark. The basis step proves $P(1)$ is true. The inductive step then automatically states: *"Since $P(1)$ is true, $P(2)$ is true. Since $P(2)$ is true, $P(3)$ is true..."* cascading all the way to infinity.

### Detailed Textbook Walkthroughs

Let's carefully construct two classic proofs using this exact blueprint.

#### EXAMPLE 1: Proving a Summation Formula
> **Theorem:** Show that the sum of the first $n$ positive odd integers is $n^2$. That is, prove:
> $$1 + 3 + 5 + \dots + (2n - 1) = n^2$$

Let $P(n)$ denote the proposition that this summation equation holds for a given integer $n$.

* **Step 1: The Basis Step ($n = 1$)**
  We must test if $P(1)$ is true.
  * Left-Hand Side (the first term): $1$
  * Right-Hand Side ($n^2$ where $n=1$): $1^2 = 1$
  Since $1 = 1$, the statement $P(1)$ is **True**. The basis step is complete.
* **Step 2: The Inductive Step**
  We assume the **Inductive Hypothesis** is true for an arbitrary integer $k$. That is, we assume $P(k)$ is a verified fact:
  $$\text{Assumption } P(k): \quad 1 + 3 + 5 + \dots + (2k - 1) = k^2$$
  Now, our ultimate goal is to show that this assumption forces $P(k + 1)$ to be true. Let's write down what $P(k + 1)$ looks like so we know our target:
  $$\text{Target } P(k + 1): \quad 1 + 3 + 5 + \dots + (2k - 1) + (2(k + 1) - 1) = (k + 1)^2$$
  Simplifying the last input term: $2(k+1) - 1 = 2k + 2 - 1 = \mathbf{2k + 1}$.
* **Step 3: The Algebraic Bridge**
  Let's write down the left-hand side of our target expression $P(k + 1)$:
  $$\underbrace{1 + 3 + 5 + \dots + (2k - 1)}_{\text{Hypothesis LHS}} + (2k + 1)$$
  Notice that the first $k$ terms are *exactly* the left-hand side of our Inductive Hypothesis! Because we assumed the hypothesis is true, we can completely substitute $k^2$ in place of that entire block:
  $$= k^2 + (2k + 1)$$
  $$= k^2 + 2k + 1$$
  Using standard algebra, we recognize that $k^2 + 2k + 1$ factors perfectly into a perfect square:
  $$= (k + 1)^2$$
  This matches our target right-hand side exactly! We have successfully shown that $P(k) \rightarrow P(k + 1)$ is true.

**Conclusion:** Both the basis step and inductive step are verified. Therefore, by the principle of mathematical induction, the theorem holds true for all positive integers $n$. $\blacksquare$

---

#### EXAMPLE 2: Proving an Inequality
Induction isn't just for equality formulas; it handles inequalities beautifully.
> **Theorem:** Prove that $n < 2^n$ for all positive integers $n$.

Let $P(n)$ be the assertion that $n < 2^n$.

* **Step 1: The Basis Step ($n = 1$)**
  We test $P(1)$: Is $1 < 2^1$?
  Since $1 < 2$ is true, $P(1)$ is **True**.
* **Step 2: The Inductive Step**
  We assume the **Inductive Hypothesis** holds true for an arbitrary positive integer $k$:
  $$\text{Assumption } P(k): \quad k < 2^k$$
  Our target is to prove $P(k + 1)$, which states:
  $$\text{Target } P(k + 1): \quad k + 1 < 2^{k + 1}$$
* **Step 3: The Algebraic Bridge**
  Let's start with the left side of our target, $k + 1$. We know from our inductive hypothesis assumption that $k < 2^k$. Let's add $1$ to both sides of that inequality:
  $$k + 1 < 2^k + 1$$
  Now we need a logical stepping stone. We know that for any positive integer $k \ge 1$, the value $1$ is less than or equal to $2^k$ (since $1 \le 2, 1 \le 4, \dots$). Let's use this to bound our expression:
  $$2^k + 1 \le 2^k + 2^k$$
  Fusing identical terms together ($x + x = 2x$):
  $$2^k + 2^k = 2 \cdot (2^k)$$
  Using basic laws of exponents ($2^1 \cdot 2^k = 2^{k+1}$):
  $$2 \cdot (2^k) = 2^{k + 1}$$
  Let's look at the outer edges of our continuous inequality chain:
  $$k + 1 < 2^{k+1}$$
  The target is established! The inductive step is proven.

**Conclusion:** By the principle of mathematical induction, $n < 2^n$ is true for all positive integers $n$. $\blacksquare$

## 5.1.3 Why Mathematical Induction is Valid

An important question to answer is why mathematical induction is a valid proof technique. The validity of mathematical induction is based on a fundamental axiom of the set of integers: the **Well-Ordering Property**.

> **The Well-Ordering Property:** Every nonempty set of nonnegative integers has a least (smallest) element.

Let's use a **Proof by Contradiction** (a framework we mastered in Section 1.7.7) to show why mathematical induction *must* work if the Well-Ordering Property is true.

### The Contradiction Proof Framework
1. Suppose we have verified both the **Basis Step** ($P(1)$ is true) and the **Inductive Step** ($\forall k (P(k) \rightarrow P(k+1))$ is true).
2. Now, let's assume the exact opposite of our final goal: **Assume that mathematical induction is invalid**, meaning there is *at least one* positive integer for which $P(n)$ is false.
3. Let $S$ be the collection of all positive integers $n$ for which $P(n)$ is false. Because of our assumption in step 2, $S$ is a **nonempty** set of positive integers.
4. By the **Well-Ordering Property**, because $S$ is nonempty, it *must* contain a **least element**, which we will name $m$. This element $m$ is the absolute smallest integer for which $P(m)$ fails.
5. We know that $m$ cannot equal $1$ because our Basis Step explicitly proved that $P(1)$ is completely true. Therefore, $m$ must be strictly greater than $1$ ($m > 1$).
6. Because $m > 1$, the integer immediately preceding it, **$m - 1$**, is a valid positive integer.
7. Since $m$ is the absolute *smallest* integer for which $P(n)$ is false, the statement must be completely true for the number before it. Therefore, **$P(m - 1)$ is True**.
8. Now, let's look at our verified Inductive Step: $\forall k (P(k) \rightarrow P(k+1))$. If we set our arbitrary index variable $k = m - 1$, the conditional bridge states:
   $$P(m - 1) \rightarrow P((m - 1) + 1) \quad \equiv \quad P(m - 1) \rightarrow P(m)$$
9. By applying **Modus Ponens** to the facts "$P(m - 1)$ is true" and "$P(m - 1) \rightarrow P(m)$ is true," we are forced to deduce that **$P(m)$ is True**.

**The Crash Point:** Look at our tracking steps. Step 4 states that $P(m)$ is **False** (because $m$ belongs to the set $S$), but Step 9 states that $P(m)$ is **True**. This is a direct logical contradiction ($P(m) \land \neg P(m)$).

Because our mathematical steps were completely legal, the fault lies entirely with our opening assumption that induction is invalid. Therefore, the principle of mathematical induction is proven to be completely valid.

## 5.1.4 Choosing the Correct Basis Step

Up to this point, our examples have used $n = 1$ as the Basis Step. However, many theorems are not true for the very first positive integers, but become universally true after a certain point.

Your textbook highlights that the domain of discourse does not have to be the set of all positive integers. It can be the set of all integers greater than or equal to a specific starting integer $b$.

> **The Generalized Induction Rule:**
> To prove that $P(n)$ is true for all integers $n \ge b$ (where $b$ can be any integer, including $0$, negative numbers, or positive integers):
> 1. **Basis Step:** Prove that $P(b)$ is true.
> 2. **Inductive Step:** Prove that if $P(k)$ is true for an arbitrary integer $k \ge b$, then $P(k+1)$ is true.

### Textbook Walkthrough: Example 8 (Nonzero Starting Point)
Let's see how a divisibility problem uses a different baseline to lock down its structure.
> **Theorem:** Use mathematical induction to prove that $n^3 - n$ is divisible by 3 whenever $n$ is a positive integer.

Let $P(n)$ denote the proposition: "$n^3 - n$ is divisible by 3."

* **Step 1: The Basis Step ($n = 1$)**
  We test our lowest boundary constraint:
  $$1^3 - 1 = 1 - 1 = 0$$
  Is $0$ divisible by $3$? Yes, because $0 = 3 \cdot 0$. Thus, $P(1)$ is **True**.
* **Step 2: The Inductive Step**
  We assume the **Inductive Hypothesis** is true for an arbitrary integer $k$:
  $$\text{Assumption } P(k): \quad k^3 - k \text{ is divisible by 3. (Meaning } k^3 - k = 3c \text{ for an integer } c\text{).}$$
  Our target is to show that $P(k+1)$ is true, which states:
  $$\text{Target } P(k+1): \quad (k+1)^3 - (k+1) \text{ is divisible by 3.}$$
* **Step 3: The Algebraic Bridge**
  Let's expand our target expression using standard binomial expansion:
  $$(k+1)^3 - (k+1) = (k^3 + 3k^2 + 3k + 1) - (k + 1)$$
  Let's remove the parentheses and clean up the constants ($+1$ and $-1$ cancel out):
  $$= k^3 + 3k^2 + 3k - k$$
  Now, let's rearrange the terms to group our Inductive Hypothesis piece ($k^3 - k$) together at the front:
  $$= \mathbf{(k^3 - k)} + 3k^2 + 3k$$
  Look closely at this rearranged expression:
  * The first block $\mathbf{(k^3 - k)}$ is divisible by 3 based on our explicit Inductive Hypothesis assumption.
  * The remaining block can be factored into $3(k^2 + k)$, which is explicitly a multiple of 3.
  Let's combine them formally:
  $$= 3c + 3(k^2 + k) = 3(c + k^2 + k)$$
  Because the entire expression simplifies down to $3 \times (\text{an integer})$, $(k+1)^3 - (k+1)$ is cleanly proven to be divisible by 3.

**Conclusion:** The inductive step holds, and the theorem is proven for all positive integers. $\blacksquare$

## 5.1.5 Guidelines for Proofs by Mathematical Induction

To ensure your induction proofs are rigorous and free of logical bugs, follow these five essential structural guidelines established in your textbook:
1. **Express the statement clearly:** Explicitly define your propositional function $P(n)$ before writing any math. State what your variable $n$ represents and identify the domain of discourse.
2. **Explicitly write out the Basis Step:** Do not skip checking the baseline. State the base value $b$, show the calculation for both sides of the expression, and confirm that they match or satisfy the condition.
3. **State the Inductive Hypothesis clearly:** Write out the exact expression for $P(k)$. Explicitly state the assumption: *"Assume $P(k)$ is true for an arbitrary integer $k \ge b$."*
4. **Identify your target finish line:** Before manipulating equations, write down what $P(k+1)$ looks like. This gives you a clear algebraic target to aim for.
5. **Construct the algebraic bridge transparently:** When performing the inductive step, explicitly point out exactly where you substitute the inductive hypothesis. Your proof should move cleanly from line to line until the target $P(k+1)$ is isolated.

## 5.1.6 The Good and the Bad of Mathematical Induction

Understanding the scope and limitations of mathematical induction is essential for applying it correctly in computer science.

### The Good (The Advantages)
* **Infinite Scalability:** It allows you to prove a statement holds for an infinite sequence of cases using just a finite, two-step proof template.
* **Algorithmic Alignment:** It perfectly maps to **loops** and **recursive function blocks** in software design. Proving a loop invariant using induction is a primary way to mathematically verify that a program works accurately.

### The Bad (The Limitations)
* **It Cannot Find the Formula:** Induction is strictly a *verification technique*, not a discovery tool. To use induction, you must already know the exact formula or inequality beforehand. It cannot help you discover that a sum equals $n^2$; it can only confirm it once someone hands you the guess.
* **No Conceptual Insight:** An induction proof often relies on clever algebraic tricks or substitutions to link $k$ to $k+1$. While this securely establishes *that* a theorem is true, it sometimes fails to explain *why* the underlying pattern happens in the first place compared to a direct geometric or combinatorial proof.

### EXAMPLE 3: The Classical Summation Formula

> **Theorem:** Use mathematical induction to prove that the sum of the first $n$ positive integers is $n(n + 1)/2$. That is, show that:
> $$1 + 2 + 3 + \dots + n = \frac{n(n + 1)}{2}$$
> for all positive integers $n$.

Let $P(n)$ be the proposition that the identity holds for $n$.

* **Step 1: Basis Step ($n = 1$)**
  We must show that $P(1)$ is true.
  * Left-Hand Side (first term): $1$
  * Right-Hand Side: $\frac{1(1 + 1)}{2} = \frac{2}{2} = 1$
  Since both sides equal $1$, $P(1)$ is **True**.
* **Step 2: Inductive Step**
  We assume the **Inductive Hypothesis** is true for an arbitrary positive integer $k$:
  $$\text{Assumption } P(k): \quad 1 + 2 + 3 + \dots + k = \frac{k(k + 1)}{2}$$
  Our target is to show that this forces $P(k + 1)$ to be true:
  $$\text{Target } P(k + 1): \quad 1 + 2 + 3 + \dots + k + (k + 1) = \frac{(k + 1)((k + 1) + 1)}{2} = \frac{(k + 1)(k + 2)}{2}$$
* **Step 3: The Algebraic Bridge**
  Let's write down the left side of our target $P(k+1)$ and look for our hypothesis:
  $$\underbrace{1 + 2 + 3 + \dots + k}_{\text{Substitute from } P(k)} + (k + 1)$$
  Substitute $\frac{k(k + 1)}{2}$ in place of the underlined sum:
  $$= \frac{k(k + 1)}{2} + (k + 1)$$
  Find a common denominator to merge the terms:
  $$= \frac{k(k + 1)}{2} + \frac{2(k + 1)}{2} = \frac{k(k + 1) + 2(k + 1)}{2}$$
  Factor out the common binomial term $(k + 1)$ from the numerator:
  $$= \frac{(k + 1)(k + 2)}{2}$$
  This matches our target exactly! The inductive step is complete, proving the theorem for all positive integers $n$. $\blacksquare$

### EXAMPLE 4: Sum of a Geometric Series

> **Theorem:** Prove that if $r \neq 1$, then for all nonnegative integers $n$:
> $$\sum_{j=0}^{n} ar^j = a + ar + ar^2 + \dots + ar^n = \frac{ar^{n+1} - a}{r - 1}$$

Let $P(n)$ be the statement of the formula. Notice the theorem specifies **nonnegative integers**, meaning the domain starts at $0$.

* **Step 1: Basis Step ($n = 0$)**
  We must test $P(0)$:
  * Left-Hand Side (the zeroth term): $ar^0 = a \cdot 1 = a$
  * Right-Hand Side: $\frac{ar^{0+1} - a}{r - 1} = \frac{ar - a}{r - 1} = \frac{a(r - 1)}{r - 1} = a$
  The basis step is verified. $P(0)$ is **True**.
* **Step 2: Inductive Step**
  Assume the inductive hypothesis $P(k)$ is true for an arbitrary nonnegative integer $k$:
  $$\text{Assumption } P(k): \quad a + ar + ar^2 + \dots + ar^k = \frac{ar^{k+1} - a}{r - 1}$$
  Our target is to establish $P(k + 1)$:
  $$\text{Target } P(k + 1): \quad a + ar + ar^2 + \dots + ar^k + ar^{k+1} = \frac{ar^{(k+1)+1} - a}{r - 1} = \frac{ar^{k+2} - a}{r - 1}$$
* **Step 3: The Algebraic Bridge**
  Take the left side of our target and substitute the inductive hypothesis for the first $k+1$ terms:
  $$\left( \sum_{j=0}^{k} ar^j \right) + ar^{k+1} = \left( \frac{ar^{k+1} - a}{r - 1} \right) + ar^{k+1}$$
  Give $ar^{k+1}$ a common denominator of $(r - 1)$:
  $$= \frac{ar^{k+1} - a}{r - 1} + \frac{ar^{k+1}(r - 1)}{r - 1} = \frac{ar^{k+1} - a + ar^{k+1}\cdot r - ar^{k+1}}{r - 1}$$
  Simplify the numerator (notice $ar^{k+1}$ and $-ar^{k+1}$ cancel out completely):
  $$= \frac{-a + ar^{k+2}}{r - 1} = \frac{ar^{k+2} - a}{r - 1}$$
  The target is reached! The formula is proven for all $n \ge 0$. $\blacksquare$

### EXAMPLE 5: A Multi-Variable Sum Inequality

> **Theorem:** Use mathematical induction to prove the inequality:
> $$1 + \frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{9} + \dots + \frac{1}{n^2} \le 2 - \frac{1}{n}$$
> for all positive integers $n$.

Let $P(n)$ be the inequality statement.

* **Step 1: Basis Step ($n = 1$)**
  * Left side: $1$
  * Right side: $2 - \frac{1}{1} = 1$
  Since $1 \le 1$ is true, the basis step holds.
* **Step 2: Inductive Step**
  Assume $P(k)$ is true for an arbitrary positive integer $k$:
  $$\text{Assumption } P(k): \quad 1 + \frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{9} + \dots + \frac{1}{k^2} \le 2 - \frac{1}{k}$$
  We want to show that $P(k + 1)$ must be true:
  $$\text{Target } P(k + 1): \quad 1 + \frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{9} + \dots + \frac{1}{k^2} + \frac{1}{(k+1)^2} \le 2 - \frac{1}{k+1}$$
* **Step 3: The Algebraic Bridge**
  Add $\frac{1}{(k+1)^2}$ to both sides of our assumed hypothesis inequality $P(k)$:
  $$1 + \frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{9} + \dots + \frac{1}{k^2} + \frac{1}{(k+1)^2} \le 2 - \frac{1}{k} + \frac{1}{(k+1)^2}$$
  To complete the proof, we need to show that the right side of this new expression is less than or equal to our final target right side ($2 - \frac{1}{k+1}$). Let's check if:
  $$2 - \frac{1}{k} + \frac{1}{(k+1)^2} \le 2 - \frac{1}{k+1}$$
  This is equivalent to showing that:
  $$-\frac{1}{k} + \frac{1}{(k+1)^2} \le -\frac{1}{k+1}$$
  Let's verify this by finding a common denominator for the left side:
  $$\frac{k - (k+1)^2}{k(k+1)^2} = \frac{k - (k^2 + 2k + 1)}{k(k+1)^2} = \frac{-k^2 - k - 1}{k(k+1)^2}$$
  Now look at $-\frac{1}{k+1}$, which can be rewritten as:
  $$-\frac{1}{k+1} = \frac{-k(k+1)}{k(k+1)^2} = \frac{-k^2 - k}{k(k+1)^2}$$
  Comparing the two numerators: $-k^2 - k - 1$ is strictly less than $-k^2 - k$. Therefore, the inequality chain securely links to $2 - \frac{1}{k+1}$. The inductive step is proven! $\blacksquare$

### EXAMPLE 7: Factorial Inequalities (Shifted Base)

> **Theorem:** Use mathematical induction to prove that $2^n < n!$ for every integer $n$ with $n \ge 4$.

Notice our base starting constraint is **$b = 4$**.

* **Step 1: Basis Step ($n = 4$)**
  We test $P(4)$:
  * Left side: $2^4 = 16$
  * Right side: $4! = 4 \cdot 3 \cdot 2 \cdot 1 = 24$
  Since $16 < 24$, $P(4)$ is **True**.
* **Step 2: Inductive Step**
  Assume $P(k)$ is true for an arbitrary integer $k \ge 4$:
  $$\text{Assumption } P(k): \quad 2^k < k!$$
  Our target is $P(k + 1)$:
  $$\text{Target } P(k + 1): \quad 2^{k+1} < (k+1)!$$
* **Step 3: The Algebraic Bridge**
  Start with the left side of our target, $2^{k+1}$, and break it apart using exponent rules:
  $$2^{k+1} = 2 \cdot 2^k$$
  Apply our inductive hypothesis ($2^k < k!$):
  $$2 \cdot 2^k < 2 \cdot k!$$
  Since our constraint specifies that $k \ge 4$, we know that the number $2$ is strictly less than the number $(k + 1)$. Let's use this property to bound the inequality further:
  $$2 \cdot k! < (k + 1) \cdot k!$$
  By definition of factorials, $(k + 1) \cdot k!$ is equal to $(k + 1)!$:
  $$(k + 1) \cdot k! = (k + 1)!$$
  Connecting the outer edges of our chain: $2^{k+1} < (k+1)!$. The theorem is verified. $\blacksquare$

### EXAMPLE 9: Advanced Divisibility Challenge

This is a more mathematically rigorous example from this section of the text.

> **Theorem:** Use mathematical induction to prove that $7^{n+2} + 8^{2n+1}$ is divisible by 57 for every nonnegative integer $n$.

Let $P(n)$ be the proposition that $57$ cleanly divides $7^{n+2} + 8^{2n+1}$. Our domain starts at $n = 0$.

* **Step 1: Basis Step ($n = 0$)**
  We evaluate $P(0)$:
  $$7^{0+2} + 8^{2(0)+1} = 7^2 + 8^1 = 49 + 8 = 57$$
  Since $57$ is cleanly divisible by $57$, $P(0)$ is **True**.
* **Step 2: Inductive Step**
  Assume $P(k)$ is true for an arbitrary nonnegative integer $k$. This means that $7^{k+2} + 8^{2k+1}$ is equal to **$57c$** for some integer $c$:
  $$\text{Assumption } P(k): \quad 7^{k+2} + 8^{2k+1} = 57c$$
  Our target is to show that $P(k+1)$ evaluates to a multiple of 57:
  $$\text{Target } P(k+1): \quad 7^{(k+1)+2} + 8^{2(k+1)+1} = 7^{k+3} + 8^{2k+3} \text{ is divisible by 57.}$$
* **Step 3: The Algebraic Bridge**
  Let's write down our target expression and strip out multipliers to isolate terms that match our hypothesis:
  $$7^{k+3} + 8^{2k+3} = 7 \cdot 7^{k+2} + 8^2 \cdot 8^{2k+1}$$
  $$= 7 \cdot 7^{k+2} + 64 \cdot 8^{2k+1}$$
  Now, we apply an algebraic trick: split the coefficient $64$ into $(7 + 57)$ so we can factor out a 7 to match our inductive hypothesis:
  $$= 7 \cdot 7^{k+2} + (7 + 57) \cdot 8^{2k+1}$$
  Distribute the terms:
  $$= 7 \cdot 7^{k+2} + 7 \cdot 8^{2k+1} + 57 \cdot 8^{2k+1}$$
  Factor out a $7$ from the first two terms:
  $$= 7 \cdot \mathbf{(7^{k+2} + 8^{2k+1})} + 57 \cdot 8^{2k+1}$$
  Substitute our inductive hypothesis ($57c$) into the bolded block:
  $$= 7 \cdot \mathbf{(57c)} + 57 \cdot 8^{2k+1}$$
  Factor out our target divisor, $57$:
  $$= 57 \cdot (7c + 8^{2k+1})$$
  Because the entire expression simplifies down to $57 \times (\text{an integer})$, the expression is proven to be divisible by 57 for all $n \ge 0$. $\blacksquare$
