# 1.1.3 Conditional Statements

Conditional statements play an essential role in mathematical and computational reasoning. They allow us to connect propositions by establishing a relationship where one statement depends on another.

---

### Definition and Core Syntax

> **Definition:** Let $p$ and $q$ be propositions. The **conditional statement $p \rightarrow q$** is the proposition **"if $p$, then $q$."** 

In the conditional statement $p \rightarrow q$:
* **$p$** is called the **hypothesis** (or antecedent or premise).
* **$q$** is called the **conclusion** (or consequence).

A conditional statement is also widely referred to as an **implication**. It asserts that $q$ is true on the condition that $p$ holds.

---

### The Truth Value of Implication

A useful way to perfectly grasp the truth value of a conditional statement is to think of a **contract** or a **campaign pledge**.

Imagine a politician running for office who pledges: *"If I am elected, then I will lower taxes."* 

1. **Elected ($T$) and Lowers Taxes ($T$):** The politician kept the promise. The statement is **True**.
2. **Not Elected ($F$):** Voters have no expectation for them to lower taxes. They can buy a mansion, raise or lower taxes in their personal businessâ€”they haven't broken any contract. Therefore, the statement remains **True** by default.
3. **Elected ($T$) but Does NOT Lower Taxes ($F$):** This is the only scenario where the politician broke their contract. The statement is **False**.

> **The Golden Rule:** The conditional statement $p \rightarrow q$ is **false when $p$ is true and $q$ is false**, and **true otherwise**.

**The Truth Table for $p \rightarrow q$:** 

| $p$ | $q$ | $p \rightarrow q$ |
| :---: | :---: | :---: |
| **T** | **T** | **T** |
| **T** | **F** | **F** |
| **F** | **T** | **T** |
| **F** | **F** | **T** |

---

### Mathematical vs. Natural Language

In normal English conversation, we only use "if-then" statements when there is a clear cause-and-effect relationship between the hypothesis and the conclusion (e.g., *"If it is sunny, then we will go to the beach"*).

However, in **mathematical reasoning**, the concept of a conditional statement is entirely independent of any cause-and-effect connection. It is an artificial language defined strictly by its truth values.

* *"If Juan has a smartphone, then $2 + 3 = 5$"* is **True** simply because the conclusion ($2 + 3 = 5$) is true.
* *"If Juan has a smartphone, then $2 + 3 = 6$"* is **True** if Juan does *not* have a smartphone, because a false hypothesis automatically makes the implication true.

---

### Expressing $p \rightarrow q$ in English

Because implications are so fundamental, natural language has evolved a huge variety of ways to state them. You will encounter all of the following terminology expressions to describe $p \rightarrow q$:

* "if $p$, then $q$"
* "$p$ implies $q$"
* "if $p$, $q$"
* "$p$ only if $q$"
* "$p$ is sufficient for $q$"
* "a sufficient condition for $q$ is $p$"
* "$q$ if $p$"
* "$q$ whenever $p$"
* "$q$ when $p$"
* "$q$ is necessary for $p$"
* "a necessary condition for $p$ is $q$"
* "$q$ follows from $p$"
* "$q$ unless $\neg p$"
* "$q$ provided that $p$"

#### Deep Dive on Tricky Phrasings:

* **"$p$ only if $q$":** This means $p$ cannot be true unless $q$ is also true. If $p$ happens, $q$ *must* have happened. (e.g., *"You can receive an A in the course only if your score on the final is at least 90%"* means if you got an A, your final score was definitely $\ge 90\%$).
* **"Necessary" vs. "Sufficient":**
  * **Sufficient:** Having $p$ is *enough* to guarantee $q$.
  * **Necessary:** You *must* have $q$ for $p$ to even stand a chance at being true.
* **"$q$ unless $\neg p$":** This means if $\neg p$ is false (meaning $p$ is true), then $q$ *must* be true. It shares the exact same truth values as $p \rightarrow q$.

---

### Logic vs. Programming `if-then`

As a computer science student, you use `if-then` statements constantly, but be careful not to confuse programming syntax with formal logic!

In programming languages, a statement like `if p then S` takes a proposition $p$ and an executable segment of instructions $S$.
* $S$ is **not a proposition**; it is a command to be executed.
* If $p$ evaluates to True, the computer runs $S$. If $p$ evaluates to False, the computer completely skips $S$.

**Example (Textbook Example 11):** What is the value of $x$ after encountering:
`if 2 + 2 = 4 then x := x + 1` (given that $x = 0$ initially)?

* *Solution:* Because $2 + 2 = 4$ is logically **True**, the assignment statement is executed. The value becomes $0 + 1 = 1$.

---

### The Three Related Conditional Statements

Starting with an original conditional statement $p \rightarrow q$, we can generate three highly specific variants. They occur so often they have dedicated names:

1. **The Converse:** $q \rightarrow p$
2. **The Contrapositive:** $\neg q \rightarrow \neg p$
3. **The Inverse:** $\neg p \rightarrow \neg q$

> **Critical Fact:** Only the **contrapositive** ($\neg q \rightarrow \neg p$) is logically equivalent to the original conditional statement $p \rightarrow q$. It is false *only* when $\neg p$ is false and $\neg q$ is true, which is exactly when $p$ is true and $q$ is false.
> 
> Assuming that the converse or inverse is equivalent to the original statement is one of the most common logical errors.

**Textbook Example 12:** Let's find all three variants for the phrase: *"The home team wins whenever it is raining."* 

* **Original ($p \rightarrow q$):** "If it is raining, then the home team wins."
* **Contrapositive ($\neg q \rightarrow \neg p$):** "If the home team does not win, then it is not raining." *(Logically equivalent to the original)*.
* **Converse ($q \rightarrow p$):** "If the home team wins, then it is raining."
* **Inverse ($\neg p \rightarrow \neg q$):** "If it is not raining, then the home team does not win."

---

### Biconditionals (Bi-implications)

What happens when we want to state that two propositions must share the exact same truth value? We use a **biconditional**.

> **Definition:** Let $p$ and $q$ be propositions. The **biconditional statement $p \leftrightarrow q$** is the proposition **"$p$ if and only if $q$."** 

The statement $p \leftrightarrow q$ is true **when $p$ and $q$ have the exact same truth values**, and is false otherwise. It is structurally equivalent to $(p \rightarrow q) \land (q \rightarrow p)$.

**The Truth Table for Biconditional $p \leftrightarrow q$:** 

| $p$ | $q$ | $p \leftrightarrow q$ |
| :---: | :---: | :---: |
| **T** | **T** | **T** |
| **T** | **F** | **F** |
| **F** | **T** | **F** |
| **F** | **F** | **T** |

#### Common Ways to Express $p \leftrightarrow q$:
* "$p$ is necessary and sufficient for $q$"
* "if $p$ then $q$, and conversely"
* "$p$ iff $q$" *(The abbreviation "iff" stands for "if and only if")*.
* "$p$ exactly when $q$"

#### Implicit Use in Common English:
In everyday language, people rarely say "if and only if". Instead, they say "if-then" but *implicitly mean* a biconditional.
* *Parent to child:* "If you finish your meal, then you can have dessert."
* *What they actually mean:* "You can have dessert **if and only if** you finish your meal." (Because if you don't finish it, you aren't getting dessert!).

In formal mathematics and logic, we must always strip away this conversational imprecision and strictly differentiate between a simple conditional $p \rightarrow q$ and a biconditional $p \leftrightarrow q$.
