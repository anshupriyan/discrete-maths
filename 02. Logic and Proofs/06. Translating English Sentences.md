# 1.2.2 Translating English Sentences

Logic has many important applications to mathematics, computer science, and numerous other disciplines. Statements in mathematics, the sciences, and in natural language often are imprecise or ambiguous. To make such statements precise, they can be translated into the language of logic.

For example, logic is used extensively in the specification of software and hardware systems because these specifications need to be completely precise before actual physical development or coding begins. Furthermore, propositional logic and its formal rules are applied directly to:

* Designing digital computer circuits.
* Constructing reliable computer programs.
* Verifying the absolute formal correctness of programs.
* Building complex expert systems within artificial intelligence.

---

### Translating English Sentences

There are many critical reasons to translate English sentences into formal expressions involving propositional variables and logical connectives. In particular, English (and every other human natural language) is often highly ambiguous. Translating sentences into compound statements removes this ambiguity.

When translating, this process often involves making a set of reasonable assumptions based on the intended meaning of the sentence. Once sentences are successfully translated from English into logical expressions, we can analyze them to determine their truth values, manipulate them mathematically, and apply **rules of inference** to reason about them systematically.

#### The Strategy for Translation

Instead of trying to represent an entire complex sentence with a single variable (which is not useful for analyzing its internal meaning or reasoning with it), you should break the sentence into its logical components:

1. Identify each distinct atomic sentence part that declares a fact.
2. Assign a lowercase propositional variable ($p, q, r, \dots$) to represent each individual part.
3. Determine the appropriate logical connectives ($\neg, \land, \lor, \rightarrow, \leftrightarrow$) based on the grammatical phrasing.

### Textbook Step-by-Step Breakdowns

#### EXAMPLE 1: Campus Internet Access

> **English Sentence:** “You can access the Internet from campus only if you are a computer science major or you are not a freshman.” 

**Step 1: Identify the atomic parts and assign variables:**
* Let $a$ represent: “You can access the Internet from campus.” 
* Let $c$ represent: “You are a computer science major.” 
* Let $f$ represent: “You are a freshman.” 

**Step 2: Translate individual fragments:**
* "...you are not a freshman" translates to $\neg f$.
* "...you are a computer science major or you are not a freshman" uses a disjunction, giving us: $(c \lor \neg f)$.

**Step 3: Handle the structural connective:**
Recall from our previous section that **"$p$ only if $q$"** is a specific way of expressing the conditional statement $p \rightarrow q$. Here, accessing the campus internet ($a$) happens *only if* the condition $(c \lor \neg f)$ is met.

$$\text{Final Logical Expression: } a \rightarrow (c \lor \neg f)$$

---

#### EXAMPLE 2: The Roller Coaster Requirement

> **English Sentence:** “You cannot ride the roller coaster if you are under 4 feet tall unless you are older than 16 years old.” 

**Step 1: Identify the atomic parts and assign variables:**
* Let $q$ represent: “You can ride the roller coaster.” 
* Let $r$ represent: “You are under 4 feet tall.” 
* Let $s$ represent: “You are older than 16 years old.” 

**Step 2: Translate individual fragments:**
* "You cannot ride the roller coaster" translates to $\neg q$.
* "if you are under 4 feet tall unless you are older than 16 years old" specifies the conditions under which you are banned from the ride.

**Step 3: Parse "unless":**
Remember that **"$q$ unless $\neg p$"** is equivalent to $p \rightarrow q$, meaning if the exception condition isn't met, the outcome triggers. In this context, the phrase means you are barred if you are under 4 feet tall AND you lack the exception of being older than 16 years old ($r \land \neg s$). If that combined state ($r \land \neg s$) is true, then it implies you cannot ride ($\neg q$).

$$\text{Final Logical Expression: } (r \land \neg s) \rightarrow \neg q$$

### 💡 Solutions & Explanation

> [!NOTE]
> Here are the step-by-step variables and logical interpretations showing how formal logic removes natural language ambiguity:
> 
> * **1. Atomic Variables:**
>   * Let $p$ represent: *"You can pass the discrete math exam."*
>   * Let $s$ represent: *"You study hard."*
>   * Let $l$ represent: *"You completely skip the lectures."*
> 
> * **2. Logical Formulations (Resolving Grammatical Ambiguity):**
>   * **Interpretation A (Combined Constraint - Recommended):** 
>     If we read the sentence as: *"To pass the exam, you must study hard and you cannot completely skip the lectures"*, this means the outcome $p$ is provided under the combined conditions of studying hard ($s$) and not skipping lectures ($\neg l$). This yields:
>     $$(s \land \neg l) \rightarrow p$$
>   * **Interpretation B (Alternative Paths):**
>     If we interpret the sentence to mean that either path is sufficient to pass (either you study hard OR you simply don't completely skip the lectures):
>     $$(s \lor \neg l) \rightarrow p$$
>   * **Interpretation C (Direct Exception Trigger):**
>     If we translate the exceptions directly as implications—studying hard guarantees passing ($s \rightarrow p$), but completely skipping lectures guarantees failing ($l \rightarrow \neg p$):
>     $$(s \rightarrow p) \land (l \rightarrow \neg p)$$
