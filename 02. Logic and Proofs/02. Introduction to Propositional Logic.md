# 1.1.2 Propositions

Our study begins with the absolute core building blocks of logic: **propositions**.

> **Definition:** A **proposition** is a declarative sentence (a sentence that declares a solid fact) that is either **true** or **false**, but **not both**.

Propositions can be straightforward or complex, but they must unambiguously evaluate to one of the two truth values: **True (denoted by T)** or **False (denoted by F)**.

If a proposition is completely fundamental and cannot be broken down into simpler component statements, it is called an **atomic proposition**. The domain of logic that focuses entirely on analyzing these statements is called **propositional calculus** or **propositional logic**.

---

### Textbook Examples of Valid Propositions

The following declarative sentences are all valid propositions according to the text:

1. **"Washington, D.C., is the capital of the United States of America."** 
   * *Truth Value:* **T**.
2. **"Toronto is the capital of Canada."** 
   * *Truth Value:* **F** (The actual capital is Ottawa).
3. **"$1 + 1 = 2$"** 
   * *Truth Value:* **T**.
4. **"$2 + 2 = 3$"** 
   * *Truth Value:* **F**.

*Take Note:* Even if a statement is factually wrong (like examples 2 and 4), it is still a valid proposition because it is a declarative sentence with a definitive truth value.

---

### Textbook Non-Examples (Sentences that are NOT Propositions)

The following sentences do not qualify as propositions:

5. **"What time is it?"** 
   * *Reason:* This is a question, not a declarative sentence.
6. **"Read this carefully."** 
   * *Reason:* This is a command (imperative sentence), not a declarative sentence.
7. **"$x + 1 = 2$"** 
   * *Reason:* It contains a variable ($x$) and is currently neither true nor false.
8. **"$x + y = z$"** 
   * *Reason:* Like the statement above, it cannot be assigned a clear truth value until values are assigned to its variables.

> **Crucial Rule:** Sentences with variables (like 7 and 8) are **not** propositions initially. However, they can be turned into valid propositions if we explicitly assign specific values to those variables.

---

### Propositional Variables & Compound Expressions

Just like variables like $x$ and $y$ represent numbers in algebra, logic uses lowercase letters to denote **propositional variables** (or sentential variables). The conventional letters used are **$p, q, r, s, \dots$**.

When a variable's value is confined strictly to being True or False, it can be seamlessly represented in computer systems as a **Boolean variable** using a single **bit** ($1$ representing True, $0$ representing False).

When we want to construct sophisticated mathematical arguments, we do not stick to individual atomic statements. Instead, we combine multiple propositional variables together using **logical operators** (also called **connectives**) to form **compound propositions**.

---

## The Core Logical Operators (Connectives)

Let’s deep-dive into each primary operator covered in your textbook, including their official definitions, truth tables, and practical syntax.

### 1. Negation (NOT)

> **Definition:** Let $p$ be a proposition. The negation of $p$, denoted by **$\neg p$** (or sometimes $\overline{p}$), is the statement *"It is not the case that p."* 

The truth value of a negation is always the exact opposite of the original proposition.
(Note: Other common notation styles you might see across software engineering or mathematics include $\sim p, -p, p', \text{Np},$ or $!p$ ).

**The Truth Table for Negation:** 

| $p$ | $\neg p$ |
| --- | --- |
| **T** | **F** |
| **F** | **T** |

* **Example A:** If $p$ is *"Michael’s PC runs Linux"*, then $\neg p$ translates cleanly to *"Michael’s PC does not run Linux"*.
* **Example B:** If $q$ is *"Vandana’s smartphone has at least 32 GB of memory"*, then $\neg q$ translates to *"Vandana’s smartphone has less than 32 GB of memory"*.

---

### 2. Conjunction (AND)

> **Definition:** Let $p$ and $q$ be propositions. The conjunction of $p$ and $q$, denoted by **$p \land q$**, is the proposition *"p and q."* 

A conjunction is strictly **true when both $p$ and $q$ are true**, and evaluates to **false otherwise**.

**The Truth Table for Conjunction:** 

| $p$ | $q$ | $p \land q$ |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **T** | **T** | **T** |
| **T** | **F** | **F** |
| **F** | **T** | **F** |
| **F** | **F** | **F** |

* **Natural Language Nuance:** In common language, the word **"but"** is logically treated as a conjunction equivalent to **"and"**. For example, *"The sun is shining, but it is raining"* translates perfectly to $p \land q$.
* **Example:** Let $p$ be *"Rebecca’s PC has more than 16 GB free hard disk space"* and $q$ be *"The processor in Rebecca’s PC runs faster than 1 GHz"*. The compound statement $p \land q$ is true only if both internal states are simultaneously met.

---

### 3. Disjunction (OR / Inclusive Or)

> **Definition:** Let $p$ and $q$ be propositions. The disjunction of $p$ and $q$, denoted by **$p \lor q$**, is the proposition *"p or q."* 

A disjunction is **false only when both $p$ and $q$ are false**, and is **true otherwise**. It behaves as an **inclusive or**, meaning it is true if statement $p$ is true, statement $q$ is true, or if *both* are true.

**The Truth Table for Disjunction:** 

| $p$ | $q$ | $p \lor q$ |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **T** | **T** | **T** |
| **T** | **F** | **T** |
| **F** | **T** | **T** |
| **F** | **F** | **F** |

* **Example:** *"Students who have taken calculus or introductory computer science can take this class."* This statement uses an inclusive or because a student who has taken *both* prerequisites is absolutely welcome to enroll along with students who have taken only one.

---

### 4. Exclusive Or (XOR)

> **Definition:** Let $p$ and $q$ be propositions. The exclusive or of $p$ and $q$, denoted by **$p \oplus q$** (or $p \text{ XOR } q$), is true **when exactly one of $p$ and $q$ is true**, and is **false otherwise**.

Unlike a regular disjunction, if both component statements are true, the exclusive or evaluates to **false**.

**The Truth Table for Exclusive Or:** 

| $p$ | $q$ | $p \oplus q$ |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **T** | **T** | **F** |
| **T** | **F** | **T** |
| **F** | **T** | **T** |
| **F** | **F** | **F** |

* **Example A:** *"A student can have soup or salad with dinner."* In a restaurant context, this naturally implies an exclusive choice: you can pick one, but you are not permitted to have both without paying extra.
* **Example B:** *"I will use all my savings to travel to Europe or to buy an electric car."* Since both options independently exhaust *all* of your savings, it is physically impossible to do both simultaneously, forcing an exclusive choice ($p \oplus q$).
