# 1.2.4 Boolean Searches

Logical connectives are used extensively in searches of large collections of information, such as indexes of Web pages. Because these searches employ techniques from propositional logic, they are called **Boolean searches**.

In Boolean searches, the connectives are utilized to match or exclude records systematically:
* The connective **AND** is used to match records that contain *both* of two search terms.
* The connective **OR** is used to match *one or both* (at least one) of two search terms.
* The connective **NOT** (sometimes written as **AND NOT**) is used to *exclude* a particular search term from the results.

---

### Real-World Search Engine Formatting

Most web search engines use modified symbols or default behaviors to perform these exact logical functions:
* **AND By Default:** Many modern search engines (like Google) do not require you to explicitly type the word "AND" because they automatically look for pages containing all your search terms by default.
* **Exclusion Subtraction (`-`):** The logical word `NOT` is often replaced by the minus symbol (`-`) placed immediately before the word you want to exclude.
* **Exact Phrases (`""`):** Most search engines support the use of quotation marks to look for specific, multi-word phrases as a single block instead of individual terms.

### Textbook Walkthrough: Example 6 (Web Page Searching)

Let's look at how a search query changes its focus depending entirely on how you organize your logical connectives.

#### Scenario A: Finding universities in New Mexico 
* **Logical Query:** `NEW` **AND** `MEXICO` **AND** `UNIVERSITIES` 
* **How it works:** The engine returns pages that must contain all three words. This will give you the correct pages, but it will also pull up pages about *new universities located in the country of Mexico*.
* **Optimization:** To refine this and fix the ambiguity, you can group the phrase using quotation marks: `"NEW MEXICO"` **AND** `UNIVERSITIES`.

#### Scenario B: Broadening the scope to multiple regions 
Suppose you want to find pages dealing with universities in New Mexico *or* Arizona.
* **Logical Query:** (`"NEW MEXICO"` **OR** `ARIZONA`) **AND** `UNIVERSITIES` 
* **Precedence Alert:** Remember our logical order of operations! Because the `AND` operator takes precedence over the `OR` operator, using parentheses is necessary to ensure the engine groups `NEW MEXICO` and `ARIZONA` as the regional options. Without parentheses, `"NEW MEXICO"` **OR** `ARIZONA` **AND** `UNIVERSITIES` is evaluated as `"NEW MEXICO"` **OR** (`ARIZONA` **AND** `UNIVERSITIES`), which would pull up pages about New Mexico that do not mention universities.
* **How it works:** The engine looks for pages that contain the word `UNIVERSITIES` along with *either* the combined phrase `NEW MEXICO` or the single word `ARIZONA`.

#### Scenario C: Stripping out unneeded results 
What if you want to find universities in Mexico, but you want to completely filter out any pages talking about *New* Mexico?
* **Logical Query:** (`MEXICO` **AND** `UNIVERSITIES`) **NOT** `NEW` 
* **Google Syntax Equivalent:** `MEXICO UNIVERSITIES -NEW` 
* **How it works:** This query forces the engine to return pages that explicitly contain both `MEXICO` and `UNIVERSITIES`, but it will completely discard any document that contains the word `NEW`.

### 🧠 Track Your Understanding

**Question:** If you wanted to look for data about **men's shoes** or **boots**, but you wanted to make sure they are **not designed for work**, how would you write that logical expression using terms and connectives?

---

### 💡 Solutions & Explanation

> [!NOTE]
> Here are the step-by-step variables and logical interpretations showing how search engine logic parses queries:
> 
> * **1. Structured Logical Query:**
>   $$(\text{"men's shoes"} \lor \text{"boots"}) \land \neg \text{"work"}$$
>   *(Or written in text format: `("men's shoes" OR boots) AND NOT work`)*
> 
> * **2. Google Syntax Equivalent:**
>   `("men's shoes" OR boots) -work`
> 
> * **3. Importance of Parentheses (Precedence Proof):**
>   * Because the `AND` connective binds tighter than `OR`, if you omit parentheses and write `"men's shoes" OR boots AND NOT work`, the computer evaluates it as:
>     $$\text{"men's shoes"} \lor (\text{"boots"} \land \neg \text{"work"})$$
>     This incorrect query would match and return *any* web pages containing `"men's shoes"` even if they are work shoes, failing the exclusion requirement. The parentheses are mandatory to force the disjunction to evaluate before the negation/conjunction.
