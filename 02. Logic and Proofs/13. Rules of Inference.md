# 1.6 Rules of Inference

### 1.6.1 Introduction

When building mathematical arguments or verifying computer systems, we need a way to deduce new truths from statements we already know to be true. A **proof** is a valid argument that establishes the absolute truth of a mathematical statement.

To break this down precisely:
* **Argument:** A sequence of statements that ends with a final statement called the **conclusion**.
* **Premises:** The preceding statements in the sequence that provide the foundation or hypotheses for the argument.
* **Valid:** An argument is legally **valid** if and only if it is completely impossible for all of its premises to be true while its conclusion is false.

To seamlessly construct these valid arguments and deduce new facts without getting bogged down in massive tables, logicians use formal templates called **rules of inference**. These rules serve as our primary mathematical building blocks for establishing truth.

---

### Understanding Argument Forms vs. Specific Content

In logic, the structural validity of an argument depends entirely on its **form**, not on the specific words used. Let’s look at a classic real-world example:
1. *“If you have a current password, then you can log onto the network.”* (Premise 1)
2. *“You have a current password.”* (Premise 2)
3. *Therefore, “You can log onto the network.”* (Conclusion)

If we replace the specific statements with propositional variables—letting $p$ be *"You have a current password"* and $q$ be *"You can log onto the network"*—we expose the abstract **argument form**:

$$\begin{aligned}
& p \rightarrow q \\
& p \\
\hline
\therefore \quad & q
\end{aligned}$$

*(Note: The symbol $\therefore$ is a mathematical shorthand that denotes **“therefore”**).*

This particular argument form is universally valid because its underlying compound proposition, **$((p \rightarrow q) \land p) \rightarrow q$**, is a **tautology** (it evaluates to True under every possible scenario). Whenever an argument form is a tautology, any specific statements you substitute into it will result in a valid logical deduction.

---

### The Fundamental Rule of Truth in Proofs

A common point of confusion in computer science and discrete mathematics is the difference between an argument being **valid** and its conclusion being **true**.

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **The Golden Rule:** A valid argument form only guarantees a true conclusion **if all of its premises are factually true**. If even a single premise is false, the argument remains structurally valid, but you can no longer conclude that the final statement is true.

#### Textbook Example 2: A Valid Form with a False Conclusion

Consider this mathematical argument:
* *“If $\sqrt{2} > \frac{3}{2}$, then $(\sqrt{2})^2 > \left(\frac{3}{2}\right)^2$.”* (Premise 1)
* *“We know that $\sqrt{2} > \frac{3}{2}$.”* (Premise 2)
* *“Consequently, $2 > \frac{9}{4}$.”* (Conclusion)

**The Logic Analysis:** This argument perfectly matches our valid shorthand form above. However, Premise 2 ($\sqrt{2} > \frac{3}{2}$, which is roughly $1.414 > 1.5$) is factually **false**. Because a premise fails, the argument cannot guarantee a true outcome—and indeed, the final conclusion ($2 > 2.25$) is completely incorrect.

---

### Looking Ahead: Why We Need Rules of Inference

While you can always verify a small argument form by checking a truth table, this approach breaks down exponentially in computer engineering.
* If a system specification or an AI reasoning program involves just **10 distinct propositional variables**, a truth table requires **$2^{10} = 1,024$ rows** to evaluate completely.

Rather than executing a tedious, massive calculation, we treat simple, verified argument forms as **rules of inference**. We chain these simple rules together line-by-line to build ironclad proofs for highly complex computational systems.

---

## 1.6.2 Valid Arguments in Propositional Logic

In Section 1.6.1, we learned that an argument is a sequence of propositions that ends with a conclusion. Now, let's look at how we formally define and evaluate the structural validity of these arguments within propositional logic.

---

### Key Definitions

To reason properly, we must first establish the precise terminology used in propositional calculus:
* **Argument:** A sequence of propositions. All propositions in the sequence except for the final one are called **premises** (or hypotheses or constraints). The very last proposition in the sequence is called the **conclusion**.
* **Valid Argument:** An argument is **valid** if and only if the truth of all its premises forces the conclusion to be true. In other words, it is completely impossible for all the premises to be true and the conclusion to be false at the same time.
* **Argument Form:** An argument form in propositional logic is a sequence of compound propositions involving propositional variables ($p, q, r, \dots$).
* **Valid Argument Form:** An argument form is **valid** if, no matter which specific propositions are substituted for the variables in its premises, the conclusion is guaranteed to be true whenever the premises are all true.

---

### The Fundamental Structural Rule

The definition of a valid argument form gives us a direct mathematical relationship:

> **The Tautology Rule:** An argument form with premises $p_1, p_2, \dots, p_n$ and conclusion $q$ is structurally valid if and only if the conditional statement **$(p_1 \land p_2 \land \dots \land p_n) \rightarrow q$ is a tautology**.

This means the key to showing that a real-world argument is valid is to extract its underlying variables, convert it into an abstract argument form, and prove that the form is a tautology.

---

### Detailed Walkthrough of an Argument Form

Let's look at how an argument form behaves under different truth assignments to see this rule in action. Consider the standard form we introduced in our introduction:

$$\begin{aligned}
& p \rightarrow q \\
& p \\
\hline
\therefore \quad & q
\end{aligned}$$

This structural form corresponds to the conditional statement: **$((p \rightarrow q) \land p) \rightarrow q$**.

#### Scenario A: True Premises
Suppose we substitute factually true statements into this form:
* $p$: *"You have a current password."*
* $p \rightarrow q$: *"If you have a current password, then you can log onto the network."*

If both of these statements are completely true, it is logically impossible for $q$ (*"You can log onto the network"*) to be false. The structural form guarantees that the conclusion inherits factual truth from its premises.

#### Scenario B: False Premises (The Most Common Pitfall)
What happens if we substitute propositions where the premises are *not* both true? Let's assign different meanings:
* Let $p$ represent: *"You have access to the network."*
* Let $q$ represent: *"You can change your grade."*

Now suppose that $p$ is factually **True** (you do have network access), but the implication $p \rightarrow q$ is factually **False** (network access does *not* grant you the permission to change your grade).

If we write out the argument, it looks like this:
1. *"If you have access to the network, then you can change your grade."* (False Premise)
2. *"You have access to the network."* (True Premise)
3. $\therefore$ *"You can change your grade."* (Conclusion)

* **Is this argument valid?** **Yes!** The argument remains completely valid because its *form* is a tautology.
* **Is the conclusion true?** **No.** Because one of our incoming premises was false, we cannot conclude that the conclusion is true. In fact, the conclusion here is almost certainly false.

## 1.6.3 Rules of Inference for Propositional Logic

While you can technically use a truth table to verify the validity of any argument form, this approach grows tedious and impractical when expressions involve many variables. Instead, we can establish the validity of a collection of relatively simple, foundational argument forms called **rules of inference**. These simple rules serve as the standardized building blocks used to construct much more complex and sophisticated valid arguments.

---

### Understanding the Shorthand Column Notation

When presenting an argument form or a rule of inference, we list the premises vertically in a column, draw a horizontal bar, and conclude with the therefore symbol ($\dots$) followed by the final conclusion statement:

$$\begin{aligned}
& \text{Premise 1} \\
& \text{Premise 2} \\
\hline
\dots \quad & \text{Conclusion}
\end{aligned}$$

This shorthand vertical layout is mathematically based on an underlying **tautology**. It asserts that the conjunction of all the premises conditionally implies the conclusion: **$(\text{Premise 1} \land \text{Premise 2}) \rightarrow \text{Conclusion}$**.

---

### The Complete Toolkit of Propositional Rules

Here is the complete, comprehensive list of the core rules of inference detailed in Table 1 of your textbook:

#### 1. Modus Ponens (The Law of Detachment)
* **Latin Meaning:** *"Mode that affirms"*.
* **Logical Meaning:** If a conditional statement and its hypothesis are both true, then the conclusion of that conditional statement must also be true.
* **Column Structure:**
  $$\begin{aligned}
  & p \\
  & p \rightarrow q \\
  \hline
  \therefore \quad & q
  \end{aligned}$$
* **Underlying Tautology:** $((p \land (p \rightarrow q)) \rightarrow q)$.
* **Textbook Example:** If the statement *"If it snows today, then we will go skiing"* is true, and its hypothesis *"It is snowing today"* is also true, then by Modus Ponens, the conclusion *"We will go skiing"* is true.

#### 2. Modus Tollens (The Law of Denying the Conclusion)
* **Logical Meaning:** If an implication is true, but the conclusion is false, then the hypothesis *must* be false (based on the contrapositive).
* **Column Structure:**
  $$\begin{aligned}
  & \neg q \\
  & p \rightarrow q \\
  \hline
  \therefore \quad & \neg p
  \end{aligned}$$
* **Underlying Tautology:** $(\neg q \land (p \rightarrow q)) \rightarrow \neg p$.

#### 3. Hypothetical Syllogism (The Transitive Property)
* **Logical Meaning:** If statement $p$ leads to statement $q$, and statement $q$ leads directly to statement $r$, then statement $p$ transitively leads to statement $r$.
* **Column Structure:**
  $$\begin{aligned}
  & p \rightarrow q \\
  & q \rightarrow r \\
  \hline
  \therefore \quad & p \rightarrow r
  \end{aligned}$$
* **Underlying Tautology:** $((p \rightarrow q) \land (q \rightarrow r)) \rightarrow (p \rightarrow r)$.
* **Textbook Example:** Consider these premises:
  1. *"If it rains today, then we will not have a barbecue today."*
  2. *"If we do not have a barbecue today, then we will have a barbecue tomorrow."*
  3. $\therefore$ *"If it rains today, then we will have a barbecue tomorrow."* This is a Hypothetical Syllogism.

#### 4. Disjunctive Syllogism (The Rule of Elimination)
* **Logical Meaning:** If we are told that either statement $p$ or statement $q$ is true, and we subsequently find out that statement $p$ is false, then statement $q$ *must* be the true element.
* **Column Structure:**
  $$\begin{aligned}
  & p \lor q \\
  & \neg p \\
  \hline
  \therefore \quad & q
  \end{aligned}$$
* **Underlying Tautology:** $((p \lor q) \land \neg p) \rightarrow q$.

#### 5. Addition
* **Logical Meaning:** If we know for a fact that a standalone statement $p$ is completely true, then we can legally construct an inclusive disjunction ($p \lor q$) with *any* other arbitrary statement $q$, and it will remain true.
* **Column Structure:**
  $$\begin{aligned}
  & p \\
  \hline
  \therefore \quad & p \lor q
  \end{aligned}$$
* **Underlying Tautology:** $p \rightarrow (p \lor q)$.
* **Textbook Example:** *"It is below freezing now. Therefore, it is below freezing or raining now."* This represents the addition rule.

#### 6. Simplification
* **Logical Meaning:** If a joint conjunction ($p \land q$) is true, we know that both parts are independently true. Therefore, we can structurally extract or "simplify" down to just statement $p$ on its own.
* **Column Structure:**
  $$\begin{aligned}
  & p \land q \\
  \hline
  \therefore \quad & p
  \end{aligned}$$
* **Underlying Tautology:** $(p \land q) \rightarrow p$.
* **Textbook Example:** *"It is below freezing and raining now. Therefore, it is below freezing now."* This structural step utilizes the simplification rule.

#### 7. Conjunction
* **Logical Meaning:** If statement $p$ is independently true as a premise, and statement $q$ is independently true as a premise, we can mathematically fuse them together into a joint compound statement ($p \land q$).
* **Column Structure:**
  $$\begin{aligned}
  & p \\
  & q \\
  \hline
  \therefore \quad & p \land q
  \end{aligned}$$
* **Underlying Tautology:** $((p) \land (q)) \rightarrow (p \land q)$.

#### 8. Resolution
* **Logical Meaning:** If we know that either $p$ or $q$ is true, and we separately know that either $\neg p$ or $r$ is true, then $p$ cannot satisfy both conditions simultaneously. Therefore, the remaining choices must bridge together to form the *resolvent* clause ($q \lor r$). This is an exceptionally crucial rule used heavily in automated theorem proving and software verification.
* **Column Structure:**
  $$\begin{aligned}
  & p \lor q \\
  & \neg p \lor r \\
  \hline
  \therefore \quad & q \lor r
  \end{aligned}$$
* **Underlying Tautology:** $((p \lor q) \land (\neg p \lor r)) \rightarrow (q \lor r)$.

## 1.6.4 Using Rules of Inference to Build Arguments

When a logical argument contains multiple premises, we rarely can get to the conclusion using just a single rule of inference. Instead, we must string several rules together step-by-step to show that the argument is valid.

To write a rigorous, formal mathematical proof using this technique, we display the steps of the argument on separate lines. Each line must be explicitly paired with its exact **reason** (such as being an initial premise or matching a specific rule of inference applied to previous lines).

---

### Step-by-Step Textbook Proof Walkthroughs

#### EXAMPLE 6: A 5-Variable Argument Flow
> **Problem:** Show that the premises below lead to the conclusion **"We will be home by sunset"**:
> 1. *"It is not sunny this afternoon and it is colder than yesterday."*
> 2. *"We will go swimming only if it is sunny."*
> 3. *"If we do not go swimming, then we will take a canoe trip."*
> 4. *"If we take a canoe trip, then we will be home by sunset."*

**Step 1: Translate the atomic segments into propositional variables:**
* Let $p$: *"It is sunny this afternoon."*
* Let $q$: *"It is colder than yesterday."*
* Let $r$: *"We will go swimming."*
* Let $s$: *"We will take a canoe trip."*
* Let $t$: *"We will be home by sunset."*

**Step 2: Rewrite the premises and target conclusion symbolically:**
* **Premise 1:** $\neg p \land q$
* **Premise 2:** $r \rightarrow p$ (Remember: *"r only if p"* translates to $r \rightarrow p$)
* **Premise 3:** $\neg r \rightarrow s$
* **Premise 4:** $s \rightarrow t$
* **Target Conclusion:** $t$

*(Note: A truth table for this problem would require $2^5 = 32$ rows. Building it line-by-line using rules of inference is vastly more efficient).*

**Step 3: Construct the line-by-line proof layout:**

| Step | Statement | Reason |
| :---: | :--- | :--- |
| **1** | $\neg p \land q$ | **Premise** |
| **2** | $\neg p$ | **Simplification** using step (1) |
| **3** | $r \rightarrow p$ | **Premise** |
| **4** | $\neg r$ | **Modus Tollens** using steps (2) and (3) |
| **5** | $\neg r \rightarrow s$ | **Premise** |
| **6** | $s$ | **Modus Ponens** using steps (4) and (5) |
| **7** | $s \rightarrow t$ | **Premise** |
| **8** | $t$ | **Modus Ponens** using steps (6) and (7) |

By executing this continuous chain of logic, the final conclusion $t$ is securely established.

---

#### EXAMPLE 7: Establishing a Conditional Conclusion
> **Problem:** Show that these premises lead to the conclusion **"If I do not finish writing the program, then I will wake up feeling refreshed"**:
> 1. *"If you send me an e-mail message, then I will finish writing the program."*
> 2. *"If you do not send me an e-mail message, then I will go to sleep early."*
> 3. *"If I go to sleep early, then I will wake up feeling refreshed."*

**Step 1: Assign propositional variables:**
* Let $p$: *"You send me an e-mail message."*
* Let $q$: *"I will finish writing the program."*
* Let $r$: *"I will go to sleep early."*
* Let $s$: *"I will wake up feeling refreshed."*

**Step 2: Map to symbolic forms:**
* **Premise 1:** $p \rightarrow q$
* **Premise 2:** $\neg p \rightarrow r$
* **Premise 3:** $r \rightarrow s$
* **Target Conclusion:** $\neg q \rightarrow s$

**Step 3: Construct the line-by-line proof layout:**

| Step | Statement | Reason |
| :---: | :--- | :--- |
| **1** | $p \rightarrow q$ | **Premise** |
| **2** | $\neg q \rightarrow \neg p$ | **Contrapositive equivalence** of step (1) |
| **3** | $\neg p \rightarrow r$ | **Premise** |
| **4** | $\neg q \rightarrow r$ | **Hypothetical Syllogism** using steps (2) and (3) |
| **5** | $r \rightarrow s$ | **Premise** |
| **6** | $\neg q \rightarrow s$ | **Hypothetical Syllogism** using steps (4) and (5) |

## 1.6.5 Resolution

Computer programs have been systematically developed to automate the task of logical reasoning and proving theorems. Many of these automated systems make use of a powerful, unified rule of inference known as **Resolution**.

---

### The Logical Basis of Resolution

The resolution rule is based entirely on a single, elegant tautology:

$$( (p \lor q) \land (\neg p \lor r) ) \rightarrow (q \lor r)$$

#### How to Read the Rule:
* **Premise 1:** $p \lor q$ (States that at least $p$ or $q$ must be true)
* **Premise 2:** $\neg p \lor r$ (States that at least $\neg p$ or $r$ must be true)
* **The Resolution Mechanism:** Notice that the variable $p$ appears in the first premise, and its exact negation ($\neg p$) appears in the second premise. Because $p$ must be either true or false, it cannot satisfy both clauses at the same time.
  * If $p$ is False, then $q$ **must** be true to satisfy Premise 1.
  * If $p$ is True (meaning $\neg p$ is False), then $r$ **must** be true to satisfy Premise 2.
* **The Resolvent:** Therefore, no matter what happens to $p$, we can legally deduce that **$q \lor r$** is guaranteed to be true. This final resulting disjunction, $q \lor r$, is called the **resolvent**.

#### Standard Shorthand Column Structure:
$$\begin{aligned}
& p \lor q \\
& \neg p \lor r \\
\hline
\therefore \quad & q \lor r
\end{aligned}$$

---

### Important Special Cases of Resolution

Resolution is so powerful because it inherently encompasses other classical rules of inference by substituting specific values:

1. **Connection to Disjunctive Syllogism:**
   If we let the variable $r = \text{F}$ (False) inside our core tautology, the second premise becomes $\neg p \lor \text{F}$, which simplifies directly to $\neg p$. The resolvent becomes $q \lor \text{F}$, which simplifies to $q$. This yields:
   $$( (p \lor q) \land \neg p ) \rightarrow q$$
   This is exactly the tautology on which the rule of **Disjunctive Syllogism** is based!

2. **Fusing Identical Terms:**
   If we let $q = r$ in the tautology, we obtain $( (p \lor q) \land (\neg p \lor q) ) \rightarrow q$. If an outcome $q$ occurs whether $p$ is true or false, $q$ is verified independently.

---

### Textbook Walkthroughs

#### EXAMPLE 8: Real-World Syllogism
> **Problem:** Use resolution to show that the hypotheses **“Jasmine is skiing or it is not snowing”** and **“It is snowing or Bart is playing hockey”** imply the conclusion **“Jasmine is skiing or Bart is playing hockey”**.

**Step 1: Assign propositional variables:**
* Let $p$: “It is snowing.”
* Let $q$: “Jasmine is skiing.”
* Let $r$: “Bart is playing hockey.”

**Step 2: Represent the hypotheses symbolically:**
* Hypothesis 1: $\neg p \lor q$
* Hypothesis 2: $p \lor r$

**Step 3: Execute the Resolution step:**
Align the clauses vertically:
$$\begin{aligned}
& \neg p \lor q \\
& p \lor r \\
\hline
\therefore \quad & q \lor r
\end{aligned}$$

The variable $p$ and its negation $\neg p$ complementarily cancel out, leaving the final resolvent $q \lor r$.

**Translation back to English:** “Jasmine is skiing or Bart is playing hockey.”

---

### Application Rule: Converting Expressions into Clauses

To construct proofs in propositional logic using resolution as the *only* rule of inference, all hypotheses and conclusions must be explicitly expressed as **clauses**.

> **Definition:** A **clause** is a compound expression consisting strictly of a disjunction ($\lor$) of variables or negations of variables.

If an initial system statement is not written as a valid clause, you must use established logical equivalences to mathematically transform it into one or more matching clauses:
* **Mixed Disjunctions:** A statement like $p \lor (q \land r)$ is not a single clause. By applying the *Distributive Law*, you replace it with two separate valid clauses: **$(p \lor q)$** and **$(p \lor r)$**.
* **Negated Blocks:** A statement like $\neg(p \lor q)$ can be split into two separate standalone unit clauses, **$\neg p$** and **$\neg q$**, using *De Morgan's Law*.
* **Implications:** Any conditional statement $p \rightarrow q$ is instantly converted into a valid clause by replacing it with its standard conditional-disjunction equivalent: **$\neg p \lor q$**.

#### EXAMPLE 9: Advanced Resolution Proof
> **Problem:** Show that the premises $(p \land q) \lor r$ and $r \rightarrow s$ imply the conclusion $p \lor s$.

**Step 1: Convert all incoming premises into formal clauses:**
* The first premise, $(p \land q) \lor r$, is distributed to yield two distinct clauses: **$p \lor r$** and **$q \lor r$**.
* The second premise, $r \rightarrow s$, is rewritten into the equivalent disjunction clause: **$\neg r \lor s$**.

**Step 2: Construct the Resolution Proof Table:**
We select our newly generated clause components to execute the extraction:

| Step | Clause Statement | Reason |
| :---: | :---: | :--- |
| **1** | $p \lor r$ | **Converted Clause from Premise 1** |
| **2** | $\neg r \lor s$ | **Converted Clause from Premise 2** |
| **3** | $p \lor s$ | **Resolution** applied to steps (1) and (2) using $r$ |

The rule isolates and discards $r$ and $\neg r$, structurally delivering the target conclusion **$p \lor s$** cleanly!

## 1.6.6 Fallacies

Even when an argument seems structured like a formal proof, it can contain deep errors in reasoning. A mistake in a logical argument that leads to an invalid deduction is called a **fallacy**.

As a computer science student, understanding fallacies is critical. When you write a computer program or verify system properties, a fallacy in your logical structure translates directly to software bugs or vulnerabilities.

Your textbook focuses on three specific types of fallacies. Let's break down how they work and how to spot them.

---

### 1. Fallacy of Affirming the Conclusion

This is one of the most common logical errors. It occurs when you mistake a conditional statement's conclusion for its hypothesis.

#### The Abstract Form:
$$\begin{aligned}
& p \rightarrow q \\
& q \\
\hline
\therefore \quad & p \quad \text{ (INVALID!)}
\end{aligned}$$

#### Why it is a Fallacy:
The underlying proposition for this argument form is **$((p \rightarrow q) \land q) \rightarrow p$**. If we test this form using a truth table, we quickly find that it is **not a tautology**. Specifically, when $p$ is False and $q$ is True, both premises are true, but the conclusion ($p$) is false.

#### Textbook Example 10 Walkthrough:
Consider this argument:
1. *"If you do every problem in this book, then you will learn discrete mathematics."* ($p \rightarrow q$)
2. *"You learned discrete mathematics."* ($q$)
3. $\therefore$ *"You did every problem in this book."* ($p$)

* **The Analysis:** This argument is completely invalid. It is entirely possible to learn discrete mathematics through other means—such as attending lectures, reading notes, or studying with a peer—without doing *every* single problem in the textbook. Assuming $p$ must happen just because $q$ happened is a logical failure.

---

### 2. Fallacy of Denying the Hypothesis

This fallacy occurs when you incorrectly assume that if the hypothesis of a conditional statement is false, then the conclusion must automatically be false as well.

#### The Abstract Form:
$$\begin{aligned}
& p \rightarrow q \\
& \neg p \\
\hline
\therefore \quad & \neg q \quad \text{ (INVALID!)}
\end{aligned}$$

#### Why it is a Fallacy:
The underlying proposition is **$((p \rightarrow q) \land \neg p) \rightarrow \neg q$**. Like the previous fallacy, this is **not a tautology**. When $p$ is False and $q$ is True, the premises evaluate to True, but the conclusion ($\neg q$) evaluates to False.

#### Textbook Example 11 Walkthrough:
Consider this argument:
1. *"If you do every problem in this book, then you will learn discrete mathematics."* ($p \rightarrow q$)
2. *"You did not do every problem in this book."* ($\neg p$)
3. $\dots$ *"You did not learn discrete mathematics."* ($\neg q$)

* **The Analysis:** This argument is invalid. Even if you skip some problems in the book ($\neg p$), you might still master the subject material and learn discrete mathematics ($q$). Denying the premise does not mean the conclusion is denied.

---

### 3. Circular Reasoning (Begging the Question)

This fallacy is structurally different from the first two. In circular reasoning, the argument's form might technically be valid, but it is entirely useless because **the statement to be proven is already implicitly or explicitly buried inside the premises**.

When you assume the very truth of the conclusion at the start of your proof, you haven't actually proven anything.

#### Textbook Example:
> *"Prove that $n^2$ is an even integer when $n$ is an even integer."*

Suppose a student writes this proof:
> *"Suppose that $n^2$ is even. Then $n^2 = 2k$ for some integer $k$. Because $n^2$ is even, it follows that $n$ must be even."*

* **The Analysis:** Look closely at what happened. The goal was to *start* with $n$ being even and *prove* that $n^2$ is even. Instead, the student started by assuming $n^2$ is even to conclude that $n$ is even. This is **begging the question**. The core link of the proof was never actually demonstrated.

**Scenario:** An engineer writes a network routing rule:
1. *"If a data packet is corrupted, then the router drops it."*
2. Then, the router diagnostic log reports: *"Packet #404 was successfully dropped by the router."*
3. The engineer concludes: *"Therefore, packet #404 was definitely corrupted."*

Based on what we just studied:
1. Which specific fallacy did the engineer commit?
2. Provide a quick real-world "counterexample" scenario showing why the router might have dropped the packet even if it wasn't corrupted.

---

### 💡 Solutions & Explanation

> [!NOTE]
> Here is the diagnostic breakdown of the routing logic bug:
> 
> 1. **Fallacy Committed:** **Fallacy of Affirming the Conclusion**
>    * **Reasoning:** Let $p$ be *"Packet is corrupted"* and $q$ be *"Router drops packet"*. The premise is $p \rightarrow q$. The engineer observes $q$ (packet dropped) and concludes $p$ (packet is corrupted). This is the invalid form:
>      $$\begin{aligned} p \rightarrow q \\ q \\ \hline \therefore p \end{aligned}$$
> 2. **Factual CS Counterexamples:**
>    A router can drop packets for many valid operational reasons that have nothing to do with packet corruption:
>    * **Network Congestion (Tail Drop):** The router's buffer queues are completely full, so it drops newly arriving packets.
>    * **TTL Expiry:** The packet's Time-To-Live (TTL) field reached 0, so the router discarded it to prevent routing loops.
>    * **Firewall / Security Filtering:** The packet violated firewall rules or access control lists (ACLs) and was intentionally dropped.
>    * **No Route to Host:** The destination address was not present in the routing table, so the packet was unroutable.
