# 1.2.5 Logic Puzzles

Puzzles that can be solved using logical reasoning are known as **logic puzzles**. Solving these puzzles is an excellent, practical way to master working with the formal rules of logic.

In computer science, programs designed to carry out automated logical reasoning often use well-known logic puzzles as standard benchmarks to illustrate and test their capabilities.

Let's look at the three classic types of logic puzzles presented in your textbook, moving up in their level of difficulty.

---

### Type 1: The Inscribed Trunks Puzzle (Deductive Elimination)

This type of puzzle requires you to translate clues into basic compound propositions to systematically eliminate impossible scenarios.

#### Textbook Example 7 (The Treasure Trunks)

A King offers you a treasure hidden inside one of three closed trunks. The other two trunks are completely empty. Each trunk has an inscription:
* **Trunk 1:** "This trunk is empty".
* **Trunk 2:** "This trunk is empty".
* **Trunk 3:** "The treasure is in Trunk 2".

The Queen, who *never lies*, tells you that **exactly one of these inscriptions is true**, while the other two are completely false. Which trunk must you choose to win the treasure?

#### Step-by-Step Logical Solution:

Let $p_1, p_2,$ and $p_3$ be the propositions stating that the treasure is in Trunk 1, Trunk 2, and Trunk 3, respectively.
Now let's rewrite the three inscriptions in terms of our variables:
* Inscription 1 states: $\neg p_1$ 
* Inscription 2 states: $\neg p_2$ 
* Inscription 3 states: $p_2$ 

**The Key Insight:** Look at Inscriptions 2 and 3 ($\neg p_2$ and $p_2$). They are exact logical opposites of each other! This means that no matter where the treasure actually is, **one of these two inscriptions MUST be true, and the other MUST be false**.

Since the Queen guaranteed that *exactly one* of the three inscriptions is true, that single true inscription must be either Inscription 2 or Inscription 3. Therefore, **Inscription 1 ($\neg p_1$) is guaranteed to be false**.
* If Inscription 1 ($\neg p_1$) is false, its negation is true: $\neg(\neg p_1) \equiv p_1$.
* This proves that $p_1$ is True.

**Conclusion:** The treasure is in **Trunk 1**. (This makes Inscriptions 1 and 3 false, and Inscription 2 true, which perfectly matches the Queen's rule).

### Type 2: Knights and Knaves (Self-Reference Logic)

This highly famous puzzle archetype was created by the logician Raymond Smullyan. It takes place on an island inhabited by two types of people:
* **Knights:** Who *always* tell the truth.
* **Knaves:** Who *always* lie.

When solving these, you must test hypothetical truth assignments to see if they create a structural contradiction.

#### Textbook Example 8 (Two Inhabitants)

You meet two people, $A$ and $B$, on the island.
* $A$ tells you: **"B is a knight"**.
* $B$ tells you: **"The two of us are opposite types"**.

What are the identity types of $A$ and $B$?

#### Step-by-Step Logical Solution:

Let $p$ represent "$A$ is a knight" and $q$ represent "$B$ is a knight".
*(This implies $\neg p$ means "$A$ is a knave" and $\neg q$ means "$B$ is a knave")*.

Let's evaluate by testing the state of person $A$:

1. **Hypothesis 1: Assume $A$ is a Knight ($p = \text{True}$)**.
   * If $A$ is a knight, $A$ must speak the absolute truth.
   * Since $A$ said "B is a knight", then **$B$ must be a knight too ($q = \text{True}$)**.
   * If $B$ is a knight, then $B$'s statement must *also* be completely true.
   * However, $B$ stated "The two of us are opposite types". Since our tracking shows they are both knights, $B$'s statement is false, which is a structural contradiction.
   * Therefore, our initial assumption is wrong: **$A$ cannot be a knight ($\neg p = \text{True}$)**.

2. **Hypothesis 2: Assume $A$ is a Knave ($p = \text{False}$)**.
   * If $A$ is a knave, everything $A$ says is an absolute lie.
   * Since $A$ lied when saying "B is a knight", $B$ must actually be a **knave ($q = \text{False}$)**.
   * Let's verify this with $B$'s statement. $B$ claims that they are opposite types. Since $A$ is a knave and $B$ is a knave, they are actually the *same* type. This means $B$'s statement is a lie, which perfectly fits the profile of $B$ being a knave.

**Conclusion:** Both $A$ and $B$ are **knaves**.

### Type 3: The Muddy Children Puzzle (Epistemic Logic)

This represents an advanced class of puzzles dealing with **common knowledge**—what individuals know about what *other* individuals know.

#### Textbook Example 9 (Two Children)

A father tells his son and daughter to play in the backyard without getting dirty. While playing, both children happen to get mud on their own foreheads.

When they come inside, the father sits them down and says: **"At least one of you has a muddy forehead"**.
He then asks them to simultaneously answer "Yes" or "No" to the question: **"Do you know whether you have a muddy forehead?"** 

The children cannot see their own foreheads, but they can see their sibling's forehead. Assuming both children are perfectly honest and exceptional logicians, what do they answer each time the father asks the question?

#### Step-by-Step Logical Solution:

Let $s$ be the statement "the son has a muddy forehead" and $d$ be the statement "the daughter has a muddy forehead". The father's opening statement establishes that the disjunction **$s \lor d$ is True**.

* **Round 1: The First Time the Question is Asked**
  * The son looks at the daughter and sees mud on her ($d = \text{True}$). Knowing $d$ is true means the father's condition ($s \lor d$) is satisfied regardless of whether the son's own forehead is clean or muddy. He cannot deduce his own state, so he answers **"No"**.
  * The daughter looks at the son, sees mud on him ($s = \text{True}$), goes through the exact same logic, and answers **"No"**.

* **Round 2: The Second Time the Question is Asked**
  * Both children are brilliant logicians, so they pause to analyze why their sibling said "No" in Round 1.
  * Let's look at it from the daughter's perspective: She reasons, "If my forehead were completely clean ($d = \text{False}$), then my brother would have looked at me in Round 1, seen a clean face, and immediately known that he must be the muddy one to satisfy the father's statement ($s \lor d$). He would have answered 'Yes' right away. But he answered 'No'. Therefore, my forehead cannot be clean!" 
  * Because the son went through the exact same deduction about her "No", they both crack the puzzle.

**Conclusion:** The second time the question is asked, both children confidently answer **"Yes"**.
