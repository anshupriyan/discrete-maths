# 1.7 Introduction to Proofs

### 1.7.1 Introduction

In this section, we transition from the formal mechanics of system specifications and rules of inference to the core art of mathematics: **constructing proofs**.

A **proof** is a valid argument that establishes the absolute truth of a mathematical statement. A program code analyzer or an AI reasoning engine cannot simply assume a software protocol works; it requires a structural proof.

Constructing proofs is more than just applying mechanical operations. It requires a deep understanding of mathematical concepts, combined with creativity and intuition. In this section, we will introduce the basic vocabulary and the foundational methods used to prove mathematical assertions.

---

### 1.7.2 Some Terminologies

To reason precisely about mathematical statements, we must establish a clear, standardized vocabulary. In mathematical exposition, you will routinely encounter the following terms:
* **Theorem:** A mathematical statement that can be shown to be absolutely true.
  * *Note:* The term is usually reserved for statements that are considered quite important. For less critical statements, we use terms like *proposition*, *fact*, or *result*.
* **Proof:** A valid argument that establishes the truth of a theorem. It uses a sequence of statements where each statement is either a premise, an axiom, or logically deduced from preceding statements.
* **Axioms (or Postulates):** Statements that are assumed to be true without proof. These form the fundamental baseline assumptions of a mathematical system.
* **Lemma:** A "pre-theorem" or a less important theorem that is proven primarily to help establish the truth of a larger, more significant theorem.
* **Corollary:** A theorem that can be established directly and almost immediately from a major theorem that has just been proven.
* **Conjecture:** A statement that is proposed to be true, usually based on some partial evidence, a hunch, or intuition, but which has **not yet been proven or disproven**. Once a conjecture is formally proven, it upgrades to a **theorem**. If it is disproven, it is discarded.

---

### Legal Axioms for Number Theory Proofs

To practice building your very first proofs, your textbook utilizes the properties of **integers**. When writing proofs about numbers, we can freely assume the standard axioms of basic algebra (such as the commutative, associative, and distributive laws of addition and multiplication).

However, we must strictly adhere to the formal mathematical definition of what makes an integer **even** or **odd**:

> **Definition 1 (Even Integer):** An integer $n$ is **even** if there exists an integer $k$ such that:
> $$n = 2k$$

> **Definition 2 (Odd Integer):** An integer $n$ is **odd** if there exists an integer $k$ such that:
> $$n = 2k + 1$$

* *Note:* An integer is either even or odd, and no integer can be both.
* Two integers are said to have the **same parity** if they are both even or both odd. They have **opposite parity** if one is even and the other is odd.

---

### Turning Logic into Proof Frameworks

Most theorems are stated as conditionally quantified assertions, typically taking the form:

$$\forall x (P(x) \rightarrow Q(x))$$

To prove an implication like $P \rightarrow Q$, we don't need to look at every single element in an infinite domain. Instead, we select an **arbitrary, unnamed element $x$** from the domain, assume that the hypothesis $P(x)$ holds true for it, and show that the conclusion $Q(x)$ must also be true.

If our step-by-step logic is clean and holds for an arbitrary element, it applies universally to *all* elements in the domain.

---

## 1.7.3 Understanding How Theorems Are Stated

Before we dive into the actual methods of proving theorems, we must examine how mathematical theorems are structurally constructed.

In your textbook, you will find that many theorems are written as conditional statements:
$$\text{"If } P, \text{ then } Q\text{"} \quad \text{or} \quad P \rightarrow Q$$

However, in formal mathematical exposition, the full statement is almost always an implicitly or explicitly **universally quantified statement**. The complete formal blueprint looks like this:
$$\forall x (P(x) \rightarrow Q(x))$$

### The Universal Shortcut

When a theorem says something like *"The square of an even integer is even,"* it doesn't explicitly display the $\forall$ symbol, but it implicitly means: *"For **every** integer $x$ in the universe of discourse, if $x$ is even, then $x^2$ is even."*

To prove a statement structured this way, we use a logical rule of inference called **Universal Generalization**:
1. We select an **arbitrary, unnamed element $x$** from our domain of discourse. "Arbitrary" means we cannot assume anything special about $x$ other than the fact that it belongs to the domain and satisfies our hypothesis.
2. We show that the implication holds true for this single, mystery element.
3. Because our step-by-step reasoning relied strictly on algebra and logic without utilizing any unique properties of a specific number, the proof cleanly generalizes to **all** elements across the infinite domain.

---

## 1.7.4 Methods of Proving Theorems

To prove a conditional statement $P \rightarrow Q$ (or its universally quantified version), we need to show that it is completely impossible for the hypothesis $P$ to be true while the conclusion $Q$ is simultaneously false.

Your textbook outlines three major structural frameworks to accomplish this:
1. **Direct Proofs:** You assume $P$ is true, and march straight through algebra to show $Q$ must be true.
2. **Proof by Contraposition (Indirect):** You assume $Q$ is false ($\neg Q$), and use algebra to prove $P$ must be false ($\neg P$).
3. **Proof by Contradiction:** You assume the entire theorem is completely false ($P \land \neg Q$) and show that this assumption breaks a fundamental law of mathematics, causing a total logical system collapse.

---

## 1.7.5 Direct Proofs

A **direct proof** is the most straightforward method of establishing truth.

> **The Direct Proof Blueprint:**
> 1. Start by explicitly assuming that the hypothesis $P$ is **True**.
> 2. Use established definitions, legal algebraic properties, and rules of inference as stepping stones.
> 3. Arrive cleanly at the final statement showing that the conclusion $Q$ is **True**.

Let's look at two classic textbook proofs using this direct structure.

### Textbook Example 1: Multiplying an Even Integer
> **Theorem:** Prove that if $n$ is an even integer, then $n^2$ is an even integer.

* **Step 1: Identify the components.** Our hypothesis $P$ is *"$n$ is an even integer."* Our conclusion $Q$ is *"$n^2$ is an even integer."*
* **Step 2: State your assumption.** We begin by directly assuming our hypothesis: *"Suppose $n$ is an even integer."*
* **Step 3: Apply the strict definition.** By our formal definition of an even integer, because $n$ is even, there **must exist some integer $k$** such that:
  $$n = 2k$$
* **Step 4: Execute the target operation.** We need to find out what happens to $n^2$. Let's square both sides of our definition equation:
  $$n^2 = (2k)^2 = 4k^2$$
* **Step 5: Factor to reveal the target definition.** To prove $n^2$ is even, we must mathematically show it fits our structural definition template: $\text{Even} = 2 \times (\text{some integer})$. Let's factor a $2$ out of our result:
  $$n^2 = 2(2k^2)$$
  Since $k$ is an integer, $2k^2$ is also guaranteed to be an integer (let's call it $j$, where $j = 2k^2$).
* **Step 6: Close the loop.** Because we have expressed $n^2 = 2j$ where $j$ is an integer, $n^2$ perfectly satisfies the definition of an even integer.

The proof is complete! $\blacksquare$ *(The solid square symbol denotes **Q.E.D.**, which stands for quod erat demonstrandum—"which was to be demonstrated").*

### Textbook Example 2: Summing Two Odd Numbers
> **Theorem:** Prove that the sum of two odd integers is an even integer.

* **Step 1: State assumptions using distinct variables.** Suppose we have two arbitrary integers, $n$ and $m$, and they are both **odd**.
* **Step 2: Apply definitions.** By definition, there must exist integers $j$ and $k$ such that:
  $$n = 2j + 1 \quad \text{and} \quad m = 2k + 1$$
  *⚠️ Critical Error Alert:* Do not write $m = 2j + 1$. If you use the same variable $j$ for both numbers, you are accidentally assuming that the two numbers are identical! Your choice of variables must remain completely independent.
* **Step 3: Compute the sum.** Let's add the two expressions together algebraically:
  $$n + m = (2j + 1) + (2k + 1)$$
  $$n + m = 2j + 2k + 2$$
* **Step 4: Factor to reveal parity.** Let's factor out our target multiplier, $2$:
  $$n + m = 2(j + k + 1)$$
  Because $j$ and $k$ are integers, the interior block $(j + k + 1)$ resolves to a valid integer (let's name it $r$).
* **Step 5: Conclude.** Since $n + m = 2r$, the sum is formally proven to be an even integer. $\blacksquare$

## 1.7.6 Proof by Contraposition

Sometimes, a direct proof runs into a wall because unwrapping the hypothesis directly is algebraically awkward. When this happens, we turn to an **indirect proof** method called **proof by contraposition**.

This method relies on the core logical equivalence:
$$P \rightarrow Q \equiv \neg Q \rightarrow \neg P$$

Instead of trying to prove $P \rightarrow Q$ directly, we prove its identical twin statement: **"If the conclusion is false, then the hypothesis must be false."**

> **The Contraposition Blueprint:**
> 1. Start by explicitly assuming that the conclusion $Q$ is **False** (meaning **$\neg Q$ is True**).
>    * If $Q$ was stated to be even, you assume it is odd (and vice versa).
> 2. Work through algebra until you can demonstrate that the initial hypothesis $P$ is **False** (meaning **$\neg P$ is True**).

### Textbook Example 3: Reversing the Square Property
> **Theorem:** Prove that if $3n + 2$ is an odd integer, then $n$ is an odd integer.

* **Why a direct proof is painful here:** If you start by assuming $3n + 2 = 2k + 1$, solving for $n$ gives you $n = \frac{2k - 1}{3}$. Dealing with a fraction inside a discrete math definition is messy and difficult to evaluate. Let's use contraposition instead!
* **Step 1: Set up the contrapositive hypothesis ($\neg Q$).** Our conclusion was *"$n$ is an odd integer."* Its negation ($\neg Q$) is: *"Suppose $n$ is **not** an odd integer (which means $n$ is an **even** integer)."*
* **Step 2: Apply the definition.** Because $n$ is even, there exists an integer $k$ such that:
  $$n = 2k$$
* **Step 3: Substitute into our target expression.** Let's plug this value of $n$ directly into our initial expression, $3n + 2$:
  $$3n + 2 = 3(2k) + 2$$
  $$3n + 2 = 6k + 2$$
* **Step 4: Factor to evaluate parity.** Let's extract a $2$ to see if the resulting expression is even or odd:
  $$3n + 2 = 2(3k + 1)$$
  Since $k$ is an integer, $(3k + 1)$ is also an integer (let's call it $j$). Therefore, $3n + 2 = 2j$.
* **Step 5: Evaluate the outcome ($\neg P$).** We have just shown that $3n + 2$ satisfies the exact definition of an **even** integer. This means it is **not** odd, which is precisely **$\neg P$**.

**Conclusion:** We have proven that $\neg Q \rightarrow \neg P$ is true. Since the contrapositive is logically equivalent to the original statement, the original theorem is fully established! $\blacksquare$

## 1.7.7 Proofs by Contradiction

A **proof by contradiction** is an incredibly versatile mathematical tool. Instead of focusing on moving from a starting line to a finish line, you intentionally construct an impossible scenario.

This method is based on the logic that if an assumption forces a complete logical contradiction (such as proving a statement is both True and False simultaneously, or that $1 = 0$), then your initial assumption **must be completely false**.

> **The Contradiction Blueprint:**
> 1. Suppose you want to prove a statement $T$. You begin by assuming the exact opposite: **"Assume $\neg T$ is True."**
> 2. For a conditional statement $P \rightarrow Q$, the negation is **$P \land \neg Q$**. So, you assume the hypothesis $P$ is true **AND** the conclusion $Q$ is completely false.
> 3. Execute step-by-step logical calculations until you hit a breaking point where two statements clash directly (e.g., $r \land \neg r$).
> 4. Conclude that because your assumption broke mathematics, the assumption is false, meaning **$T$ must be true**.

### Textbook Example 10: The Classic Rationality Breakdown
To understand how deep contradiction proofs can go, let's look at one of the most famous proofs in human history: showing that the square root of 2 cannot be written as a clean fraction.

> **Theorem:** Prove that $\sqrt{2}$ is an irrational number.

* **Step 1: Assume the exact opposite ($\neg T$).** We start by assuming the negation of the theorem: *"Suppose $\sqrt{2}$ is **not** irrational. This means $\sqrt{2}$ is a **rational** number."*
* **Step 2: Apply the definition of a rational number.** By definition, any rational number can be written as a fraction of two integers, $a$ and $b$. Crucially, we can assume this fraction is written in **lowest terms** (meaning $a$ and $b$ share absolutely no common factors; they are coprime).
  $$\sqrt{2} = \frac{a}{b}$$
* **Step 3: Remove the radical using algebra.** Let's square both sides to eliminate the square root sign:
  $$2 = \frac{a^2}{b^2}$$
  Multiply both sides by $b^2$:
  $$2b^2 = a^2$$
* **Step 4: Deduce parity properties.** Look at the equation $a^2 = 2b^2$. Because $a^2$ is expressed as $2 \times (\text{an integer})$, **$a^2$ must be an even integer**.
  *(From our work in Example 1, we know that if a square of a number is even, the root number itself must be even).* Therefore, **$a$ is an even integer**.
* **Step 5: Track the variable substitution.** Since $a$ is even, we can write it as $a = 2k$ for some integer $k$. Let's plug this back into our tracking equation:
  $$2b^2 = a^2$$
  $$2b^2 = (2k)^2$$
  $$2b^2 = 4k^2$$
  Divide both sides by $2$:
  $$b^2 = 2k^2$$
* **Step 6: Discover the contradiction.** Look at our new equation: $b^2 = 2k^2$. This states that $b^2$ is equal to $2 \times (\text{an integer})$, which means **$b^2$ must be even**. Consequently, **$b$ must be an even integer too!**
* **The Crash point:** Look at what we have deduced:
  1. $a$ is even.
  2. $b$ is even.
  If both $a$ and $b$ are even, they both share a common factor of **2**. However, in Step 2, our absolute structural constraint was that the fraction $\frac{a}{b}$ was in **lowest terms and shared no common factors**.

Our calculations have forced a direct logical contradiction ($A \land \neg A$). Because our math was completely legal, the fault lies entirely with our opening assumption.

**Conclusion:** The assumption that $\sqrt{2}$ is rational is false. Therefore, $\sqrt{2}$ is **irrational**. $\blacksquare$

## 1.7.8 Mistakes in Proofs

Even experienced mathematicians and computer science engineers make mistakes when constructing proofs. Identifying structural "bugs" in mathematical reasoning is a critical skill, mirroring the process of debugging source code.

Your textbook outlines several common, specific errors that routinely appear in incorrect proofs. Let's look at how to recognize and avoid them.

---

### 1. The Fallacy of Affirming the Conclusion / Denying the Hypothesis
As we covered in Section 1.6.6, these are structural logic errors where a conditional statement is incorrectly flipped or negated.
* *Example:* Assuming that because $n^2$ is even, you can directly declare $n$ is even *without proving it first* (by contraposition or contradiction) is a structural leap that invalidates a proof step.

---

### 2. Arithmetic and Algebraic Mistakes
These are mechanical bugs where an invalid algebraic operation is executed. The most dangerous aspect of these errors is that they are often hidden behind variables, making it easy to accidentally divide by zero.

#### Textbook Example 14: Proving $1 = 2$ (The Division-by-Zero Bug)
Look closely at this step-by-step algebraic manipulation:
1. Let $a$ and $b$ be equal positive integers:
   $$a = b$$
2. Multiply both sides by $a$:
   $$a^2 = ab$$
3. Subtract $b^2$ from both sides:
   $$a^2 - b^2 = ab - b^2$$
4. Factor both sides using standard algebra (difference of squares on the left):
   $$(a - b)(a + b) = b(a - b)$$
5. Divide both sides by the common term $(a - b)$:
   $$a + b = b$$
6. Since $a = b$, substitute $b$ in place of $a$:
   $$b + b = b$$
   $$2b = b$$
7. Divide both sides by $b$:
   $$2 = 1$$

**Where is the bug?** The proof completely collapses at **Step 5**. Because we defined $a = b$ at the very beginning, the term $(a - b)$ is equal to **0**. Dividing both sides by $(a - b)$ is a **division by zero**, which is an mathematically undefined operation. Once an illegal operation is performed, you can structurally deduce any absurd conclusion you want (like $2 = 1$).

---

### 3. Begging the Question (Circular Reasoning)
This mistake occurs when you implicitly or explicitly introduce the conclusion you are trying to prove as a premise at the start of your argument.

#### Textbook Example 15: The Unproven Step
Suppose someone attempts to prove that if $n^2$ is an even integer, then $n$ is an even integer using this phrasing:
> *"Suppose $n^2$ is even. Then $n^2 = 2k$ for some integer $k$. Because $n^2$ is even, it follows that $n$ must be even."*

**The Error:** The sentence *"it follows that $n$ must be even"* completely skips the entire burden of proof. The author simply asserts the finish line as an obvious fact without providing the intermediate algebraic bridging steps.

---

### 4. Making Assumptions About Parity (The Variable Locking Bug)
When proving properties about multiple numbers, a frequent mistake is accidentally restricting your variables.
* *The Bug:* Proving a statement about "two odd integers $n$ and $m$" by writing:
  $$n = 2k + 1 \quad \text{and} \quad m = 2k + 1$$
* *Why it breaks:* By utilizing the exact same index variable $k$ for both definitions, you have accidentally forced $n$ to equal $m$. Your proof now only applies to scenarios where you add or multiply an odd number *by itself*, failing to generalize to distinct numbers like $3$ and $7$. You must always use unique independent variables: $n = 2k + 1$ and $m = 2j + 1$.

## 1.7.9 Just a Beginning

Section 1.7 closes with a crucial philosophical reminder about the nature of mathematics: **this is just the beginning of your journey with proofs.**

Up to this point, we have focused on relatively straightforward properties of even and odd integers. However, the foundational frameworks you have mastered here—**Direct Proofs, Contraposition, and Contradiction**—are the exact same tools utilized to construct massive, complex systems across modern computer science and mathematics.

As you advance, you will apply these exact proof frameworks to establish the correctness of:
* **Relational Databases:** Proving that queries retrieve data accurately.
* **Cryptography Protocols:** Formally proving that an encryption scheme cannot be broken in polynomial time.
* **Graph Theory and Networks:** Demonstrating that a routing protocol will always find the shortest path without entering an infinite loop.

**Scenario:** A student submits the following proof attempting to show that *“The product of any two irrational numbers is irrational”*:

> *"Let $x = \sqrt{2}$ and $y = \sqrt{3}$. Both $x$ and $y$ are irrational numbers. Their product is $x \cdot y = \sqrt{2} \cdot \sqrt{3} = \sqrt{6}$. Since $\sqrt{6}$ is irrational, the theorem is proven."*

Based on the textbook rules we have studied:
1. What fundamental mistake did this student make regarding how theorems are universally quantified?
2. Provide a simple **counterexample** involving two irrational numbers whose product turns out to be completely **rational** (disproving the student's statement!).

---

### 💡 Solutions & Explanation

> [!NOTE]
> Here is the debugging breakdown of the student's incorrect proof:
> 
> 1. **Mistake (Proving by Example):**
>    * **Reasoning:** The theorem is a universally quantified statement: *"For **all** real numbers $x$ and $y$, if $x$ and $y$ are irrational, then $x \cdot y$ is irrational."*
>    * The student attempted to prove this by showing it works for *one specific pair* of numbers ($x = \sqrt{2}$ and $y = \sqrt{3}$). An example is never sufficient to establish a universal statement. To prove a universal statement, the reasoning must hold for *arbitrary* elements, or we must show it works algebraically.
> 2. **Factual Counterexample:**
>    To disprove a universal statement, we only need to provide a single counterexample:
>    * Let $x = \sqrt{2}$ and $y = \sqrt{2}$. Both are irrational numbers.
>    * Their product is: $x \cdot y = \sqrt{2} \cdot \sqrt{2} = 2$.
>    * Since $2$ is an integer (and therefore rational), the product of these two irrational numbers is **rational**, disproving the theorem.
>    * *(Other valid counterexamples include $x = \sqrt{2}$ and $y = \sqrt{8}$, where $x \cdot y = \sqrt{16} = 4$).*
