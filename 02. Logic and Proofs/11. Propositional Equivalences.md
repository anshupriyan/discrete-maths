# 1.3 Propositional Equivalences

### 1.3.1 Introduction

An essential step in constructing a mathematical or computational argument is replacing a statement with another statement that shares the exact same truth value. Because of this, methods that produce propositions with identical truth values are used extensively when building valid arguments.

In this discussion, the term **compound proposition** refers to any logical expression formed from propositional variables using logical operators (such as $p \land q$). We can systematically classify these compound propositions into three distinct categories based on their possible truth values:

---

### The Three Classifications of Compound Propositions

#### 1. Tautology

> **Definition:** A compound proposition that is **always true**, no matter what individual truth values are assigned to the propositional variables occurring within it.

* **Significance:** Tautologies represent absolute logical truths and serve as the core foundation for rules of mathematical reasoning and inference.

#### 2. Contradiction

> **Definition:** A compound proposition that is **always false**, regardless of the truth values assigned to its component variables.

* **Significance:** Contradictions are the exact polar opposite of tautologies. They represent logical impossibilities and are used heavily as targets in proofs by contradiction.

#### 3. Contingency

> **Definition:** A compound proposition that is **neither a tautology nor a contradiction**.

* **Significance:** A contingency's final truth value is not fixed; it is completely dependent on the specific values allocated to its underlying variables. It is true in some scenarios and false in others.

---

### Textbook Walkthrough: Example 1 (Tautology vs. Contradiction)

We can easily construct basic examples of a tautology and a contradiction using just a single propositional variable, $p$. Let's examine their truth tables side-by-side to see how they behave:

| $p$ | $\neg p$ | $p \lor \neg p$ (Disjunction) | $p \land \neg p$ (Conjunction) |
| :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: |
| **T** | F | **T** | **F** |
| **F** | T | **T** | **F** |

#### Analysis:

* **Evaluating $p \lor \neg p$:** This statement translates in English to *"p is true, or p is not true"*. Looking at the third column of the truth table, it evaluates to **True** in every single row. Because it is always true, **$p \lor \neg p$ is a Tautology**.
* **Evaluating $p \land \neg p$:** This statement translates to *"p is true, and p is not true"*. Looking at the fourth column of the truth table, it evaluates to **False** in every single row. Because it can never be true under any condition, **$p \land \neg p$ is a Contradiction**.

### 💡 Solutions & Explanation

> [!NOTE]
> Here is the logical breakdown of the expression:
> 
> * **Classification:** **Tautology**
> * **Reasoning:**
>   Let's analyze the implication $A \to B$, where the hypothesis is $A = p$ and the conclusion is $B = (p \lor q)$. An implication is only false if the hypothesis is true and the conclusion is false ($1 \to 0$).
>   
>   1. **Case 1: $p$ is False ($0$)**
>      If $p$ is false, the hypothesis of the implication is false. Since a false hypothesis makes the conditional statement vacuously true, the entire statement $p \to (p \lor q)$ is **True** (regardless of the value of $q$).
>   2. **Case 2: $p$ is True ($1$)**
>      If $p$ is true, the hypothesis is true. Now we look at the conclusion $(p \lor q)$. Since $p$ is true, the disjunction $(p \lor q)$ must also be **True** (at least one operand is true). Since both the hypothesis and conclusion are true, the implication $1 \to 1$ evaluates to **True**.
>   
>   Since the expression is True under all assignments of $p$ and $q$, it is a **tautology**.

## 1.3.2 Logical Equivalences

In formal logic, compound propositions that have the same truth values in all possible cases are called **logically equivalent**. This concept is a cornerstone of discrete mathematics, as it allows us to substitute a complex statement with a simpler or more convenient variant without changing the logical meaning.

---

### Definition and Notation

> **Definition:** The compound propositions $p$ and $q$ are called **logically equivalent** if $p \leftrightarrow q$ is a tautology.

We use the symbol **$\equiv$** (or sometimes $\Leftrightarrow$) to denote logical equivalence:

$$p \equiv q$$

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **Crucial Remark:** The symbol $\equiv$ is **not** a logical connective, and $p \equiv q$ is not a compound proposition. Instead, it is a meta-logical statement or assertion that the biconditional expression $p \leftrightarrow q$ is a tautology.

---

### Verifying Equivalence Using Truth Tables

One direct way to determine whether two compound propositions are logically equivalent is to use a truth table. The compound propositions are equivalent **if and only if the columns giving their truth values match exactly row-for-row**.

#### Textbook Example 2: Verifying De Morgan's Laws

Let's establish an extremely important logical equivalence: $\neg(p \lor q) \equiv \neg p \land \neg q$. This rule is one of the two **De Morgan's Laws**.

Let's build the truth table columns to compare them:

| $p$ | $q$ | $p \lor q$ | $\neg(p \lor q)$ | $\neg p$ | $\neg q$ | $\neg p \land \neg q$ |
| :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: |
| **T** | **T** | T | **F** | F | F | **F** |
| **T** | **F** | T | **F** | F | T | **F** |
| **F** | **T** | T | **F** | T | F | **F** |
| **F** | **F** | F | **T** | T | T | **T** |

* **Analysis:** Look closely at the 4th column ($\neg(p \lor q)$) and the 7th column ($\neg p \land \neg q$). Because their evaluated truth values agree completely for all possible combinations of inputs, the biconditional between them is a tautology, proving they are logically equivalent.

---

### Overcoming the Growth of Truth Tables

A truth table involving $n$ unique propositional variables requires **$2^n$ rows**.
* For 2 variables ($p, q$), you need $2^2 = 4$ rows.
* For 3 variables ($p, q, r$), you need $2^3 = 8$ rows.
* For 4 variables, you need $2^4 = 16$ rows.

Because $2^n$ grows exponentially, using truth tables to establish logical equivalences quickly becomes impractical as the number of variables expands. To handle large compound expressions efficiently, we must utilize a standard toolkit of **established logical identities**.

---

### Core Tables of Logical Identities

Here are the critical, textbook-defined equivalences you need to commit to memory. In these tables, **$\text{T}$** represents a compound proposition that is always true, and **$\text{F}$** represents one that is always false.

#### 1. Fundamental Logical Equivalences

| Equivalence Identity | Name of the Law |
| :--- | :--- |
| $p \land \text{T} \equiv p$<br>$p \lor \text{F} \equiv p$ | **Identity Laws** |
| $p \lor \text{T} \equiv \text{T}$<br>$p \land \text{F} \equiv \text{F}$ | **Domination Laws** |
| $p \lor p \equiv p$<br>$p \land p \equiv p$ | **Idempotent Laws** |
| $\neg(\neg p) \equiv p$ | **Double Negation Law** |
| $p \lor q \equiv q \lor p$<br>$p \land q \equiv q \land p$ | **Commutative Laws** |
| $(p \lor q) \lor r \equiv p \lor (q \lor r)$<br>$(p \land q) \land r \equiv p \land (q \land r)$ | **Associative Laws** |
| $p \lor (q \land r) \equiv (p \lor q) \land (p \lor r)$<br>$p \land (q \lor r) \equiv (p \land q) \lor (p \land r)$ | **Distributive Laws** |
| $\neg(p \land q) \equiv \neg p \lor \neg q$<br>$\neg(p \lor q) \equiv \neg p \land \neg q$ | **De Morgan’s Laws** |
| $p \lor (p \land q) \equiv p$<br>$p \land (p \lor q) \equiv p$ | **Absorption Laws** |
| $p \lor \neg p \equiv \text{T}$<br>$p \land \neg p \equiv \text{F}$ | **Negation Laws** |

#### 2. Equivalences Involving Conditional Statements ($\rightarrow$)

This table allows us to algebraically strip away implications and replace them with negations, disjunctions, and conjunctions.

| Equivalence |
| :--- |
| **$p \rightarrow q \equiv \neg p \lor q$** *(Conditional-Disjunction Equivalence)* |
| **$p \rightarrow q \equiv \neg q \rightarrow \neg p$** *(Contrapositive Equivalence)* |
| $p \lor q \equiv \neg p \rightarrow q$ |
| $p \land q \equiv \neg(p \rightarrow \neg q)$ |
| $\neg(p \rightarrow q) \equiv p \land \neg q$ |
| $(p \rightarrow q) \land (p \rightarrow r) \equiv p \rightarrow (q \land r)$ |
| $(p \rightarrow r) \land (q \rightarrow r) \equiv (p \lor q) \rightarrow r$ |
| $(p \rightarrow q) \lor (p \rightarrow r) \equiv p \rightarrow (q \lor r)$ |
| $(p \rightarrow r) \lor (q \rightarrow r) \equiv (p \land q) \rightarrow r$ |

#### 3. Equivalences Involving Biconditional Statements ($\leftrightarrow$)

| Equivalence |
| :--- |
| $p \leftrightarrow q \equiv (p \rightarrow q) \land (q \rightarrow p)$ |
| $p \leftrightarrow q \equiv \neg p \leftrightarrow \neg q$ |
| $p \leftrightarrow q \equiv (p \land q) \lor (\neg p \land \neg q)$ |
| $\neg(p \leftrightarrow q) \equiv p \leftrightarrow \neg q$ |

---

### ⚠️ Common Pitfall Warning

> [!WARNING]
> Be exceptionally careful **not** to apply logical identities like the associative, distributive, or De Morgan's laws to an expression that contains a mixed combination of conjunctions ($\land$) and disjunctions ($\lor$) unless the specific identity explicitly permits it.
> * The associative law **only** works when all operators are identical (e.g., $p \lor q \lor r$ or $p \land q \land r$). Mixing them without clear parentheses changes the meaning entirely!

## 1.3.4 Constructing New Logical Equivalences

The established logical identities found in Table 6, along with conditional and biconditional identities (Tables 7 and 8), can be systematically used to construct additional logical equivalences.

### The Core Principle: Substitution

The structural reason we can build new equivalences without resorting to truth tables is that **a component proposition inside a compound expression can be replaced by a logically equivalent statement without changing the final truth value of the overall expression**.

Furthermore, logic follows the **transitive property**: if an expression $A \equiv B$ and $B \equiv C$, then we can confidently state that $A \equiv C$. This allows us to construct proofs as a continuous chain of step-by-step algebraic replacements, changing exactly one law at a time.

---

### Step-by-Step Textbook Proof Walkthroughs

#### EXAMPLE 6: Negation of an Implication

> **Problem:** Show that $\neg(p \rightarrow q)$ and $p \land \neg q$ are logically equivalent by developing a series of logical equivalences.

$$\neg(p \rightarrow q)$$

1. $\equiv \neg(\neg p \lor q)$
   * **Reason:** *Conditional-Disjunction Equivalence* ($p \rightarrow q \equiv \neg p \lor q$).
2. $\equiv \neg(\neg p) \land \neg q$
   * **Reason:** *Second De Morgan's Law* ($\neg(A \lor B) \equiv \neg A \land \neg B$), distributing the negation inside and flipping the operator from $\lor$ to $\land$.
3. $\equiv p \land \neg q$
   * **Reason:** *Double Negation Law* ($\neg(\neg p) \equiv p$).

**Conclusion:** $\neg(p \rightarrow q) \equiv p \land \neg q$ is formally proven.

---

#### EXAMPLE 7: Simplifying a Complex Disjunction

> **Problem:** Show that $\neg(p \lor (\neg p \land q))$ and $\neg p \land \neg q$ are logically equivalent.

$$\neg(p \lor (\neg p \land q))$$

1. $\equiv \neg p \land \neg(\neg p \land q)$
   * **Reason:** *Second De Morgan's Law* applied to the outer disjunction.
2. $\equiv \neg p \land [\neg(\neg p) \lor \neg q]$
   * **Reason:** *First De Morgan's Law* applied to the inner clause, transforming the internal $\land$ to an $\lor$.
3. $\equiv \neg p \land (p \lor \neg q)$
   * **Reason:** *Double Negation Law* cleans up $\neg(\neg p)$ to $p$.
4. $\equiv (\neg p \land p) \lor (\neg p \land \neg q)$
   * **Reason:** *Second Distributive Law*, expanding $\neg p \land$ across the terms inside the parentheses.
5. $\equiv \text{F} \lor (\neg p \land \neg q)$
   * **Reason:** *Negation Law* ($\neg p \land p \equiv \text{F}$), since a variable and its opposite can never be true at the same time.
6. $\equiv (\neg p \land \neg q) \lor \text{F}$
   * **Reason:** *Commutative Law for Disjunction*, flipping the order of the elements.
7. $\equiv \neg p \land \neg q$
   * **Reason:** *Identity Law for $\text{F}$* ($A \lor \text{F} \equiv A$), leaving the expression simplified.

**Conclusion:** Both expressions are logically equivalent.

---

#### EXAMPLE 8: Proving a Tautology Algebraically

> **Problem:** Demonstrate that $(p \land q) \rightarrow (p \lor q)$ is a tautology using logical identities.

To prove an expression is a tautology, our goal is to systematically reduce it until it evaluates directly to **$\text{T}$** (True):

$$(p \land q) \rightarrow (p \lor q)$$

1. $\equiv \neg(p \land q) \lor (p \lor q)$
   * **Reason:** *Conditional-Disjunction Equivalence* applied to the main implication.
2. $\equiv (\neg p \lor \neg q) \lor (p \lor q)$
   * **Reason:** *First De Morgan's Law* distributed across the first term.
3. $\equiv (\neg p \lor p) \lor (\neg q \lor q)$
   * **Reason:** *Associative and Commutative Laws for Disjunction*, grouping variables with their respective negations.
4. $\equiv \text{T} \lor \text{T}$
   * **Reason:** *Negation Laws* ($A \lor \neg A \equiv \text{T}$) along with the commutative law.
5. $\equiv \text{T}$
   * **Reason:** *Domination Law* ($\text{T} \lor \text{T} \equiv \text{T}$).

**Conclusion:** Because the statement collapses entirely to $\text{T}$, it is proven to be a tautology.

### 💡 Solutions & Explanation

> [!NOTE]
> Here is the step-by-step algebraic proof:
> 
> $$\text{LHS: } \neg p \rightarrow (q \rightarrow r)$$
> 
> 1. **$\equiv \neg p \rightarrow (\neg q \lor r)$**
>    * **Reason:** *Conditional-Disjunction Equivalence* ($q \rightarrow r \equiv \neg q \lor r$) on the inner implication.
> 2. **$\equiv \neg(\neg p) \lor (\neg q \lor r)$**
>    * **Reason:** *Conditional-Disjunction Equivalence* on the outer implication.
> 3. **$\equiv p \lor (\neg q \lor r)$**
>    * **Reason:** *Double Negation Law* ($\neg(\neg p) \equiv p$).
> 4. **$\equiv \neg q \lor (p \lor r)$**
>    * **Reason:** *Associative and Commutative Laws for Disjunction*, allowing us to regroup terms as all operators are disjunctions ($\lor$).
> 5. **$\equiv q \rightarrow (p \lor r)$**
>    * **Reason:** *Conditional-Disjunction Equivalence* ($\neg q \lor A \equiv q \rightarrow A$) rewritten in conditional form.
> 
> Since LHS simplifies to RHS, the equivalence is proven: **$\neg p \rightarrow (q \rightarrow r) \equiv q \rightarrow (p \lor r)$**.
