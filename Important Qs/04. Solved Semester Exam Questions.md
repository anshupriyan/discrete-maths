### Question 1(a)
**Question:**
Of a group of 20 students, 10 are interested in music, 7 are interested in photography, and 4 like swimming. Furthermore 4 are interested in both music and photography, 3 are interested in both music and swimming, 2 are interested in both photography and swimming and 1 is interested in music, photography and swimming. How many students are interested in photography but not in music and swimming?

---

#### Solution:
Let $M, P,$ and $S$ be the sets of students interested in Music, Photography, and Swimming respectively. We are given:
* Total students $|U| = 20$
* $|M| = 10, \quad |P| = 7, \quad |S| = 4$
* $|M \cap P| = 4, \quad |M \cap S| = 3, \quad |P \cap S| = 2$
* $|M \cap P \cap S| = 1$

We want to find the number of students interested in photography but *not* in music and swimming. This corresponds to the set difference:
$$|P \setminus (M \cup S)|$$

Using the Venn diagram partition of $P$:
$$|P \setminus (M \cup S)| = |P| - |P \cap M| - |P \cap S| + |P \cap M \cap S|$$
$$|P \setminus (M \cup S)| = 7 - 4 - 2 + 1 = \mathbf{2 \text{ students}}$$

**Answer:**
There are **2** students interested in photography but not in music and swimming.

### Question 1(b)
**Question:**
Prove that the given Boolean expression is a tautology using equivalence rules:
$$(\neg p \land q) \rightarrow (\neg(q \rightarrow p))$$

---

#### Solution:
To prove this expression is a tautology, we apply propositional equivalence rules to simplify the implication:

$$\text{Expression} \equiv (\neg p \land q) \rightarrow \neg(q \rightarrow p)$$

1. **Apply the Conditional Identity** to the right-hand side's inner implication ($A \rightarrow B \equiv \neg A \lor B$):
   $$\equiv (\neg p \land q) \rightarrow \neg(\neg q \lor p)$$

2. **Apply De Morgan's Law** to the right-hand side negated term:
   $$\equiv (\neg p \land q) \rightarrow (\neg(\neg q) \land \neg p)$$

3. **Apply the Double Negation Law** ($\neg(\neg q) \equiv q$):
   $$\equiv (\neg p \land q) \rightarrow (q \land \neg p)$$

4. **Apply the Commutative Law** of conjunction to the right-hand side:
   $$\equiv (\neg p \land q) \rightarrow (\neg p \land q)$$

5. **Apply the Conditional Identity** to the main implication ($X \rightarrow X \equiv \neg X \lor X$):
   $$\equiv \neg(\neg p \land q) \lor (\neg p \land q)$$

6. **Apply the Negation Law** ($\neg Y \lor Y \equiv \mathbf{T}$):
   $$\equiv \mathbf{T}$$

Since the expression simplifies directly to $\mathbf{T}$, it is a **tautology**.

**Question:**
Given $f(x) = x^2 + 1$ and $g(x) = x + 2$. Find $f \circ g$ and $g \circ f$ where $f$ and $g$ are functions from R to R.

#### Solution:

2. **Find $g \circ f(x)$:**
   $$g \circ f(x) = g(f(x)) = g(x^2 + 1) = (x^2 + 1) + 2 = \mathbf{x^2 + 3}$$

### Question 1(d)
**Question:**
Consider the following advertisement of a game:
(i) There are three statements in this advertisement.
(ii) Two of them are not true.
(iii) The average increase in IQ scores of people who learned this game is more than 20 points.
Prove that the statement (iii) is true using truth table.

---

#### Solution:
Let's define the three statements as propositional variables:
* $S_1$: "There are three statements in this advertisement."
* $S_2$: "Two of them are not true."
* $S_3$: "The average increase in IQ scores of people who learned this game is more than 20 points."

1. **Establish the truth value of $S_1$:**
   * Counting the bullet points in the advertisement, there are exactly three statements: (i), (ii), and (iii).
   * Therefore, $S_1$ is factually **True** ($S_1 = \mathbf{T}$).

2. **Construct the truth table cases for $S_2$ and $S_3$:**
   Since $S_1 = \mathbf{T}$, we analyze the remaining combinations of truth values for $S_2$ and $S_3$:

| Case | $S_1$ | $S_2$ | $S_3$ | Number of False Statements | Does $S_2$ ("Two of them are false") match the count? | Consistency |
| :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: |
| 1. | **T** | **T** | **T** | 0 | No (claims 2 are false, but 0 are) | Inconsistent ($S_2 \to \mathbf{F}$) |
| 2. | **T** | **T** | **F** | 1 | No (claims 2 are false, but only 1 is) | Inconsistent ($S_2 \to \mathbf{F}$) |
| **3.** | **T** | **F** | **T** | **1** | **Yes** (claims 2 are false, which is false since only 1 is) | **Consistent** |
| 4. | **T** | **F** | **F** | 2 | No (claims 2 are false, which is true, making $S_2$ true) | Inconsistent ($S_2 \to \mathbf{T}$) |

3. **Conclusion:**
   * The only logically consistent row is **Case 3**, where $S_1 = \mathbf{T}$, $S_2 = \mathbf{F}$, and $S_3 = \mathbf{T}$.
   * Therefore, statement (iii) must be **True**.

### Question 1(f)
**Question:**
Determine whether the given graphs G1 and G2 are isomorphic or not.

*(G1 is a pentagon cycle $u_1-u_2-u_3-u_4-u_5-u_1$ and G2 is a 5-pointed star)*

---

#### Solution:

1. **Analyze simple invariants:**
   * Both graphs have 5 vertices and 5 edges.
   * Both graphs are 2-regular (every vertex has degree 2).

2. **Trace cycles:**
   * Graph $G_1$ is a cycle of length 5 ($C_5$).
   * Let's trace the edges of $G_2$ starting at $v_1$:
     $$v_1 - v_3 - v_5 - v_2 - v_4 - v_1$$
   * This path visits every vertex in $G_2$ exactly once and returns to the start. Thus, $G_2$ is also a cycle of length 5 ($C_5$).

3. **Define isomorphism mapping $f: V(G_1) \to V(G_2)$:**
   * Map the vertices sequentially along the cycle paths:
     $$f(u_1) = v_1, \ f(u_2) = v_3, \ f(u_3) = v_5, \ f(u_4) = v_2, \ f(u_5) = v_4$$
   * This bijection preserves all edge adjacencies:
     * $(u_1, u_2) \to (v_1, v_3)$ (edge in $G_2$)
     * $(u_2, u_3) \to (v_3, v_5)$ (edge in $G_2$)
     * $(u_3, u_4) \to (v_5, v_2)$ (edge in $G_2$)
     * $(u_4, u_5) \to (v_2, v_4)$ (edge in $G_2$)
     * $(u_5, u_1) \to (v_4, v_1)$ (edge in $G_2$)

Since there exists an adjacency-preserving bijection, the two graphs are **isomorphic**.

### Question 1(g)
**Question:**
Does there exist a simple graph with seven vertices having following given degree sequence: (1, 3, 3, 4, 5, 6, 6)?

---

#### Solution:

1. **Check Handshaking Theorem:**
   * Sum of degrees $= 1 + 3 + 3 + 4 + 5 + 6 + 6 = 28$ (which is even, so it passes the Handshaking Lemma).

2. **Analyze degree constraints for simple graphs:**
   * Let $G$ be a simple graph on $V = 7$ vertices.
   * The maximum possible degree of any vertex in a simple graph with 7 vertices is $V - 1 = 6$.
   * The degree sequence contains **two** vertices of degree 6. Let these vertices be $u$ and $v$.
   * Since $u$ and $v$ have degree 6, they must be connected to every other vertex in the graph.
   * This implies that every vertex in the graph must have an edge to both $u$ and $v$.
   * Consequently, the minimum degree of any vertex in $G$ must be at least 2.
   * However, the given degree sequence contains a vertex of degree 1.

Since a vertex of degree 1 cannot exist in a 7-vertex simple graph containing two vertices of degree 6, no such simple graph can exist.

### Question 1(h)
**Question:**
Use Master method to find asymptotic bounds for the following recurrence relation:
$$T(n) = T(9n/10) + n$$

---

#### Solution:
The Master Theorem applies to recurrences of the form $T(n) = a T(n/b) + f(n)$.

1. **Identify parameters:**
   * $a = 1, \quad b = \frac{10}{9}$
   * $f(n) = n$

2. **Compute $n^{\log_b a}$:**
   * $n^{\log_{10/9} 1} = n^0 = 1$

3. **Compare $f(n)$ and $n^{\log_b a}$:**
   * $f(n) = n = \Omega(n^{0 + \epsilon})$ for $\epsilon = 1 > 0$. We check **Case 3** of the Master Theorem.

4. **Verify the regularity condition for Case 3:**
   * We must show that $a f(n/b) \le c f(n)$ for some constant $c < 1$ and all sufficiently large $n$:
     $$1 \cdot \left( \frac{9n}{10} \right) \le c n \implies \frac{9}{10} n \le c n$$
   * This is true for $c = \frac{9}{10} < 1$.

5. **Apply Master Theorem:**
   * Since Case 3 holds, the asymptotic bound is:
     $$T(n) = \Theta(f(n)) = \mathbf{\Theta(n)}$$

### Question 1(i)
**Question:**
Solve the following recurrence relation using generating function method:
$$a_r = 3 a_{r-1} + 2, \quad \text{where } r \ge 1 \text{ with the boundary condition } a_0 = 1$$

---

#### Solution:

1. **Define the generating function:**
   $$A(z) = \sum_{r=0}^\infty a_r z^r$$

2. **Apply the recurrence relation:**
   Multiply the recurrence by $z^r$ and sum from $r=1$ to $\infty$:
   $$\sum_{r=1}^\infty a_r z^r = 3 \sum_{r=1}^\infty a_{r-1} z^r + 2 \sum_{r=1}^\infty z^r$$
   $$A(z) - a_0 = 3 z A(z) + 2 \left( \frac{1}{1-z} - 1 \right)$$
   Substitute $a_0 = 1$:
   $$A(z) - 1 = 3 z A(z) + \frac{2z}{1-z}$$
   $$A(z)(1 - 3z) = 1 + \frac{2z}{1-z} = \frac{1+z}{1-z}$$
   $$A(z) = \frac{1+z}{(1-z)(1-3z)}$$

3. **Perform partial fraction decomposition:**
   $$\frac{1+z}{(1-z)(1-3z)} = \frac{X}{1-z} + \frac{Y}{1-3z} \implies 1+z = X(1-3z) + Y(1-z)$$
   * Set $z = 1$: $2 = X(-2) \implies X = -1$
   * Set $z = 1/3$: $\frac{4}{3} = Y\left(\frac{2}{3}\right) \implies Y = 2$
   So:
   $$A(z) = \frac{2}{1-3z} - \frac{1}{1-z}$$

4. **Expand using geometric series:**
   $$A(z) = 2 \sum_{r=0}^\infty (3z)^r - \sum_{r=0}^\infty z^r = \sum_{r=0}^\infty \left[ 2 \cdot 3^r - 1 \right] z^r$$

5. **Identify $a_r$:**
   $$a_r = 2 \cdot 3^r - 1 \quad \text{for } r \ge 0$$

---

### Question 2(a)
**Question:**
A jigsaw puzzle consists of a number of pieces. Two or more pieces with matched boundaries can be put together to form a "big" piece. Finally, when all pieces are put together as one single block, the jigsaw puzzle is set to be solved. Putting two blocks with matched boundaries together is counted as one move. Use principle of Mathematical Induction to prove that for a jigsaw puzzle with $n$ pieces it will take $n - 1$ moves to solve the puzzle.

---

#### Solution:
We will prove this by **Strong Mathematical Induction** on the number of pieces $n \ge 1$:

Let $P(n)$ be the statement: "Any jigsaw puzzle with $n$ pieces requires exactly $n - 1$ moves to solve."

1. **Basis Step ($n = 1$):**
   * A puzzle with 1 piece is already assembled (0 moves).
   * Since $n - 1 = 1 - 1 = 0$ moves, $P(1)$ is true.

2. **Inductive Step:**
   * Assume $P(k)$ is true for all $1 \le k \le n$ (**Inductive Hypothesis**):
     "Any puzzle segment with $k$ pieces requires exactly $k-1$ moves to assemble."
   * We show $P(n+1)$ is true: "A puzzle with $n+1$ pieces requires exactly $n$ moves to solve."
   * Consider the final move of solving a puzzle with $n+1$ pieces:
     * This move joins two assembled blocks: Block 1 (size $n_1$) and Block 2 (size $n_2$).
     * Since all pieces are combined, $n_1 + n_2 = n+1$, with $1 \le n_1 \le n$ and $1 \le n_2 \le n$.
     * By the inductive hypothesis, Block 1 took $n_1 - 1$ moves, and Block 2 took $n_2 - 1$ moves.
     * The final step of joining the two blocks takes exactly $1$ move.
     * The total moves $M$ is:
       $$M = (n_1 - 1) + (n_2 - 1) + 1 = n_1 + n_2 - 1$$
     * Substitute $n_1 + n_2 = n+1$:
       $$M = (n+1) - 1 = n \text{ moves}$$
   * Thus, $P(n+1)$ holds.

**Conclusion:**
By strong mathematical induction, it takes exactly $n-1$ moves to solve a jigsaw puzzle with $n$ pieces.

### Question 3(c)
**Question:**
Give Big-O notation for factorial function.

---

#### Solution:
The factorial function is $f(n) = n! = 1 \times 2 \times 3 \times \dots \times n$ for $n \ge 1$.

1. **Derive the upper bound:**
   * Each term in the product $n!$ is less than or equal to $n$:
     $$n! = 1 \times 2 \times \dots \times n \le n \times n \times \dots \times n = n^n$$
   * Therefore, $n! \le n^n$ for all $n \ge 1$.
   * By definition, choosing $C = 1$ and $n_0 = 1$ gives:
     $$n! = O(n^n)$$

2. **Logarithmic Big-O bound:**
   * Often, we analyze the growth of $\log(n!)$:
     $$\log(n!) = \sum_{i=1}^n \log i \le \sum_{i=1}^n \log n = n \log n$$
   * Therefore, $\log(n!) = O(n \log n)$.

### Question 4(b)
**Question:**
A factory makes custom sports cars at an increasing rate. In the first month only one car is made, in the second month two cars are made, and so on, with n cars made in the nth month.
(i) Set up a recurrence relation for the number of cars produced in the first n months by this factory.
(ii) Use recurrence equation to solve how many cars are produced in the first year.
(iii) Find an explicit formula for the number of cars produced in the first n months by this factory.

---

#### Solution:
Let $c_n$ be the total number of cars produced in the first $n$ months.

1. **(i) Recurrence Relation:**
   * The number of cars produced in month $n$ is $n$.
   * The total cars up to month $n$ is the total up to month $n-1$ plus the production in month $n$:
     $$c_n = c_{n-1} + n \quad \text{for } n \ge 1, \quad \text{with } c_0 = 0$$

2. **(ii) Cars produced in the first year (12 months):**
   * Using the recurrence relation starting from $c_0 = 0$:
     * $c_1 = 0 + 1 = 1$
     * $c_2 = 1 + 2 = 3$
     * $c_3 = 3 + 3 = 6$
     * $c_4 = 6 + 4 = 10$
     * $c_5 = 10 + 5 = 15$
     * $c_6 = 15 + 6 = 21$
     * $c_7 = 21 + 7 = 28$
     * $c_8 = 28 + 8 = 36$
     * $c_9 = 36 + 9 = 45$
     * $c_{10} = 45 + 10 = 55$
     * $c_{11} = 55 + 11 = 66$
     * $c_{12} = 66 + 12 = \mathbf{78 \text{ cars}}$

3. **(iii) Explicit Formula:**
   * Summing the recurrence relation:
     $$c_n - c_0 = \sum_{i=1}^n (c_i - c_{i-1}) = \sum_{i=1}^n i$$
   * Since $c_0 = 0$:
     $$c_n = \frac{n(n+1)}{2}$$

### Question 5(a)
**Question:**
Show that the following argument is valid:
"If Mohan is a lawyer then he is ambitious. If Mohan is an early riser, then he does not like rice. If Mohan is ambitious then he is early riser. Then if Mohan is a lawyer, then he does not like rice."

---

#### Solution:
Let's define the propositional variables:
* $L$: "Mohan is a lawyer."
* $A$: "Mohan is ambitious."
* $E$: "Mohan is an early riser."
* $R$: "Mohan likes rice."

1. **Translate the premises and the conclusion:**
   * **Premise 1:** $L \rightarrow A$
   * **Premise 2:** $E \rightarrow \neg R$
   * **Premise 3:** $A \rightarrow E$
   * **Conclusion:** $L \rightarrow \neg R$

2. **Proof:**

| Step | Proposition | Justification |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 1. | $L \rightarrow A$ | Premise 1 |
| 2. | $A \rightarrow E$ | Premise 3 |
| 3. | $L \rightarrow E$ | Hypothetical Syllogism from (1) and (2) |
| 4. | $E \rightarrow \neg R$ | Premise 2 |
| 5. | $L \rightarrow \neg R$ | Hypothetical Syllogism from (3) and (4) |

Since the conclusion $L \rightarrow \neg R$ has been derived using valid rules of inference, the argument is **valid**.
