### Question 1(a)
**Question:**
Among 100 students, 32 study mathematics, 20 study physics, 45 study biology, 15 study mathematics and biology, 7 study mathematics and physics, 10 study physics and biology and 30 do not study any of the three subjects.
(i) Find the number of students studying all three subjects.
(ii) Find the number of students studying exactly one of the 3 subjects.

---

#### Solution:
Let $M, P,$ and $B$ be the sets of students studying Mathematics, Physics, and Biology, respectively. Let the universal set $U$ be the set of all students. We are given:
* $|U| = 100$
* Students studying none of the three subjects $= 30 \implies |M \cup P \cup B| = 100 - 30 = 70$
* $|M| = 32, \quad |P| = 20, \quad |B| = 45$
* $|M \cap B| = 15, \quad |M \cap P| = 7, \quad |P \cap B| = 10$

1. **(i) Find the number of students studying all three subjects ($|M \cap P \cap B|$):**
   * By the **Principle of Inclusion-Exclusion**:
     $$|M \cup P \cup B| = |M| + |P| + |B| - |M \cap P| - |M \cap B| - |P \cap B| + |M \cap P \cap B|$$
     $$70 = 32 + 20 + 45 - 7 - 15 - 10 + |M \cap P \cap B|$$
     $$70 = 97 - 32 + |M \cap P \cap B|$$
     $$70 = 65 + |M \cap P \cap B| \implies |M \cap P \cap B| = 70 - 65 = \mathbf{5 \text{ students}}$$

2. **(ii) Find the number of students studying exactly one of the 3 subjects:**
   * Let's calculate the disjoint regions of the Venn diagram:
     * **Only Mathematics:** $|M| - |M \cap P| - |M \cap B| + |M \cap P \cap B| = 32 - 7 - 15 + 5 = 15$
     * **Only Physics:** $|P| - |M \cap P| - |P \cap B| + |M \cap P \cap B| = 20 - 7 - 10 + 5 = 8$
     * **Only Biology:** $|B| - |M \cap B| - |P \cap B| + |M \cap P \cap B| = 45 - 15 - 10 + 5 = 25$
   * Total studying exactly one subject:
     $$\text{Exactly One} = 15 + 8 + 25 = \mathbf{48 \text{ students}}$$

### Question 1(b)
**Question:**
(i) Write the Contrapositive, Converse and Inverse of the following statement:
"The home team wins whenever it is raining"
(ii) Show that $\neg(p \leftrightarrow q) \equiv (p \lor q) \land \neg(p \land q)$

---

#### Solution:

1. **(i) Conditional Variations:**
   * Original statement $p \rightarrow q$: "If it is raining ($p$), then the home team wins ($q$)."
   * **Contrapositive ($\neg q \rightarrow \neg p$):** "If the home team does not win, then it is not raining."
   * **Converse ($q \rightarrow p$):** "If the home team wins, then it is raining."
   * **Inverse ($\neg p \rightarrow \neg q$):** "If it is not raining, then the home team does not win."

2. **(ii) Show Equivalence $\neg(p \leftrightarrow q) \equiv (p \lor q) \land \neg(p \land q)$:**
   We construct a truth table to prove this equivalence:

| $p$ | $q$ | $p \leftrightarrow q$ | $\neg(p \leftrightarrow q)$ | $p \lor q$ | $p \land q$ | $\neg(p \land q)$ | $(p \lor q) \land \neg(p \land q)$ |
| :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: |
| **T** | **T** | T | **F** | T | T | F | **F** |
| **T** | **F** | F | **T** | T | F | T | **T** |
| **F** | **T** | F | **T** | T | F | T | **T** |
| **F** | **F** | T | **F** | F | F | T | **F** |

Since the columns for $\neg(p \leftrightarrow q)$ and $(p \lor q) \land \neg(p \land q)$ match exactly under all truth assignments, the two statements are logically equivalent.

*(Algebraic Proof alternative:)*
$$\neg(p \leftrightarrow q) \equiv \neg((p \rightarrow q) \land (q \rightarrow p)) \equiv \neg(p \rightarrow q) \lor \neg(q \rightarrow p)$$
$$\equiv (p \land \neg q) \lor (q \land \neg p) \quad \text{(exclusive or definition)}$$
$$(p \lor q) \land \neg(p \land q) \equiv (p \lor q) \land (\neg p \lor \neg q) \equiv (p \land \neg p) \lor (p \land \neg q) \lor (q \land \neg p) \lor (q \land \neg q)$$
$$\equiv \mathbf{F} \lor (p \land \neg q) \lor (q \land \neg p) \lor \mathbf{F} \equiv (p \land \neg q) \lor (q \land \neg p)$$
Both expand to the same normal form, confirming the equivalence.

     * **Hamilton Circuit:** **No**. The vertex $f$ has degree 1, which prevents a circuit.

### Question 1(d)
**Question:**
Define Bipartite Graph and Complete Bipartite Graph. Check whether the graph $C_6$ is Bipartite or not? Justify your answer.

---

#### Solution:

1. **Definitions:**
   * **Bipartite Graph:** A simple graph $G = (V, E)$ is bipartite if its vertex set $V$ can be partitioned into two disjoint non-empty sets $V_1$ and $V_2$ such that every edge in $E$ connects a vertex in $V_1$ to a vertex in $V_2$ (no edges exist between vertices in the same set).
   * **Complete Bipartite Graph ($K_{m,n}$):** A bipartite graph where every vertex in partition $V_1$ (size $m$) is connected by an edge to every vertex in partition $V_2$ (size $n$).

2. **Bipartiteness of $C_6$:**
   * **Answer:** **Yes, $C_6$ is bipartite**.
   * **Justification:** Let the vertices of the 6-cycle $C_6$ be labeled sequentially as $v_1, v_2, v_3, v_4, v_5, v_6$.
     * We can partition the vertices into:
       $$V_1 = \{v_1, v_3, v_5\} \quad \text{and} \quad V_2 = \{v_2, v_4, v_6\}$$
     * The edges in $C_6$ are $(v_1,v_2), (v_2,v_3), (v_3,v_4), (v_4,v_5), (v_5,v_6),$ and $(v_6,v_1)$.
     * Notice that every edge connects an odd-indexed vertex (in $V_1$) to an even-indexed vertex (in $V_2$). No edge connects two odd-indexed or two even-indexed vertices.
     * Alternatively, a theorem states that a graph is bipartite if and only if it contains no cycles of odd length. Since $C_6$ has length 6 (which is even), it has no odd cycles and is therefore bipartite.

### Question 1(e)
**Question:**
Determine the numeric function for the following generating function:
$$A(z) = \frac{1}{5 - 6z + z^2}$$

---

#### Solution:

1. **Factor the denominator:**
   $$5 - 6z + z^2 = (5-z)(1-z)$$
   So, we can write $A(z)$ as:
   $$A(z) = \frac{1}{(1-z)(5-z)}$$

2. **Use partial fraction decomposition:**
   $$\frac{1}{(1-z)(5-z)} = \frac{X}{1-z} + \frac{Y}{5-z}$$
   Multiply by the denominator:
   $$1 = X(5-z) + Y(1-z)$$
   * Set $z = 1$:
     $$1 = X(4) \implies X = \frac{1}{4}$$
   * Set $z = 5$:
     $$1 = Y(-4) \implies Y = -\frac{1}{4}$$
   So:
   $$A(z) = \frac{1}{4} \left( \frac{1}{1-z} \right) - \frac{1}{4} \left( \frac{1}{5-z} \right) = \frac{1}{4} \left( \frac{1}{1-z} \right) - \frac{1}{20} \left( \frac{1}{1 - z/5} \right)$$

3. **Expand using geometric series:**
   * $\frac{1}{1-z} = \sum_{r=0}^\infty z^r$
   * $\frac{1}{1 - z/5} = \sum_{r=0}^\infty \left(\frac{1}{5}\right)^r z^r$
   Substitute back:
   $$A(z) = \sum_{r=0}^\infty \left[ \frac{1}{4} (1)^r - \frac{1}{20} \left(\frac{1}{5}\right)^r \right] z^r = \sum_{r=0}^\infty \left[ \frac{1}{4} - \frac{1}{4 \cdot 5^{r+1}} \right] z^r$$

4. **Identify the numeric function $a_r$:**
   $$a_r = \frac{1}{4} - \frac{1}{4 \cdot 5^{r+1}} \quad \text{for } r \ge 0$$

*(For completeness, if the print was $5 - 6z - z^2$:)*
$$a_r = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{14}}\left[ \left(\frac{-3+\sqrt{14}}{5}\right)^{r+1} - \left(\frac{-3-\sqrt{14}}{5}\right)^{r+1} \right]$$

### Question 1(f)
**Question:**
Show that the following system is inconsistent:
$$P \rightarrow Q, \quad P \rightarrow R, \quad Q \rightarrow \neg R, \quad P$$

---

#### Solution:
A system of premises is inconsistent if a contradiction (a statement of the form $X \land \neg X$) can be derived from them:

1. **Derive propositions using rules of inference:**
   * Step 1: $P$ (Premise 4)
   * Step 2: $P \rightarrow Q$ (Premise 1)
   * Step 3: $Q$ (Modus Ponens from Step 1 and 2)
   * Step 4: $P \rightarrow R$ (Premise 2)
   * Step 5: $R$ (Modus Ponens from Step 1 and 4)
   * Step 6: $Q \rightarrow \neg R$ (Premise 3)
   * Step 7: $\neg R$ (Modus Ponens from Step 3 and 6)

2. **Show contradiction:**
   * Combine Step 5 ($R$) and Step 7 ($\neg R$):
     $$R \land \neg R \equiv \mathbf{F} \quad \text{(Contradiction)}$$

Since the set of premises leads to a logical contradiction, the system of premises is **inconsistent**.

### Question 1(g)
**Question:**
Use Master method to find the asymptotic bounds for the following recurrence:
$$T(n) = 2T(n/2) + n^2$$

---

#### Solution:
The Master Theorem applies to recurrences of the form $T(n) = a T(n/b) + f(n)$.

1. **Identify parameters:**
   * $a = 2, \quad b = 2$
   * $f(n) = n^2$

2. **Compute $n^{\log_b a}$:**
   * $n^{\log_2 2} = n^1 = n$

3. **Compare $f(n)$ and $n^{\log_b a}$:**
   * We compare $f(n) = n^2$ with $n$:
   * Since $f(n) = n^2 = \Omega(n^{1 + \epsilon})$ for $\epsilon = 1 > 0$, we check **Case 3** of the Master Theorem.

4. **Verify the regularity condition for Case 3:**
   * We must show that $a f(n/b) \le c f(n)$ for some constant $c < 1$ and all sufficiently large $n$:
     $$a f(n/b) = 2 \left(\frac{n}{2}\right)^2 = 2 \frac{n^2}{4} = \frac{1}{2} n^2$$
   * This inequality $\frac{1}{2} n^2 \le c n^2$ holds for $c = \frac{1}{2} < 1$.

5. **Apply Master Theorem:**
   * Since Case 3 conditions are satisfied, we conclude:
     $$T(n) = \Theta(f(n)) = \mathbf{\Theta(n^2)}$$

### Question 2(a)
**Question:**
Show that:
$$1\cdot 2^1 + 2\cdot 2^2 + 3\cdot 2^3 + \dots + n\cdot 2^n = (n-1)2^{n+1} + 2$$
by induction.

---

#### Solution:
Let $P(n)$ be the statement: $\sum_{i=1}^n i \cdot 2^i = (n-1)2^{n+1} + 2$.

1. **Basis Step ($n = 1$):**
   * LHS of $P(1)$: $1 \cdot 2^1 = 2$.
   * RHS of $P(1)$: $(1-1)2^{1+1} + 2 = 0 \cdot 4 + 2 = 2$.
   * Since $\text{LHS} = \text{RHS} = 2$, $P(1)$ is true.

2. **Inductive Step:**
   * Assume $P(k)$ is true for some positive integer $k \ge 1$ (**Inductive Hypothesis**):
     $$1\cdot 2^1 + 2\cdot 2^2 + \dots + k\cdot 2^k = (k-1)2^{k+1} + 2$$
   * We must prove $P(k+1)$ is true:
     $$1\cdot 2^1 + \dots + (k+1)2^{k+1} = k \cdot 2^{k+2} + 2$$
   * Write the LHS of $P(k+1)$:
     $$\text{LHS} = \left[ 1\cdot 2^1 + \dots + k\cdot 2^k \right] + (k+1)2^{k+1}$$
   * Substitute the inductive hypothesis:
     $$\text{LHS} = \left( (k-1)2^{k+1} + 2 \right) + (k+1)2^{k+1}$$
   * Regroup terms:
     $$\text{LHS} = 2^{k+1} \left[ (k-1) + (k+1) \right] + 2$$
     $$\text{LHS} = 2^{k+1} [2k] + 2$$
     $$\text{LHS} = k \cdot 2^{k+2} + 2$$
   * Since this matches the RHS of $P(k+1)$, the inductive step holds.

**Conclusion:**
By mathematical induction, the statement is true for all positive integers $n \ge 1$.

### Question 2(b)
**Question:**
Determine whether the function $f$ is a bijection from R to R. Find $f \circ g$ and $g \circ f$ for the functions $f$ and $g$ where $f(x) = 2x^2 + 3$ and $g(x) = x + 1$. Is $f \circ g = g \circ f$?

---

#### Solution:

1. **Is $f(x) = 2x^2 + 3$ a bijection?**
   * **Answer:** **No**.
   * **Justification:** A function is a bijection if it is both one-to-one and onto.
     * **Not one-to-one:** $f(-1) = 2(-1)^2 + 3 = 5$ and $f(1) = 2(1)^2 + 3 = 5$, but $-1 \neq 1$.
     * **Not onto:** The range of $f$ is $[3, \infty)$. Negative real numbers (like $-5$) have no pre-image.

2. **Find $f \circ g(x)$:**
   $$f \circ g(x) = f(g(x)) = f(x+1) = 2(x+1)^2 + 3 = 2(x^2 + 2x + 1) + 3 = \mathbf{2x^2 + 4x + 5}$$

3. **Find $g \circ f(x)$:**
   $$g \circ f(x) = g(f(x)) = g(2x^2 + 3) = (2x^2 + 3) + 1 = \mathbf{2x^2 + 4}$$

4. **Is $f \circ g = g \circ f$?**
   * **Answer:** **No**.
   * **Justification:** $2x^2 + 4x + 5 \neq 2x^2 + 4$ for most values of $x$. For instance, at $x=0$, $f(g(0)) = 5 \neq g(f(0)) = 4$.

*(If the print $f(x)=2x^2+3$ was a typo for $f(x)=2x+3$: $f(x)$ is a bijection, $f(g(x)) = 2x+5$, $g(f(x)) = 2x+4$, and $f \circ g \neq g \circ f$.)*

**Question:**
In how many ways can four students be selected out of twelve students, if :
(i) Two particular students are not included at all ?
(ii) Two particular students are included.

---

#### Solution:

1. **(i) Two particular students are not included at all:**

   * Since 2 specific students must be on the team, their spots are fixed.
   * We must choose the remaining $4 - 2 = 2$ spots from the remaining $12 - 2 = 10$ students:
     $$\text{Ways} = \binom{10}{2} = \frac{10 \times 9}{2 \times 1} = \mathbf{45 \text{ ways}}$$

### Question 4(a)
**Question:**
Define planar graph. Is $K_{3,3}$ planar? Justify your Answer. For any connected planar graph, show that $v - e + r = 2$, where $v, e$ and $r$ are the number of vertices, edges and regions of the graph respectively.

---

#### Solution:

1. **Definition of Planar Graph:**
   A simple graph is planar if it can be drawn in the plane without any edge crossings.

2. **Is $K_{3,3}$ Planar?**
   * **Answer:** **No**.
   * **Justification:** For any connected planar bipartite graph (which contains no cycles of odd length, meaning the shortest cycle length $g \ge 4$), the number of edges must satisfy $E \le 2V - 4$.
     * $K_{3,3}$ has $V = 6$ vertices and $E = 9$ edges.
     * Bipartite limit: $2V - 4 = 2(6) - 4 = 8$.
     * Since $9 \not\le 8$, $K_{3,3}$ cannot be planar.

3. **Proof of Euler's Formula ($v - e + r = 2$):**
   We prove the formula by induction on the number of edges $e$ in a connected planar graph:
   * **Basis Step ($e = 0$):**
     * A connected graph with 0 edges must have exactly 1 vertex ($v = 1$).
     * In any planar embedding of a single vertex, there is only 1 region (the outer infinite region, $r = 1$).
     * So: $v - e + r = 1 - 0 + 1 = 2$. The basis step is true.
   * **Inductive Step:**
     * Assume that Euler's formula holds for all connected planar graphs with $k$ edges. We want to show it holds for a graph $G$ with $k+1$ edges.
     * **Case A: $G$ is a tree.**
       * If $G$ is a tree, it contains no cycles, so $r = 1$.
       * In any tree, $e = v - 1 \implies v = e + 1 = k + 2$.
       * Substitute: $v - e + r = (k+2) - (k+1) + 1 = 2$. Holds!
     * **Case B: $G$ contains a cycle.**
       * Let $e_0$ be an edge that belongs to a cycle in $G$.
       * Since $e_0$ is on a cycle, it lies on the boundary of two different regions.
       * Remove the edge $e_0$ to obtain a subgraph $G' = G - \{e_0\}$.
       * $G'$ is still connected (removing an edge from a cycle does not disconnect the graph).
       * In $G'$: $v' = v$, $e' = e - 1 = k$, and $r' = r - 1$ (the two regions separated by $e_0$ merged into one).
       * Since $G'$ has $k$ edges, by the inductive hypothesis:
         $$v' - e' + r' = 2 \implies v - (e-1) + (r-1) = 2 \implies v - e + r = 2$$
   * By induction, Euler's formula holds for all connected planar graphs.

### Question 4(b)
**Question:**
What do you mean by graph invariant ? Determine whether the graphs G1 and G2 are isomorphic.

*(G1 and G2 are the 7-vertex cycle-like graphs in Page 5)*
* **G1:** A heptagon cycle ($u_1 - u_2 - u_3 - u_4 - u_5 - u_6 - u_7 - u_1$).
* **G2:** A 7-pointed star graph.

---

#### Solution:

1. **Definition of Graph Invariant:**
   A graph invariant is a property of a graph that remains unchanged under any graph isomorphism. Examples include the number of vertices, the number of edges, the degree sequence, bipartiteness, and cycle inventory.

2. **Check Isomorphism of G1 and G2:**
   * **Invariants analysis:**
     * Both graphs have 7 vertices.
     * Both graphs have 7 edges.
     * Both graphs are 2-regular (every vertex has degree 2).
   * **Is G2 a cycle?**
     * Let's trace G2's edges starting at $v_1$:
       $$v_1 - v_4 - v_7 - v_3 - v_6 - v_2 - v_5 - v_1$$
     * Since this path visits all 7 vertices exactly once and returns to the start, G2 is a simple 7-cycle ($C_7$).
     * Since G1 is also a simple 7-cycle ($C_7$), they are isomorphic.
   * **Isomorphism Mapping $f: V(G_1) \to V(G_2)$:**
     * Map the cycle vertices in order:
       $$f(u_1)=v_1, \ f(u_2)=v_4, \ f(u_3)=v_7, \ f(u_4)=v_3, \ f(u_5)=v_6, \ f(u_6)=v_2, \ f(u_7)=v_5$$
     * This bijection preserves all edge adjacencies. Thus, the two graphs are **isomorphic**.

### Question 5(a)
**Question:**
(i) What is the chromatic number of $C_n$ where $n \ge 3$ and $K_{m,n}$?
(ii) Prove that a connected graph is a tree if and only if the number of vertices in the graph is one more than the number of edges.

---

#### Solution:

1. **(i) Chromatic Numbers:**
   * **Cycle Graph $C_n$:**
     $$\chi(C_n) = \begin{cases} 2 & \text{if } n \text{ is even} \\ 3 & \text{if } n \text{ is odd} \end{cases}$$
   * **Complete Bipartite Graph $K_{m,n}$:**
     * Since the graph is bipartite, we can color all vertices in one partition with one color and all vertices in the other partition with a second color.
     * Assuming $m, n \ge 1$ (the graph has at least one edge), we need at least 2 colors.
     * Thus, $\chi(K_{m,n}) = \mathbf{2}$.

2. **(ii) Proof: Connected graph is tree $\iff V = E + 1$:**
   * **Direction 1 ($\Rightarrow$): If $G$ is a tree, then $V = E + 1$.**
     * We prove this by induction on $V$:
       * *Basis ($V=1$):* A tree with 1 vertex has 0 edges. $1 = 0 + 1$. True.
       * *Induction:* Assume true for all trees with $k$ vertices. Let $T$ be a tree with $k+1$ vertices.
       * Since $k+1 \ge 2$, $T$ has at least one leaf $v$ (degree 1). Remove $v$ and its incident edge to get $T'$.
       * $T'$ is a tree with $k$ vertices. By inductive hypothesis, $\text{Edges}(T') = k - 1$.
       * Thus, $\text{Edges}(T) = \text{Edges}(T') + 1 = k - 1 + 1 = k = V - 1$. True!
   * **Direction 2 ($\Leftarrow$): If $G$ is connected and $V = E + 1$, then $G$ is a tree.**
     * We must show $G$ is acyclic.
     * Suppose for contradiction that $G$ contains at least one cycle.
     * We can remove edges from cycles of $G$ one by one, keeping the graph connected, until no cycles remain.
     * The resulting subgraph $G'$ is a connected acyclic graph (a spanning tree of $G$).
     * Since $G'$ is a tree, it has $V' = V$ vertices and $E' = V - 1$ edges.
     * However, since we removed at least one edge from $G$ to break the cycle(s), we must have:
       $$E' < E \implies V - 1 < E \implies V \le E$$
     * But we are given $V = E + 1 \implies V > E$, which is a contradiction.
     * Therefore, $G$ must be acyclic, and since it is connected, it is a tree.

### Question 5(b)
**Question:**
Let $f(n) = n^2 + 4n$ and $g(n) = n^2, n \ge 0$. Show that $f(n) = O(g(n))$.

---

#### Solution:
By definition, $f(n) = O(g(n))$ if there exist positive constants $C$ and $n_0$ such that for all $n \ge n_0$:
$$|f(n)| \le C |g(n)|$$

* Let's analyze $f(n) = n^2 + 4n$ for $n \ge 1$:
  * Since $n \ge 1$, we can multiply both sides of $1 \le n$ by $4n$ to get:
    $$4n \le 4n^2$$
  * Add $n^2$ to both sides:
    $$n^2 + 4n \le n^2 + 4n^2 \implies n^2 + 4n \le 5n^2$$
* Choose the constants:
  * $C = 5$
  * $n_0 = 1$
* Thus, for all $n \ge n_0$:
  $$|n^2 + 4n| \le 5 |n^2| \implies f(n) \le 5 g(n)$$

Therefore, $f(n) = O(g(n))$.

### Question 6(a)
**Question:**
Solve the following recurrence relation:
$$a_r - 7a_{r-1} + 10a_{r-2} = 3^r, \quad \text{given that } a_0 = 0 \text{ and } a_1 = 1$$

---

#### Solution:
This is a second-order linear non-homogeneous recurrence relation:

1. **Solve the homogeneous relation ($a_r - 7a_{r-1} + 10a_{r-2} = 0$):**
   * Characteristic equation:
     $$x^2 - 7x + 10 = 0 \implies (x-2)(x-5) = 0$$
   * Roots: $x_1 = 2, \ x_2 = 5$.
   * Homogeneous solution:
     $$a_r^{(h)} = C_1 (2)^r + C_2 (5)^r$$

2. **Find a particular solution ($a_r - 7a_{r-1} + 10a_{r-2} = 3^r$):**
   * Since the base of the non-homogeneous term is $3$ (which is not a characteristic root), we guess a particular solution of the form:
     $$a_r^{(p)} = K \cdot 3^r$$
   * Substitute into the recurrence:
     $$K \cdot 3^r - 7K \cdot 3^{r-1} + 10K \cdot 3^{r-2} = 3^r$$
   * Divide by $3^{r-2}$:
     $$9K - 21K + 10K = 9 \implies -2K = 9 \implies K = -\frac{9}{2}$$
   * Particular solution:
     $$a_r^{(p)} = -\frac{9}{2} \cdot 3^r$$

3. **General solution:**
   $$a_r = a_r^{(h)} + a_r^{(p)} = C_1 (2)^r + C_2 (5)^r - \frac{9}{2} \cdot 3^r$$

4. **Apply initial conditions $a_0 = 0$ and $a_1 = 1$:**
   * **For $r = 0$:**
     $$C_1 + C_2 - \frac{9}{2} = 0 \implies C_1 + C_2 = \frac{9}{2}$$
   * **For $r = 1$:**
     $$2C_1 + 5C_2 - \frac{27}{2} = 1 \implies 2C_1 + 5C_2 = \frac{29}{2}$$
   * Solve the system:
     * $2(C_1 + C_2) = 9 \implies 2C_1 + 2C_2 = 9$
     * Subtract from the second equation: $3C_2 = \frac{29}{2} - 9 = \frac{11}{2} \implies C_2 = \frac{11}{6}$
     * Then: $C_1 = \frac{9}{2} - \frac{11}{6} = \frac{16}{6} = \frac{8}{3}$

**Answer:**
$$a_r = \frac{8}{3} (2)^r + \frac{11}{6} (5)^r - \frac{9}{2} (3)^r \quad \text{for } r \ge 0$$

### Question 7(a)
**Question:**
Show the validity of the following argument: If Ram gets the job and works hard, then he will be promoted. If Ram gets promotion, then he will be happy. He will not be happy. Therefore, either he will not get the job or he will not work hard.

---

#### Solution:
We define the propositional variables:
* $J$: "Ram gets the job."
* $W$: "Ram works hard."
* $P$: "Ram gets promoted."
* $H$: "Ram is happy."

1. **Translate the premises and conclusion:**
   * **Premise 1:** $(J \land W) \rightarrow P$
   * **Premise 2:** $P \rightarrow H$
   * **Premise 3:** $\neg H$
   * **Conclusion:** $\neg J \lor \neg W$

2. **Formal Proof:**

| Step | Proposition | Justification |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 1. | $\neg H$ | Premise 3 |
| 2. | $P \rightarrow H$ | Premise 2 |
| 3. | $\neg P$ | Modus Tollens from (1) and (2) |
| 4. | $(J \land W) \rightarrow P$ | Premise 1 |
| 5. | $\neg(J \land W)$ | Modus Tollens from (3) and (4) |
| 6. | $\neg J \lor \neg W$ | De Morgan's Law from (5) |

Since the conclusion $\neg J \lor \neg W$ has been derived using valid rules of inference, the argument is **valid**.

- **Concepts:** Proof of argument validity, Modus Tollens, De Morgan's laws.
