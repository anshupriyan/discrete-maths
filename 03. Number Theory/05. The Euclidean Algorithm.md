# The Euclidean Algorithm (Section 4.3.7)

---

Extracting prime factorizations to find the greatest common divisor (GCD) is computationally inefficient because finding the prime factors of large numbers is extremely time-consuming. A much faster method known since antiquity is the **Euclidean algorithm**.

---

### 1. Theoretical Foundation

The Euclidean algorithm relies on reducing the size of the inputs step-by-step through successive division based on the following lemma:

> **Lemma 1:** Let $a = bq + r$, where $a, b, q,$ and $r$ are integers. Then $\text{gcd}(a, b) = \text{gcd}(b, r)$.

#### 📝 Proof:
If we show that the set of common divisors for $a$ and $b$ matches the set of common divisors for $b$ and $r$, their greatest common divisor must be identical.

1. **Forward Direction:** Suppose an integer $d$ divides both $a$ and $b$. It follows from divisibility properties (Theorem 1 of Section 4.1) that $d$ must also divide the expression $a - bq$, which equals $r$. Thus, any common divisor of $a$ and $b$ is a common divisor of $b$ and $r$.
2. **Backward Direction:** Conversely, if $d$ divides both $b$ and $r$, it must divide the expression $bq + r$, which equals $a$. Therefore, any common divisor of $b$ and $r$ is also a common divisor of $a$ and $b$.

Consequently, the common divisors are identical, meaning:
$$\text{gcd}(a, b) = \text{gcd}(b, r) \quad \blacksquare$$

---

### 2. How the Algorithm Works

To find $\text{gcd}(a, b)$ where $a \ge b$, let $r_0 = a$ and $r_1 = b$. We successively apply the division algorithm to generate a sequence of decreasing remainders:
$$\begin{aligned}
r_0 &= r_1q_1 + r_2 \quad (0 \le r_2 < r_1) \\
r_1 &= r_2q_2 + r_3 \quad (0 \le r_3 < r_2) \\
&\ \ \vdots \\
r_{n-2} &= r_{n-1}q_{n-1} + r_n \quad (0 \le r_n < r_{n-1}) \\
r_{n-1} &= r_nq_n + 0
\end{aligned}$$

Because each remainder is strictly smaller than the previous one ($r_1 > r_2 > r_3 \dots \ge 0$), the sequence must eventually hit a remainder of zero. By applying Lemma 1 down the chain, we see that:
$$\text{gcd}(a, b) = \text{gcd}(r_0, r_1) = \text{gcd}(r_1, r_2) = \dots = \text{gcd}(r_{n-1}, r_n) = \text{gcd}(r_n, 0) = r_n$$

Thus, **the greatest common divisor is the last nonzero remainder** in the division sequence.

#### GCD Pseudocode:
```
procedure gcd(a, b: positive integers)
    x := a
    y := b
    while y ≠ 0
        r := x mod y
        x := y
        y := r
    return x
```

### 3. Tracing the Algorithm

#### Example 16: Find $\text{gcd}(414, 662)$ using the Euclidean algorithm.
* **Solution:** Apply the division algorithm repeatedly until the remainder is 0:
  1. $662 = 414 \cdot 1 + \mathbf{248}$
  2. $414 = 248 \cdot 1 + \mathbf{166}$
  3. $248 = 166 \cdot 1 + \mathbf{82}$
  4. $166 = 82 \cdot 2 + \mathbf{2}$
  5. $82 = 2 \cdot 41 + \mathbf{0}$
* The last nonzero remainder is 2. Therefore, $\text{gcd}(414, 662) = 2$.

We can track this sequence in a neat tabular layout:

| Step ($j$) | $r_j$ (Dividend) | $r_{j+1}$ (Divisor) | $q_{j+1}$ (Quotient) | $r_{j+2}$ (Remainder) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **0** | 662 | 414 | 1 | 248 |
| **1** | 414 | 248 | 1 | 166 |
| **2** | 248 | 166 | 1 | 82 |
| **3** | 166 | 82 | 2 | **2** (Last Nonzero Remainder) |
| **4** | 82 | 2 | 41 | 0 |

#### Complexity Analysis:
The computational complexity of the Euclidean algorithm is incredibly efficient: it requires at most $O(\log b)$ divisions to compute the greatest common divisor of $a$ and $b$, compared to the exponential trial division required to find prime factorizations.
