# Prime Numbers and Factorization (Section 4.3.2 - 4.3.3)

---

A prime number is the fundamental multiplicative building block of integers. In modern computer science, primes form the bedrock of public-key cryptography. The security of these systems relies on the practical asymmetry of prime arithmetic: while it is computationally easy to multiply two large prime numbers together, it is extremely time-consuming to factor a massive integer back into its prime components.

---

### 1. Primes and Composites

Every integer greater than 1 has at least two positive divisors: 1 and itself. This observation yields the fundamental definition of prime and composite numbers:

> **Definition 1:** An integer $p > 1$ is called **prime** if its only positive factors are 1 and $p$. A positive integer greater than 1 that is not prime is called **composite**.

* **The Integer 1:** The number 1 is specifically excluded from being prime because it possesses only one positive factor (itself).
* **Composite Characterization:** An integer $n$ is composite if and only if there exists an integer $a$ such that $a \mid n$ and $1 < a < n$. Logically, $n = ab$ where $1 < a, b < n$.

#### Example 1:
The integer 7 is prime because its only positive factors are 1 and 7. The integer 9 is composite because it is divisible by 3.

---

### 2. The Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic

Primes serve as the foundational building blocks for all positive integers, a truth formalized by one of the most vital theorems in number theory:

> **Theorem 1 (The Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic):** Every integer greater than 1 can be written uniquely as a prime or as the product of two or more primes, where the prime factors are written in order of nondecreasing size.

#### Example 2:
The unique prime factorizations for the following integers are:
* $100 = 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 5 \cdot 5 = 2^2 \cdot 5^2$
* $641 = 641$ (641 is itself prime)
* $999 = 3 \cdot 3 \cdot 3 \cdot 37 = 3^3 \cdot 37$
* $1024 = 2^{10}$

---

### 3. Trial Division

To verify if an integer is prime or to map out its unique prime factorization, we can employ a brute-force approach known as **trial division**. A critical shortcut prevents us from having to test every single integer up to $n$:

> **Theorem 2:** If $n$ is a composite integer, then $n$ has a prime divisor less than or equal to $\sqrt{n}$.

#### 📝 Proof:
Because $n$ is composite, it must have a factor $a$ such that $1 < a < n$. By definition, this means $n = ab$, where $b$ is an integer greater than 1. 

We can show by contradiction that $a \le \sqrt{n}$ or $b \le \sqrt{n}$. If both $a > \sqrt{n}$ and $b > \sqrt{n}$, then multiplying them together would yield:
$$ab > \sqrt{n} \cdot \sqrt{n} = n$$
which contradicts our premise that $n = ab$. Thus, $a \le \sqrt{n}$ or $b \le \sqrt{n}$.

Since both $a$ and $b$ are divisors of $n$, $n$ must possess a positive divisor not exceeding $\sqrt{n}$. According to the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic, this divisor must either be a prime itself or contain a prime divisor smaller than it. In both paths, $n$ must have a prime divisor less than or equal to $\sqrt{n}$. $\blacksquare$

Consequently, an integer is confirmed to be prime if it is completely indivisible by any prime number up to and including its own square root ($\le \sqrt{n}$).

#### Example 3: Show that 101 is a prime number.
* **Solution:** First, calculate the boundary: $\sqrt{101} \approx 10.05$. The only prime numbers that do not exceed this boundary are 2, 3, 5, and 7. We perform trial division with each:
  * 101 is not divisible by 2 (it is odd).
  * 101 is not divisible by 3 ($\frac{101}{3}$ is not an integer).
  * 101 is not divisible by 5 (it does not end in 0 or 5).
  * 101 is not divisible by 7 ($\frac{101}{7}$ is not an integer).
* Because 101 is not divisible by any prime up to $\sqrt{101}$, it is prime.

---

### 4. Factoring an Integer using Trial Division

To extract the full prime factorization of an integer $n$, you divide it sequentially by successive primes starting at 2. If a prime factor $p$ divides $n$, you record $p$ and continue the same trial division process on the reduced quotient $\frac{n}{p}$. This loop terminates when the remaining quotient reduces completely to a prime number.

#### Example 4: Find the prime factorization of 7007.
* **Solution:** Begin testing divisions by successive primes:
  * 2, 3, and 5 do not divide 7007.
  * 7 divides 7007, since $\frac{7007}{7} = 1001$. Record **7**.
  * Check the remaining quotient, 1001, starting at the current prime 7: 7 divides 1001, since $\frac{1001}{7} = 143$. Record another **7**.
  * Check the remaining quotient, 143, starting at 7: 7 does not divide 143, so move to the next prime, 11. 11 divides 143, since $\frac{143}{11} = 13$. Record **11**.
  * The final remaining quotient is 13, which is a prime number. Record the final **13**.
* Combining these factors gives the unique nondecreasing prime factorization:
  $$7007 = 7 \cdot 7 \cdot 11 \cdot 13 = 7^2 \cdot 11 \cdot 13$$

### 🧠 Check for Understanding

1. Use trial division to test whether 143 is prime.
2. Find the unique prime factorization of 858.

---

### 💡 Solutions & Explanation

> [!NOTE]
> Here are the step-by-step solutions:
> 
> 1. **Testing 143:**
>    * $\sqrt{143} \approx 11.95$.
>    * Primes to check: 2, 3, 5, 7, 11.
>    * 2, 3, 5, 7 do not divide 143.
>    * Testing 11: $\frac{143}{11} = 13$.
>    * Since 11 divides 143, **143 is composite** ($11 \cdot 13$).
> 
> 2. **Prime Factorization of 858:**
>    * 858 is even: $\frac{858}{2} = 429$. Record **2**.
>    * 429 is odd. Sum of digits: $4+2+9=15$ (divisible by 3): $\frac{429}{3} = 143$. Record **3**.
>    * As shown above, $143 = 11 \cdot 13$. Record **11** and **13**.
>    * All remaining factors are prime, so:
>      $$858 = 2 \cdot 3 \cdot 11 \cdot 13$$
