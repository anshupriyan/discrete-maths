# Divisibility and the Division Algorithm (Section 4.1.2 - 4.1.3)

---

The foundations of modular arithmetic rest entirely on the concept of **divisibility**. When you divide an integer by a positive integer, you obtain a **quotient** and a **remainder**. Focusing primarily on these remainders leads to modular arithmetic, which plays a major role across mathematics and computer science applications.

---

### 1. Divisibility

When an integer is divided by a nonzero integer, the resulting quotient may or may not be an integer. For instance, $\frac{12}{3} = 4$ is an integer, but $\frac{11}{4} = 2.75$ is not. This behavior motivates our formal definition of divisibility:

> **Definition 1:** If $a$ and $b$ are integers with $a \neq 0$, we say that **$a$ divides $b$** if there is an integer $c$ such that $b = ac$ (or equivalently, if $\frac{b}{a}$ is an integer).
> 
> * **Terminology:** When $a$ divides $b$, we call $a$ a **factor** or **divisor** of $b$, and we say $b$ is a **multiple** of $a$.
> * **Notation:** 
>   * $a \mid b$ denotes that $a$ divides $b$.
>   * $a \nmid b$ denotes that $a$ does not divide $b$.

Using quantifiers over the universe of discourse of integers, this relation can be written as:
$$\exists c \, (ac = b)$$

#### Example 1:
Determine whether $3 \mid 7$ and whether $3 \mid 12$.
* **Solution:** $3 \nmid 7$ because $\frac{7}{3}$ is not an integer. However, $3 \mid 12$ because $\frac{12}{3} = 4$.

#### Example 2:
Let $n$ and $d$ be positive integers. How many positive integers not exceeding $n$ are divisible by $d$?
* **Solution:** Positive integers divisible by $d$ take the form $dk$, where $k$ is a positive integer. To not exceed $n$, we look for integers $k$ such that $0 < dk \le n$, which simplifies to $0 < k \le \frac{n}{d}$. Therefore, there are exactly $\lfloor n/d \rfloor$ positive integers not exceeding $n$ that are divisible by $d$.

---

### 2. Mathematical Properties of Divisibility

The relationship of divisibility satisfies several highly predictable rules:

> **Theorem 1:** Let $a, b,$ and $c$ be integers, where $a \neq 0$. Then:
> * **(i)** If $a \mid b$ and $a \mid c$, then $a \mid (b + c)$.
> * **(ii)** If $a \mid b$, then $a \mid bc$ for all integers $c$.
> * **(iii)** If $a \mid b$ and $b \mid c$, then $a \mid c$.

#### 📝 Direct Proof of Part (i):
Assume $a \mid b$ and $a \mid c$. By definition, there exist integers $s$ and $t$ such that:
$$b = as \quad \text{and} \quad c = at$$
Adding these equations together gives:
$$b + c = as + at = a(s + t)$$
Because $s + t$ is an integer (by closure of integers under addition), $a$ must divide $b + c$ by definition. $\blacksquare$

From Theorem 1, a highly useful consequence arises:

> **Corollary 1:** If $a, b,$ and $c$ are integers, where $a \neq 0$, such that $a \mid b$ and $a \mid c$, then $a \mid (mb + nc)$ whenever $m$ and $n$ are integers.

#### 📝 Proof:
By part (ii) of Theorem 1, if $a \mid b \implies a \mid mb$, and if $a \mid c \implies a \mid nc$. Applying part (i) of Theorem 1 to these two terms reveals that $a \mid (mb + nc)$. $\blacksquare$

---

### 3. The Division Algorithm

Despite its historical name, the "Division Algorithm" is actually a mathematical theorem asserting the existence of a unique quotient and remainder when dividing two integers.

> **Theorem 2 (The Division Algorithm):** Let $a$ be an integer and $d$ a positive integer. Then there are unique integers $q$ and $r$, with $0 \le r < d$, such that:
> $$a = dq + r$$

* **Definitions of Components:**
  * $d$ is the **divisor**.
  * $a$ is the **dividend**.
  * $q$ is the **quotient**.
  * $r$ is the **remainder**.
* **Functional Notation:**
  * $q = a \text{ div } d = \lfloor a/d \rfloor$
  * $r = a \text{ mod } d = a - d \lfloor a/d \rfloor$

#### Example 3:
What are the quotient and remainder when $101$ is divided by $11$?
* **Solution:** We can write $101 = 11 \cdot 9 + 2$. Hence, the quotient $q = 101 \text{ div } 11 = 9$, and the remainder $r = 101 \text{ mod } 11 = 2$.

#### Example 4:
What are the quotient and remainder when $-11$ is divided by $3$?
* **Solution:** We must find a remainder that satisfies $0 \le r < 3$. Writing $-11 = 3(-3) - 2$ is invalid because the remainder cannot be negative. Instead, we use $-11 = 3(-4) + 1$. Thus, the quotient $q = -11 \text{ div } 3 = -4$, and the remainder $r = -11 \text{ mod } 3 = 1$.

> [!WARNING]
> **Note on Programming Languages:** Be cautious when using modulo operators like `mod` or `%` in programming languages (C, C++, Java, Python, etc.) with a negative dividend ($a < 0$). Some environments evaluate operators strictly using truncation towards zero ($a - m \lceil a/m \rceil$) instead of the floor function calculation mandated by mathematical number theory ($a - m \lfloor a/m \rfloor$).

### 🧠 Check for Understanding

1. Find the quotient and remainder when $-44$ is divided by $8$.
2. Prove that if $a \mid b$ and $b \mid c$, then $a \mid c$ (Theorem 1, part iii).

---

### 💡 Solutions & Explanation

> [!NOTE]
> Here are the step-by-step solutions:
> 
> 1. **$-44$ divided by $8$:**
>    * We must find $q$ and $r$ such that $-44 = 8q + r$ with $0 \le r < 8$.
>    * We choose $q = -6$, since $8 \cdot (-6) = -48 \le -44$.
>    * Then $r = -44 - (-48) = 4$.
>    * Since $0 \le 4 < 8$, we have:
>      * $-44 \text{ div } 8 = -6$
>      * $-44 \bmod 8 = 4$
> 
> 2. **Proof of Transitivity ($a \mid b \land b \mid c \implies a \mid c$):**
>    * Since $a \mid b$, there exists an integer $s$ such that $b = as$.
>    * Since $b \mid c$, there exists an integer $t$ such that $c = bt$.
>    * Substitute the expression for $b$ into the equation for $c$:
>      $$c = (as)t = a(st)$$
>    * Since $s$ and $t$ are integers, their product $st$ is also an integer.
>    * By the definition of divisibility, this implies $a \mid c$. $\blacksquare$
