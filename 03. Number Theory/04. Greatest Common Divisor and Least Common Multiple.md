# Greatest Common Divisor and Least Common Multiple (Section 4.3.6)

---

Determining the common divisors and multiples of integers is a fundamental operation in number theory, fractions arithmetic, and computer algorithms.

---

### 1. Greatest Common Divisor (GCD)

The greatest common divisor of two integers is the largest integer that divides both numbers simultaneously.

> **Definition 2:** Let $a$ and $b$ be integers, where $a$ and $b$ are not both zero. The largest integer $d$ such that $d \mid a$ and $d \mid b$ is called the **greatest common divisor** of $a$ and $b$, denoted by $\text{gcd}(a, b)$.

#### Example 10:
What is the greatest common divisor of 24 and 36?
* **Solution:** The positive common divisors of 24 and 36 are 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, and 12. The largest of these is 12, so $\text{gcd}(24, 36) = 12$.

#### Example 11:
What is the greatest common divisor of 17 and 22?
* **Solution:** The only positive integer that divides both 17 and 22 is 1. Thus, $\text{gcd}(17, 22) = 1$.

---

### 2. Relatively Prime & Pairwise Relatively Prime

This specific situation where the greatest common divisor is 1 arises frequently in computer arithmetic and cryptography (such as selecting encryption exponents).

> **Definition 3:** The integers $a$ and $b$ are **relatively prime** if their greatest common divisor is 1 ($\text{gcd}(a,b) = 1$).

From Example 11, the numbers 17 and 22 are relatively prime. We can extend this concept to sets of more than two integers:

> **Definition 4:** The integers $a_1, a_2, \dots, a_n$ are **pairwise relatively prime** if $\text{gcd}(a_i, a_j) = 1$ whenever $1 \le i < j \le n$.

#### Example 13:
* The integers 10, 17, and 21 are **pairwise relatively prime** because:
  * $\text{gcd}(10, 17) = 1$
  * $\text{gcd}(10, 21) = 1$
  * $\text{gcd}(17, 21) = 1$
* The integers 10, 19, and 24 are **not** pairwise relatively prime because:
  * $\text{gcd}(10, 24) = 2 > 1$.

---

### 3. Finding GCD via Prime Factorizations

We can compute the GCD of two positive integers by examining their prime factorizations. Suppose $a$ and $b$ are expressed using a shared pool of prime numbers (using zero as an exponent if a prime is missing from a factorization):
$$a = p_1^{a_1}p_2^{a_2}\dots p_n^{a_n}, \quad b = p_1^{b_1}p_2^{b_2}\dots p_n^{b_n}$$
The greatest common divisor is constructed by taking the **minimum exponent** for each prime factor:
$$\text{gcd}(a, b) = p_1^{\min(a_1, b_1)}p_2^{\min(a_2, b_2)}\dots p_n^{\min(a_n, b_n)}$$

#### Example 14:
Find $\text{gcd}(120, 500)$ using their prime factorizations.
* **Solution:** The factorizations are:
  * $120 = 2^3 \cdot 3^1 \cdot 5^1$
  * $500 = 2^2 \cdot 3^0 \cdot 5^3$
* Aligning their exponents:
  $$\text{gcd}(120, 500) = 2^{\min(3,2)} \cdot 3^{\min(1,0)} \cdot 5^{\min(1,3)} = 2^2 \cdot 3^0 \cdot 5^1 = 4 \cdot 1 \cdot 5 = 20$$

---

### 4. Least Common Multiple (LCM)

The counterpart to the greatest common divisor is the least common multiple.

> **Definition 5:** The **least common multiple** of the positive integers $a$ and $b$ is the smallest positive integer that is divisible by both $a$ and $b$, denoted by $\text{lcm}(a, b)$.

To calculate the LCM from prime factorizations, use the same layout as the GCD method but select the **maximum exponent** for each prime factor instead:
$$\text{lcm}(a, b) = p_1^{\max(a_1, b_1)}p_2^{\max(a_2, b_2)}\dots p_n^{\max(a_n, b_n)}$$

#### Example 15:
What is the least common multiple of $2^33^55^07^2$ and $2^43^35^07^0$?
* **Solution:** Take the maximum exponent for each prime base:
  $$\text{lcm}(2^33^57^2, 2^43^3) = 2^{\max(3,4)} \cdot 3^{\max(5,3)} \cdot 7^{\max(2,0)} = 2^43^57^2$$

---

### 5. The GCD-LCM Relationship

GCD and LCM are closely linked by the following theorem:

> **Theorem 5:** Let $a$ and $b$ be positive integers. Then:
> $$ab = \text{gcd}(a, b) \cdot \text{lcm}(a, b)$$

*This theorem means that once we know the GCD of two numbers, we can find their LCM using basic multiplication and division, without needing their prime factorizations: $\text{lcm}(a,b) = \frac{ab}{\text{gcd}(a,b)}$.*

---

### 🧠 Check for Understanding

1. Find the GCD and LCM of 48 and 72 using their prime factorizations.
2. Verify Theorem 5 for $a=48$ and $b=72$.
3. Are the numbers 15, 21, and 28 pairwise relatively prime?

---

### 💡 Solutions & Explanation

> [!NOTE]
> Here are the step-by-step solutions:
> 
> 1. **GCD and LCM of 48 and 72:**
>    * Prime factorizations:
>      * $48 = 2^4 \cdot 3^1$
>      * $72 = 2^3 \cdot 3^2$
>    * **$\text{gcd}(48, 72)$:**
>      * $2^{\min(4,3)} \cdot 3^{\min(1,2)} = 2^3 \cdot 3^1 = 8 \cdot 3 = 24$.
>    * **$\text{lcm}(48, 72)$:**
>      * $2^{\max(4,3)} \cdot 3^{\max(1,2)} = 2^4 \cdot 3^2 = 16 \cdot 9 = 144$.
> 
> 2. **Verifying Theorem 5:**
>    * $a \cdot b = 48 \cdot 72 = 3456$.
>    * $\text{gcd}(a, b) \cdot \text{lcm}(a, b) = 24 \cdot 144 = 3456$.
>    * Since $3456 = 3456$, the theorem holds.
> 
> 3. **Pairwise relatively prime check for $\{15, 21, 28\}$:**
>    * $\text{gcd}(15, 21) = 3 \neq 1$.
>    * Since at least one pair has a GCD greater than 1, these numbers are **not** pairwise relatively prime. (Note that $\text{gcd}(15, 28) = 1$ and $\text{gcd}(21, 28) = 7 \neq 1$).
