# The Infinitude of Primes

---

Since prime numbers are the building blocks of integers, a natural question is: *Are there infinitely many primes, or do they run out at some point?* This note explores the elegant proof that primes are infinite, and how they are distributed.

---

### 1. Euclid's Proof of the Infinitude of Primes

The Greek mathematician Euclid first proved that there are infinitely many primes around 300 BCE. His proof is one of the most famous examples of a **proof by contradiction** (indirect proof) in mathematics.

> **Theorem 1:** There are infinitely many prime numbers.

#### 📝 Proof:
Suppose, for the sake of contradiction, that there are only finitely many primes.
We can list them all:
$$p_1, p_2, \dots, p_n$$
where $n$ is the total number of primes.

Now, let us construct a new integer $Q$ by multiplying all these primes together and adding 1:
$$Q = (p_1 \cdot p_2 \cdots p_n) + 1$$

According to the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic, $Q$ must be either a prime itself or a composite number that can be written as a product of prime factors.

* **Case 1: $Q$ is prime.**
  * Since $Q$ is larger than any prime $p_i$ in our finite list ($Q > p_i$ for all $i$), we have found a prime number that is not in our list. This is a contradiction.

* **Case 2: $Q$ is composite.**
  * If $Q$ is composite, it must have a prime factor $q$.
  * Could $q$ be one of the primes in our list (say, $p_i$)?
  * If $q = p_i$, then $q$ divides the product $(p_1 \cdot p_2 \cdots p_n)$.
  * Since $q$ also divides $Q$, $q$ must divide the difference:
    $$Q - (p_1 \cdot p_2 \cdots p_n) = 1$$
  * Thus, $q \mid 1$.
  * However, the only integer divisors of 1 are $\pm 1$. Since $q$ is a prime number, $q > 1$. Therefore, $q$ cannot divide 1, which is a contradiction.
  * Thus, the prime factor $q$ cannot be any prime $p_i$ in our list. We have found a new prime $q$ not in our list.

In both cases, we find a prime number that was not in our supposedly complete list. This contradicts our assumption that there are only finitely many primes. 

Therefore, there must be infinitely many prime numbers. $\blacksquare$

### 2. Mersenne Primes

Primes of a specific algebraic form have historically been easier to test for primality. These are called **Mersenne primes**, named after the French monk Marin Mersenne.

> **Definition 1:** Prime numbers of the form $M_p = 2^p - 1$, where $p$ is a prime number, are called **Mersenne primes**.

#### Examples:
* For $p = 2$: $M_2 = 2^2 - 1 = 3$ (Prime)
* For $p = 3$: $M_3 = 2^3 - 1 = 7$ (Prime)
* For $p = 5$: $M_5 = 2^5 - 1 = 31$ (Prime)
* For $p = 7$: $M_7 = 2^7 - 1 = 127$ (Prime)
* For $p = 11$: $M_{11} = 2^{11} - 1 = 2047 = 23 \cdot 89$ (Composite!)

### 3. The Prime Number Theorem

Primes become less frequent as we look at larger numbers. The **Prime Number Theorem** describes this density.

Let $\pi(x)$ be the prime-counting function, which returns the number of primes less than or equal to $x$.

> **Theorem 2 (Prime Number Theorem):** The ratio of $\pi(x)$ to $x/\ln(x)$ approaches 1 as $x$ approaches infinity.
> $$\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\pi(x)}{x/\ln(x)} = 1$$

This means that for a very large integer $x$:
* The number of primes up to $x$ is approximately $\frac{x}{\ln(x)}$.
* The probability that a randomly chosen integer near $x$ is prime is roughly $\frac{1}{\ln(x)}$.
