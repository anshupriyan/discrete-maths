# The Pigeonhole Principle (Section 6.2)

---

The **Pigeonhole Principle** (historically known as the *Dirichlet drawer principle*) is a deceptively simple yet incredibly powerful counting technique used in combinatorics.

Imagine a flock of 20 pigeons flying into a set of 19 pigeonholes to roost. Because there are more pigeons than holes, it is a mathematical certainty that **at least one pigeonhole must contain two or more pigeons**. If every hole contained at most one pigeon, you could only accommodate 19 pigeons in total.

---

### 1. The Basic Pigeonhole Principle

> **Theorem 1 (The Pigeonhole Principle):** If $k$ is a positive integer and $k + 1$ or more objects are placed into $k$ boxes, then there is at least one box containing two or more of the objects.

#### 📝 Proof (By Contraposition):
We prove the principle using a proof by contraposition:
1. **Assume the negation of the conclusion:** Suppose none of the $k$ boxes contains more than one object.
2. **Calculate the maximum total:** If each box holds at most 1 object, then the total number of objects across all $k$ boxes would be at most $k$.
3. **Reach a contradiction:** This contradicts our initial premise that we started with at least $k + 1$ objects. Therefore, at least one box must contain two or more objects. $\blacksquare$

#### A Useful Corollary for Functions:
The principle directly applies to analyzing mappings between finite sets:

> **Corollary 1:** A function $f$ from a set with $k + 1$ or more elements to a set with $k$ elements **is not one-to-one (injective)**.

#### 📝 Proof:
Imagine each element $y$ in the codomain is a "box". We place every element $x$ from the domain into the box corresponding to its output, $f(x) = y$. Since there are more domain elements (objects) than codomain elements (boxes), the Pigeonhole Principle guarantees that at least one box receives two or more domain elements. Thus, there exist $x_1 \neq x_2$ such that $f(x_1) = f(x_2)$, meaning $f$ is not one-to-one. $\blacksquare$

#### Basic Examples:
* **Example 1 (Birthdays):** Among any group of 367 people, there must be at least two who share the same birthday. This is because there are at most 366 possible birthdays in a year (including leap years), acting as our "boxes".
* **Example 2 (Initials):** In any group of 27 English words, at least two words must begin with the same letter, because there are only 26 letters in the English alphabet.
* **Example 3 (Exam Scores):** If a final exam is graded on a scale from 0 to 100 points, how many students must be in a class to guarantee that at least two students receive the same score?
  * *Solution:* There are 101 possible scores (0 through 100). Therefore, you need **102 students** to guarantee a matching score.

### 2. The Generalized Pigeonhole Principle

What happens when the number of objects vastly exceeds the number of boxes? For example, if you have 21 decimal digits distributed into 10 target categories (boxes 0–9), you can guarantee that at least *three* of those digits are identical.

> **Theorem 2 (The Generalized Pigeonhole Principle):** If $N$ objects are placed into $k$ boxes, then there is at least one box containing at least $\lceil N/k \rceil$ objects.
> 
> *(Note: $\lceil \dots \rceil$ represents the **ceiling function**, which rounds any fractional number up to the next closest integer).*

#### 📝 Proof (By Contraposition):
1. Suppose that none of the boxes contains more than $\lceil N/k \rceil - 1$ objects.
2. The total number of objects would be bounded by:
   $$k \cdot \left( \lceil N/k \rceil - 1 \right) < k \cdot \left( \left( \frac{N}{k} + 1 \right) - 1 \right) = N$$
   *(Using the algebraic inequality $\lceil x \rceil < x + 1$)*.
3. This calculation shows the total number of objects would be strictly less than $N$, which contradicts having $N$ objects to begin with. $\blacksquare$

#### Examples:
* **Example 5 (Birth Months):** Among 100 people, what is the minimum number of individuals born in the same month?
  * *Solution:* Here, $N = 100$ objects and $k = 12$ boxes (months).
    $$\lceil 100/12 \rceil = \lceil 8.33 \rceil = 9$$
    Thus, at least 9 people share a birth month.

### 3. Finding the Minimum $N$ (Worst-Case Strategy)

A common problem type flips the principle around, asking: **What is the minimum number of objects ($N$) needed to ensure that at least $r$ objects end up in one of the $k$ boxes?**

According to the generalized principle, we need $\lceil N/k \rceil \ge r$. The smallest integer $N$ satisfying this is:
$$N = k(r - 1) + 1$$

#### The Intuition:
* To avoid putting a target $r$-th object into any box, you perfectly distribute the items so that every single one of the $k$ boxes gets exactly $r - 1$ objects.
* At this point, you have used up exactly $k(r - 1)$ objects.
* The very next object you add ($+ 1$) must go into one of those boxes, forcing it to contain $r$ objects.

#### Example 6 (Class Grades):
What is the minimum number of students required in a discrete mathematics class to be absolutely sure that at least six will receive the same grade, if there are five possible grades (A, B, C, D, and F)?
* **Solution:** We want $r = 6$ identical items, and we have $k = 5$ boxes.
  $$N = 5(6 - 1) + 1 = 25 + 1 = \mathbf{26 \text{ students}}$$

### 4. Advanced Application: Properties of Numbers

The Pigeonhole Principle can prove unexpected mathematical facts that seem like they would require complex number theory.

> **Example 4:** Show that for every positive integer $n$, there exists a multiple of $n$ whose decimal expansion contains only 0s and 1s.

#### 📝 Solution:
1. Consider a list of $n + 1$ specific integers constructed using only the digit 1:
   $$1, 11, 111, 1111, \dots, \underbrace{111\dots1}_{n+1 \text{ digits}}$$
2. When any integer is divided by $n$, there are only $n$ possible remainders ($0, 1, 2, \dots, n-1$).
3. Since we have $n + 1$ individual numbers (our "objects") but only $n$ possible remainders (our "boxes"), the Pigeonhole Principle dictates that **at least two integers in our list must yield the exact same remainder** when divided by $n$.
4. Let's subtract the smaller of those two matching integers from the larger one. Because they shared the same remainder, their difference cancels out that remainder completely, making the resulting number a perfect multiple of $n$.
5. Structurally, subtracting a smaller sequence of 1s from a larger sequence of 1s leaves a number consisting entirely of 1s followed by 0s (e.g., $11111 - 11 = 11100$). This provides a multiple of $n$ made exclusively of 0s and 1s. $\blacksquare$

### 🧠 Check for Understanding

1. Show that in a group of 8 people, at least two must have been born on the same day of the week.
2. What is the minimum number of cards you must draw from a standard 52-card deck to guarantee that you have at least three cards of the same suit?

---

### 💡 Solutions & Explanation

> [!NOTE]
> Here are the step-by-step solutions:
> 
> 1. **Days of the week:**
>    * Pigeons (objects) $N = 8$.
>    * Pigeonholes (boxes) $k = 7$ (days in a week).
>    * Since $8 > 7$, by the Pigeonhole Principle, at least one day must contain $\ge 2$ birthdays.
> 
> 2. **Cards of the same suit:**
>    * We want at least $r = 3$ cards of the same suit.
>    * We have $k = 4$ suits (hearts, diamonds, clubs, spades) acting as boxes.
>    * Using the formula:
>      $$N = k(r - 1) + 1 = 4(3 - 1) + 1 = 8 + 1 = 9 \text{ cards}$$
>    * *Worst-case scenario:* You draw 2 cards of each of the 4 suits (8 cards total). The 9th card you draw must belong to one of the suits, giving you 3 of that suit.
