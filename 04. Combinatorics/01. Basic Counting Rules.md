# Basic Counting Rules (Section 3.2)

---

Combinatorics is the branch of mathematics study focused on counting, arranging, and grouping objects. The ability to count possibilities is vital for analyzing the time and space complexity of computer algorithms, calculating probability, and determining the size of address spaces. All complex counting techniques are built upon a few fundamental, intuitive rules.

---

### 1. The Sum Rule (The OR Principle)

The **Sum Rule** applies when you must choose one element from a set of mutually exclusive (disjoint) alternatives.

> **The Sum Rule:** If a task can be done in one of $n_1$ ways, or in one of $n_2$ ways, where none of the $n_1$ ways is the same as any of the $n_2$ ways, then there are $n_1 + n_2$ ways to do the task.

#### Set-Theoretic Formulation:
If $A$ and $B$ are disjoint finite sets ($A \cap B = \emptyset$), then the cardinality of their union is the sum of their individual cardinalities:
$$|A \cup B| = |A| + |B|$$

#### Generalized Sum Rule:
If tasks $T_1, T_2, \dots, T_m$ are pairwise disjoint and can be performed in $n_1, n_2, \dots, n_m$ ways respectively, then the number of ways to perform one of these tasks is:
$$\sum_{i=1}^{m} n_i = n_1 + n_2 + \dots + n_m$$

#### Example 1:
A student wants to choose a project topic. They can select from 15 topics in database systems or 12 topics in computer networks. No topic belongs to both categories. How many choices does the student have?
* **Solution:** Since the topics are disjoint, the student has:
  $$15 + 12 = 27 \text{ choices}$$

### 2. The Product Rule (The AND Principle)

The **Product Rule** applies when a task consists of a sequence of successive, independent steps or stages.

> **The Product Rule:** If a procedure can be broken down into a sequence of two tasks, where the first task can be performed in $n_1$ ways and the second task can be performed in $n_2$ ways after the first task has been performed, then there are $n_1 \times n_2$ ways to complete the procedure.

#### Set-Theoretic Formulation:
The cardinality of the Cartesian product of two finite sets $A$ and $B$ is the product of their individual cardinalities:
$$|A \times B| = |A| \cdot |B|$$

#### Generalized Product Rule:
If a procedure consists of a sequence of tasks $T_1, T_2, \dots, T_m$ that can be performed in $n_1, n_2, \dots, n_m$ ways respectively, then the number of ways to perform the sequence is:
$$\prod_{i=1}^{m} n_i = n_1 \cdot n_2 \cdots n_m$$

#### Example 2:
A new company has 12 employees. They need to select a president and a treasurer. No person can hold both offices. How many ways can they choose these officers?
* **Solution:** 
  * Step 1: Choose a president. There are $12$ choices.
  * Step 2: Choose a treasurer. Since one employee is president, there are $11$ choices remaining.
  * Total ways: $12 \cdot 11 = 132$ ways.

#### Example 3:
How many different bit strings of length 8 are there?
* **Solution:** A bit string of length 8 is a sequence of 8 independent choices. Each position has 2 possible values (0 or 1).
  * Total strings: $2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 = 2^8 = 256$.

---

### 3. The Subtraction Rule (Inclusion-Exclusion)

When the choices are **not** mutually exclusive, the Sum Rule will double-count the elements that belong to both categories. We must subtract the overlap.

> **The Subtraction Rule:** If a task can be done in either $n_1$ ways or $n_2$ ways, but some of the ways are identical, then the number of ways to do the task is $n_1 + n_2 - n_3$, where $n_3$ is the number of ways common to both.

#### Set-Theoretic Formulation:
For any two finite sets $A$ and $B$:
$$|A \cup B| = |A| + |B| - |A \cap B|$$

#### Example 4:
How many bit strings of length 8 either start with a 1 bit or end with the two bits 00?
* **Solution:**
  * Let $A$ be the set of bit strings starting with 1. Since the remaining 7 bits can be anything, $|A| = 2^7 = 128$.
  * Let $B$ be the set of bit strings ending with 00. Since the first 6 bits can be anything, $|B| = 2^6 = 64$.
  * Let $A \cap B$ be the set of strings starting with 1 and ending with 00. The remaining 5 bits can be anything, so $|A \cap B| = 2^5 = 32$.
  * Using the subtraction rule:
    $$|A \cup B| = 128 + 64 - 32 = 160 \text{ strings}$$

---

### 4. The Division Rule

The **Division Rule** is used when we initially count outcomes by a method that treats equivalent configurations as distinct, resulting in overcounting by a constant factor.

> **The Division Rule:** If a task can be performed in $n$ ways, but for every way there are exactly $d$ ways that are considered equivalent, then the actual number of distinct outcomes is:
> $$\frac{n}{d}$$

#### Set-Theoretic Formulation:
If a finite set $A$ is partitioned into subsets of equal size $d$, then the number of subsets in the partition is:
$$\frac{|A|}{d}$$

#### Example 5:
How many different ways can 4 people sit around a circular table, where two arrangements are considered identical if every person has the same left and right neighbors?
* **Solution:**
  * If the seats were in a straight line, there would be $4 \cdot 3 \cdot 2 \cdot 1 = 24$ ways to seat the people.
  * For any circular seating arrangement, shifting everyone one position to the left results in the same relative neighbors. For 4 people, there are 4 such rotations that are equivalent.
  * Using the division rule, the number of distinct circular seatings is:
    $$\frac{24}{4} = 6 \text{ seatings}$$

### 🧠 Check for Understanding

1. A computer science department has 20 faculty members and 150 graduate students. How many ways are there to select a representative who is either a faculty member or a graduate student?
2. How many ways are there to choose a committee of two people consisting of one faculty member and one graduate student?
3. How many positive integers less than 100 are divisible by either 3 or 5?

---

### 💡 Solutions & Explanation

> [!NOTE]
> Here are the step-by-step calculations:
> 
> 1. **Selecting one representative:**
>    * Mutually exclusive selection: we select faculty OR graduate student.
>    * Apply Sum Rule: $20 + 150 = 170$ choices.
> 
> 2. **Selecting a committee of two (one faculty, one graduate student):**
>    * Sequential tasks: Task 1 (choose faculty) AND Task 2 (choose graduate student).
>    * Apply Product Rule: $20 \cdot 150 = 3000$ committees.
> 
> 3. **Divisible by 3 or 5 (integers $1 \le x < 100$):**
>    * Let $A$ be integers divisible by 3: $|A| = \lfloor 99/3 \rfloor = 33$.
>    * Let $B$ be integers divisible by 5: $|B| = \lfloor 99/5 \rfloor = 19$.
>    * Let $A \cap B$ be integers divisible by both (i.e. divisible by $\text{lcm}(3,5)=15$): $|A \cap B| = \lfloor 99/15 \rfloor = 6$.
>    * Apply Subtraction Rule: $|A \cup B| = 33 + 19 - 6 = 46$ integers.
