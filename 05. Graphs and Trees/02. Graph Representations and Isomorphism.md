# Graph Representations and Isomorphism (Section 10.3)

---

To work with graphs computationally and theoretically, we must represent them systematically. Furthermore, we need to determine when two graphs have the same structure, even if they are drawn differently or their vertices are named differently.

---

### 1. Graph Representations

There are three primary methods for representing graphs: adjacency lists, adjacency matrices, and incidence matrices. The choice of representation depends on whether the graph is sparse or dense.

#### A. Adjacency Lists
An **adjacency list** specifies the vertices adjacent to each vertex in the graph.

* **Undirected Graphs:** For each vertex $v$, the list contains all vertices $u$ that share an edge with $v$.
* **Directed Graphs:** For each vertex $v$, the list contains all terminal vertices of edges starting at $v$ (adjacent *from* $v$).

**Example (Undirected Graph with $V = \{a, b, c, d\}$ and $E = \{\{a, b\}, \{a, c\}, \{b, c\}, \{c, d\}\}$):**
* $a$: $b, c$
* $b$: $a, c$
* $c$: $a, b, d$
* $d$: $c$

#### B. Adjacency Matrices
An **adjacency matrix** is a zero-one matrix (or integer matrix for multigraphs) that represents adjacency between vertices.
Let $G = (V, E)$ be a simple graph with $|V| = n$. Suppose the vertices are ordered as $v_1, v_2, \dots, v_n$. The adjacency matrix $A = [a_{ij}]$ is an $n \times n$ matrix where:
$$a_{ij} = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } \{v_i, v_j\} \in E \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

* **Symmetry:** The adjacency matrix of an undirected graph is always **symmetric** ($a_{ij} = a_{ji}$), meaning it is equal to its transpose ($A = A^T$).
* **Loops:** A loop on vertex $v_i$ is represented by a $1$ at $a_{ii}$. In this textbook, a loop in an undirected graph contributes $1$ to the diagonal entry $a_{ii}$.
* **Multigraphs:** If multiple edges connect $v_i$ and $v_j$, then $a_{ij}$ is equal to the number of edges connecting them (multiplicity).

#### C. Incidence Matrices
An **incidence matrix** represents which vertices are incident with which edges.
Let $G = (V, E)$ be an undirected graph with vertices $v_1, \dots, v_n$ and edges $e_1, \dots, e_m$. The incidence matrix $M = [m_{ij}]$ is an $n \times m$ matrix where:
$$m_{ij} = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if edge } e_j \text{ is incident with } v_i \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

* **Column Sum:** Every column of an incidence matrix for an undirected graph sums to exactly $2$ (since each edge has exactly two endpoints), unless it is a loop, in which case the column has a single $1$ and sums to $1$.

---

### 2. Trade-offs: Sparse vs. Dense Graphs

* **Sparse Graph:** A graph where the number of edges is much smaller than the maximum possible number of edges ($|E| \ll |V|^2$).
* **Dense Graph:** A graph where the number of edges is close to the maximum possible number of edges ($|E| \approx |V|^2$).

| Representation | Space Complexity | Checking Edge $\{u, v\}$ | Finding Neighbors of $u$ | Best Suited For |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Adjacency List** | $O(V + E)$ | $O(\text{deg}(u))$ | $O(\text{deg}(u))$ | Sparse graphs (saves memory) |
| **Adjacency Matrix** | $O(V^2)$ | $O(1)$ | $O(V)$ | Dense graphs, fast edge lookups |
| **Incidence Matrix** | $O(V \cdot E)$ | $O(E)$ | $O(E)$ | Analyzing edge-vertex relationships |

### 3. Graph Isomorphism

Two graphs can look completely different depending on how their vertices are laid out on a page, yet they may share the exact same structural connections.

> **Definition 1:** The simple graphs $G_1 = (V_1, E_1)$ and $G_2 = (V_2, E_2)$ are **isomorphic** if there exists a one-to-one and onto (bijective) function $f: V_1 \to V_2$ with the property that $a$ and $b$ are adjacent in $G_1$ if and only if $f(a)$ and $f(b)$ are adjacent in $G_2$, for all $a, b \in V_1$. Such a function $f$ is called an **isomorphism**.

#### Isomorphic Invariants:
To prove two graphs are *not* isomorphic, we look for **isomorphic invariants**—properties that must be preserved under isomorphism. If $G_1$ and $G_2$ are isomorphic, they must have the same:
1. Number of vertices ($|V_1| = |V_2|$).
2. Number of edges ($|E_1| = |E_2|$).
3. Degree sequence (sorted list of vertex degrees, e.g., $\{3, 3, 2, 2\}$).
4. Subgraphs (e.g., if $G_1$ contains a cycle of length 3 ($C_3$), then $G_2$ must also contain a cycle of length 3).
5. Bipartiteness (if one is bipartite, the other must be).

*Note: Having the same invariants is a necessary condition for isomorphism, but NOT a sufficient one. Two graphs can share all invariants and still not be isomorphic.*

#### Verifying Isomorphism:
To show two graphs are isomorphic:
1. Verify all basic invariants match.
2. Define a candidate bijection $f: V_1 \to V_2$.
3. Show that adjacency is preserved. A reliable way to do this is to write the adjacency matrices $A_1$ and $A_2$ of both graphs under the ordering $v_1, \dots, v_n$ and $f(v_1), \dots, f(v_n)$ respectively. If the matrices are identical, the bijection is a valid isomorphism.

### 🧠 Check for Understanding

1. Let $G_1$ be a graph with vertices $V_1 = \{1, 2, 3, 4\}$ and edges $E_1 = \{\{1, 2\}, \{2, 3\}, \{3, 4\}, \{4, 1\}\}$.
   Let $G_2$ be a graph with vertices $V_2 = \{a, b, c, d\}$ and edges $E_2 = \{\{a, c\}, \{c, b\}, \{b, d\}, \{d, a\}\}$.
   Are $G_1$ and $G_2$ isomorphic? If so, find the isomorphism function $f$.

2. Write the adjacency matrix for a complete graph $K_3$ under the vertex ordering $v_1, v_2, v_3$.

---

### 💡 Solutions & Explanation

> [!NOTE]
> Here are the step-by-step solutions:
> 
> 1. **Isomorphism of $G_1$ and $G_2$:**
>    * Both graphs have 4 vertices and 4 edges.
>    * Both graphs have degree sequence $\{2, 2, 2, 2\}$ (they are cycles of length 4).
>    * Let's trace the cycle in $G_1$: $1 - 2 - 3 - 4 - 1$.
>    * Let's trace the cycle in $G_2$: $a - c - b - d - a$.
>    * We can map the vertices in cycle order:
>      $$f(1) = a, \quad f(2) = c, \quad f(3) = b, \quad f(4) = d$$
>    * Let's check if adjacency is preserved:
>      * $\{1, 2\} \in E_1 \iff \{f(1), f(2)\} = \{a, c\} \in E_2$ (Yes)
>      * $\{2, 3\} \in E_1 \iff \{f(2), f(3)\} = \{c, b\} \in E_2$ (Yes)
>      * $\{3, 4\} \in E_1 \iff \{f(3), f(4)\} = \{b, d\} \in E_2$ (Yes)
>      * $\{4, 1\} \in E_1 \iff \{f(4), f(1)\} = \{d, a\} \in E_2$ (Yes)
>    * All adjacencies are preserved, so $f$ is a valid isomorphism, and $G_1 \cong G_2$.
> 
> 2. **Adjacency Matrix for $K_3$:**
>    * In $K_3$, every vertex is connected to every other vertex except itself (no loops).
>    * The adjacency matrix is:
>      $$A = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$
