# Planar Graphs (Section 10.7)

---

In many graph applications (such as designing printed circuit boards or designing highway systems), we want to draw graphs in a two-dimensional plane such that no edges intersect or cross one another.

---

### 1. Planar Graph Drawings & Regions

> **Definition 1:** A graph is called **planar** if it can be drawn in a plane without any edges crossing (where a crossing is the intersection of two edges at a point other than a common vertex). Such a drawing is called a **planar representation** of the graph.

* **Regions (Faces):** A planar representation of a graph splits the plane into several regions (or faces), including one outer, infinite region.
* **Degree of a Region ($\text{deg}(R)$):** The number of edges that boundary the region $R$. If an edge is traversed twice while tracing the boundary (e.g., in a tree branch), it contributes 2 to the region's degree.
* **Sum of Region Degrees:** Adding up the degrees of all regions in a planar representation counts every edge exactly twice:
  $$\sum_{R} \text{deg}(R) = 2E$$

---

### 2. Euler's Formula

Leonhard Euler proved a fundamental relationship between the number of vertices, edges, and regions of any connected planar graph.

> **Theorem 1 (Euler's Formula):** Let $G$ be a connected planar simple graph with $V$ vertices, $E$ edges, and $R$ regions in a planar representation. Then:
> $$R = E - V + 2$$

#### 📝 Corollary 1 (Edge Bounds):
If $G$ is a connected planar simple graph with $E$ edges and $V \ge 3$ vertices, then:
$$E \le 3V - 6$$

#### Proof:
1. Every region in a simple graph must be bounded by at least 3 edges (since there are no loops or multiple edges). Thus, for each region $R$, $\text{deg}(R) \ge 3$.
2. Summing over all regions:
   $$2E = \sum_{R} \text{deg}(R) \ge 3R \implies R \le \frac{2}{3}E$$
3. Substitute this inequality into Euler's formula ($R = E - V + 2$):
   $$\frac{2}{3}E \ge E - V + 2 \implies -\frac{1}{3}E \ge -V + 2 \implies E \le 3V - 6 \quad \blacksquare$$

#### 📝 Corollary 2 (Minimum Degree Bounds):
If $G$ is a connected planar simple graph, then $G$ has at least one vertex of degree at most 5.

#### Proof:
1. Suppose all vertices in $G$ have degree $\ge 6$.
2. By the Handshaking Lemma:
   $$2E = \sum_{v \in V} \text{deg}(v) \ge 6V \implies E \ge 3V$$
3. This directly contradicts Corollary 1 ($E \le 3V - 6$), which states that $E$ must be strictly less than $3V$. Thus, there must exist a vertex with degree $\le 5$. $\blacksquare$

#### 📝 Corollary 3 (Girth Bounds / Bipartite Planar Graphs):
If $G$ is a connected planar simple graph with $E$ edges and $V \ge 3$ vertices, and has **no cycles of length 3** (e.g., a simple bipartite graph), then:
$$E \le 2V - 4$$

#### Proof:
1. Since there are no 3-cycles, every region must be bounded by at least 4 edges. Thus, $\text{deg}(R) \ge 4$.
2. Summing over all regions:
   $$2E = \sum_{R} \text{deg}(R) \ge 4R \implies R \le \frac{1}{2}E$$
3. Substitute this into Euler's formula:
   $$\frac{1}{2}E \ge E - V + 2 \implies -\frac{1}{2}E \ge -V + 2 \implies E \le 2V - 4 \quad \blacksquare$$

---

### 3. Nonplanarity Proofs

We can use the corollaries of Euler's formula to prove that the classic graphs $K_5$ and $K_{3,3}$ are not planar.

#### A. Proof that $K_5$ is Nonplanar:
* In the complete graph $K_5$, the number of vertices is $V = 5$, and the number of edges is $E = \binom{5}{2} = 10$.
* If $K_5$ were planar, it must satisfy Corollary 1:
   $$E \le 3V - 6 \implies 10 \le 3(5) - 6 = 9$$
* Since $10 \le 9$ is a contradiction, **$K_5$ is nonplanar**. $\blacksquare$

#### B. Proof that $K_{3,3}$ is Nonplanar:
* In the complete bipartite graph $K_{3,3}$, the number of vertices is $V = 6$, and the number of edges is $E = 3 \times 3 = 9$.
* Since $K_{3,3}$ is bipartite, it contains no cycles of odd length, which means it has no 3-cycles.
* If $K_{3,3}$ were planar, it must satisfy Corollary 3:
   $$E \le 2V - 4 \implies 9 \le 2(6) - 4 = 8$$
* Since $9 \le 8$ is a contradiction, **$K_{3,3}$ is nonplanar**. $\blacksquare$

---

### 4. Kuratowski's Theorem

How can we determine if any arbitrary graph is planar? Polish mathematician Kazimierz Kuratowski showed that $K_5$ and $K_{3,3}$ are the fundamental "building blocks" of all nonplanar graphs.

* **Elementary Subdivision:** Obtaining a new graph by removing an edge $\{u, v\}$ and adding a new vertex $w$ along with edges $\{u, w\}$ and $\{w, v\}$.
* **Homeomorphic Graphs:** Two graphs are homeomorphic if they can be obtained from the same graph by a sequence of elementary subdivisions.

> **Theorem 2 (Kuratowski's Theorem):** A graph is planar if and only if it does not contain a subgraph homeomorphic to $K_5$ or $K_{3,3}$.

### 🧠 Check for Understanding

1. A connected planar simple graph has 20 vertices, each of degree 3. How many regions does its planar representation have?
2. True or False: If a graph has $E > 3V - 6$, it must be nonplanar.

---

### 💡 Solutions & Explanation

> [!NOTE]
> Here are the step-by-step solutions:
> 
> 1. **Regions Calculation:**
>    * We have $V = 20$.
>    * Since every vertex has degree 3, the sum of degrees is $3 \times 20 = 60$.
>    * By the Handshaking Lemma: $2E = 60 \implies E = 30$.
>    * Using Euler's Formula:
>      $$R = E - V + 2 = 30 - 20 + 2 = 12 \text{ regions}$$
> 
> 2. **Edge Bounds Contrapositive:**
>    * **True.** The contrapositive of Corollary 1 is: "If a connected simple graph does not satisfy $E \le 3V - 6$, then it is not planar." Since $E > 3V - 6$ violates this condition, the graph must be nonplanar.
