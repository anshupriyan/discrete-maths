# Introduction to Graphs (Section 10.1 - 10.2)

---

Graphs are discrete structures consisting of vertices (or nodes) and edges that connect them. They are used to model networks (internet, social, neural), routing schedules, database relationships, and molecular bonds.

---

### 1. Types of Graphs & Terminology

A graph is formally defined by its vertex set and edge set:

> **Definition 1:** A **graph $G = (V, E)$** consists of $V$, a nonempty set of **vertices** (or nodes), and $E$, a set of **edges**. Each edge has either one or two vertices associated with it, called its **endpoints**. An edge is said to **connect** its endpoints.

#### Graph Classifications:
To model different scenarios, we vary whether edges have direction, allow loops (edges connecting a vertex to itself), or allow multiple edges between the same vertices.

1. **Simple Graph:** Undirected edges. No loops. No multiple edges (at most one edge connects any pair of distinct vertices).
2. **Multigraph:** Undirected edges. No loops. Multiple edges are allowed (multiplicity $>1$).
3. **Pseudograph:** Undirected edges. Loops and multiple edges are allowed.
4. **Directed Graph (Digraph):** Directed edges. Each edge is associated with an ordered pair of vertices $(u, v)$, starting at $u$ (initial vertex) and ending at $v$ (terminal vertex). Loops are allowed.
5. **Simple Directed Graph:** Directed edges. No loops. No multiple directed edges.
6. **Directed Multigraph:** Directed edges. Loops and multiple directed edges in the same direction are allowed.
7. **Mixed Graph:** Contains both directed and undirected edges.

#### Graph Terminology Summary:
| Type | Edges | Multiple Edges Allowed? | Loops Allowed? |
| :--- | :--- | :---: | :---: |
| **Simple graph** | Undirected | No | No |
| **Multigraph** | Undirected | Yes | No |
| **Pseudograph** | Undirected | Yes | Yes |
| **Simple directed graph** | Directed | No | No |
| **Directed multigraph** | Directed | Yes | Yes |
| **Mixed graph** | Directed and undirected | Yes | Yes |

### 2. Vertex Degrees and Neighborhoods

#### Undirected Graphs:
> **Definition 2:** Two vertices $u$ and $v$ in an undirected graph $G$ are called **adjacent** (or **neighbors**) if they are endpoints of an edge $e$. The edge $e$ is **incident with** $u$ and $v$, and $e$ is said to **connect** $u$ and $v$.

* **Neighborhood ($N(v)$):** The set of all neighbors of a vertex $v$:
  $$N(v) = \{u \in V \mid \{u, v\} \in E\}$$
  For a subset $A \subseteq V$, the neighborhood is $N(A) = \bigcup_{v \in A} N(v)$.

* **Degree ($\text{deg}(v)$):** The number of edges incident with a vertex, **except that a loop contributes twice** to the degree of its vertex.
  * **Isolated Vertex:** A vertex of degree $0$ ($\text{deg}(v) = 0$).
  * **Pendant Vertex:** A vertex of degree $1$ ($\text{deg}(v) = 1$).

#### Directed Graphs:
For directed edges $(u, v)$:
* $u$ is adjacent **to** $v$, and $v$ is adjacent **from** $u$.
* $u$ is the **initial vertex** and $v$ is the **terminal vertex**.
* **In-degree ($\text{deg}^-(v)$):** The number of directed edges with $v$ as their terminal vertex.
* **Out-degree ($\text{deg}^+(v)$):** The number of directed edges with $v$ as their initial vertex.
* *(Note: A directed loop contributes 1 to both in-degree and out-degree).*

---

### 3. The Handshaking Lemma

Adding up the degrees of all vertices in an undirected graph counts every edge exactly twice: once for each endpoint (and twice for loops).

> **Theorem 1 (The Handshaking Theorem/Lemma):** Let $G = (V, E)$ be an undirected graph with $m$ edges. Then:
> $$2m = \sum_{v \in V} \text{deg}(v)$$

#### 📝 Corollary (Parity of Odd Degree Vertices):
An immediate consequence of the Handshaking Lemma is:

> **Theorem 2:** An undirected graph has an even number of vertices of odd degree.

#### Proof:
Let $V_1$ be the set of vertices of even degree, and $V_2$ be the set of vertices of odd degree. Using the Handshaking Lemma:
$$2m = \sum_{v \in V} \text{deg}(v) = \sum_{v \in V_1} \text{deg}(v) + \sum_{v \in V_2} \text{deg}(v)$$
Since $2m$ is even, and the sum $\sum_{v \in V_1} \text{deg}(v)$ is even (since every term is even), the remaining sum $\sum_{v \in V_2} \text{deg}(v)$ must also be even.
Since every term in the sum $\sum_{v \in V_2} \text{deg}(v)$ is odd, the number of terms (which is the size of $V_2$) must be even to yield an even sum. Thus, there is an even number of odd-degree vertices. $\blacksquare$

#### Directed Handshaking Analogy:
For directed graphs, every directed edge must have exactly one start and one end:

> **Theorem 3:** Let $G = (V, E)$ be a directed graph. Then:
> $$\sum_{v \in V} \text{deg}^-(v) = \sum_{v \in V} \text{deg}^+(v) = |E|$$

### 4. Special Families of Simple Graphs

1. **Complete Graphs ($K_n$):** A simple graph containing exactly one edge between each pair of distinct vertices. It has $n$ vertices and $\binom{n}{2} = \frac{n(n-1)}{2}$ edges.
2. **Cycles ($C_n, n \ge 3$):** Consists of $n$ vertices $v_1, \dots, v_n$ and edges $\{v_1, v_2\}, \{v_2, v_3\}, \dots, \{v_{n-1}, v_n\}, \{v_n, v_1\}$.
3. **Wheels ($W_n, n \ge 3$):** Formed by adding an additional vertex (hub) to a cycle $C_n$ and connecting it to all $n$ cycle vertices (spokes). $W_n$ has $n+1$ vertices and $2n$ edges.
4. **$n$-Cubes ($Q_n$):** A hypercube graph where vertices represent the $2^n$ bit strings of length $n$. Two vertices are adjacent if and only if their bit strings differ in exactly one position.
5. **Bipartite Graphs:** A graph whose vertex set $V$ can be partitioned into two disjoint sets $V_1$ and $V_2$ such that every edge connects a vertex in $V_1$ to a vertex in $V_2$. No edge connects two vertices within $V_1$ or within $V_2$.
   * **Complete Bipartite Graphs ($K_{m,n}$):** Bipartite graph where there is exactly one edge between every vertex in $V_1$ (size $m$) and every vertex in $V_2$ (size $n$). It has $m+n$ vertices and $mn$ edges.

### 🧠 Check for Understanding

1. How many edges are there in a complete graph $K_{10}$?
2. If a graph has 8 vertices, each of degree 4, how many edges does it have?
3. Is it possible to construct an undirected graph with vertices of degrees $2, 3, 3, 3, 5$?

---

### 💡 Solutions & Explanation

> [!NOTE]
> Here are the step-by-step calculations:
> 
> 1. **Edges in $K_{10}$:**
>    * $|E| = \frac{10(10-1)}{2} = \frac{90}{2} = 45$ edges.
> 
> 2. **Edges from vertex degrees:**
>    * By Handshaking Lemma: $2m = \sum \text{deg}(v) = 8 \cdot 4 = 32$.
>    * So, $m = 16$ edges.
> 
> 3. **Constructing graph with degrees $2, 3, 3, 3, 5$:**
>    * The odd-degree vertices are $3, 3, 3, 5$.
>    * The number of odd-degree vertices is 4 (which is even).
>    * Since the number of odd-degree vertices is even, this degree sequence does not violate Theorem 2, and it is mathematically possible to construct such a graph.
