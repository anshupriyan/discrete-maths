# Euler and Hamilton Paths (Section 10.5)

---

Two classic path problems in graph theory ask whether we can traverse a graph covering either all edges or all vertices. These concepts have deep historical roots (the Seven Bridges of Königsberg) and serve modern applications in circuit design, digital coding, and route optimization.

---

### 1. Euler Paths and Circuits

An **Euler path** (or circuit) is a traversal that uses **every edge** of a graph exactly once.

> **Definitions:**
> * An **Euler path** in a multigraph $G$ is a simple path containing every edge of $G$.
> * An **Euler circuit** in a multigraph $G$ is a simple circuit containing every edge of $G$.

#### Undirected Graphs Existence Theorems:
For a connected undirected multigraph:

> **Theorem 1 (Euler Circuit Theorem):** A connected multigraph $G$ has an Euler circuit if and only if every one of its vertices has an even degree.

> **Theorem 2 (Euler Path Theorem):** A connected multigraph $G$ has an Euler path (and no Euler circuit) if and only if it has exactly two vertices of odd degree. The path must start at one of the odd-degree vertices and end at the other.

#### Directed Graphs Existence Theorems:
For a weakly connected directed multigraph:

* **Euler Circuit:** Exists if and only if for every vertex $v$, its in-degree equals its out-degree:
  $$\text{deg}^-(v) = \text{deg}^+(v)$$
* **Euler Path:** Exists if and only if for all but two vertices, their in-degree equals their out-degree, where:
  * One vertex $u$ has $\text{deg}^+(u) = \text{deg}^-(u) + 1$ (starts the path).
  * One vertex $v$ has $\text{deg}^-(v) = \text{deg}^+(v) + 1$ (ends the path).

---

### 2. Hamilton Paths and Circuits

A **Hamilton path** (or circuit) is a traversal that visits **every vertex** of a graph exactly once.

> **Definitions:**
> * A **Hamilton path** in a simple graph $G$ is a simple path that passes through every vertex of $G$ exactly once.
> * A **Hamilton circuit** in a simple graph $G$ is a simple circuit that passes through every vertex of $G$ exactly once (except the start and end vertex).

*Unlike Euler circuits, which are characterized completely by vertex degrees, there are no simple necessary and sufficient conditions to determine if a graph is Hamiltonian. Deciding if a graph has a Hamilton circuit is NP-complete.*

#### Sufficient Conditions for Hamilton Circuits:
While general classification is difficult, there are two classic theorems that guarantee a Hamilton circuit in simple graphs:

> **Theorem 3 (Dirac's Theorem):** If $G$ is a simple graph with $n$ vertices, where $n \ge 3$, such that the degree of every vertex in $G$ is at least $n/2$, then $G$ has a Hamilton circuit.
> $$\forall v \in V, \text{deg}(v) \ge \frac{n}{2}$$

> **Theorem 4 (Ore's Theorem):** If $G$ is a simple graph with $n$ vertices, where $n \ge 3$, such that for every pair of nonadjacent vertices $u$ and $v$ in $G$, the sum of their degrees is at least $n$, then $G$ has a Hamilton circuit.
> $$\forall u, v \in V \text{ (nonadjacent)}, \text{deg}(u) + \text{deg}(v) \ge n$$

### 3. Applications of Hamilton Circuits

#### A. The Traveling Salesperson Problem (TSP)
The **Traveling Salesperson Problem (TSP)** is a classic optimization problem. Given a list of cities and the distances between each pair, the goal is to find the shortest possible route that visits each city exactly once and returns to the origin city.
* **Graph Representation:** This is equivalent to finding a Hamilton circuit of minimum total weight in a complete weighted graph.
* **Complexity:** Because finding a Hamilton circuit is NP-complete, TSP is also NP-hard. Finding the absolute optimal route becomes computationally infeasible for a large number of cities, requiring approximation algorithms or heuristics.

#### B. Gray Codes
A **Gray code** is an ordering of the $2^n$ binary strings of length $n$ such that consecutive strings differ in exactly one bit position.
* **Graph Representation:** Consider the $n$-cube graph $Q_n$, where vertices represent bit strings of length $n$, and edges connect strings differing by one bit.
* **Relation:** A Gray code corresponds exactly to a **Hamilton circuit** on the $n$-cube graph $Q_n$. Since $Q_n$ is Hamiltonian for all $n \ge 2$, Gray codes of any length can always be constructed.

### 🧠 Check for Understanding

1. Determine if the following graphs have an Euler circuit, Euler path, or neither:
   * **Graph A:** A cycle graph $C_5$.
   * **Graph B:** A complete graph $K_4$.
   * **Graph C:** A complete bipartite graph $K_{2,3}$.

2. Does a complete graph $K_5$ satisfy Dirac's Theorem? Does it have a Hamilton circuit?

---

### 💡 Solutions & Explanation

> [!NOTE]
> Here are the step-by-step solutions:
> 
> 1. **Eulerian Properties of Graph A, B, and C:**
>    * **Graph A ($C_5$):** Every vertex in $C_5$ has degree 2. Since all degrees are even and the graph is connected, $C_5$ has an **Euler circuit**.
>    * **Graph B ($K_4$):** Every vertex in $K_4$ is connected to all other 3 vertices, so every vertex has degree 3 (odd). Since there are 4 vertices of odd degree (more than 2), $K_4$ has **neither** an Euler path nor an Euler circuit.
>    * **Graph C ($K_{2,3}$):** In $K_{m,n}$, the vertex set is split into a set of size $m$ (where each vertex has degree $n$) and a set of size $n$ (where each vertex has degree $m$).
>      * Here, 2 vertices have degree 3, and 3 vertices have degree 2.
>      * The number of odd-degree vertices is exactly 2.
>      * Therefore, $K_{2,3}$ has an **Euler path** (starting at one of the degree-3 vertices and ending at the other).
> 
> 2. **Hamiltonian Properties of $K_5$:**
>    * For $K_5$, the number of vertices is $n = 5$.
>    * Every vertex in $K_5$ has degree $n - 1 = 4$.
>    * We check Dirac's condition: $\text{deg}(v) = 4 \ge n/2 = 2.5$.
>    * Since $4 \ge 2.5$, Dirac's Theorem is satisfied, guaranteeing a Hamilton circuit.
>    * The circuit is simply a sequence visiting the 5 vertices in any order, e.g., $1-2-3-4-5-1$.
