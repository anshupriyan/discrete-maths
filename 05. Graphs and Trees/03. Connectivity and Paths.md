# Connectivity and Paths (Section 10.4)

---

Connectivity is a fundamental property of graphs that determines whether paths exist between vertices. It forms the basis of routing protocols, network reliability, social network analysis, and clustering algorithms.

---

### 1. Paths and Circuits

Informally, a path is a sequence of edges that allows you to travel from one vertex to another.

> **Definition 1:** A **path of length $n$** from $u$ to $v$ in an undirected graph $G = (V, E)$ is a sequence of $n$ edges $e_1, e_2, \dots, e_n$ of $G$ such that $e_1$ connects $v_0 = u$ and $v_1$, $e_2$ connects $v_1$ and $v_2$, and so on, with $e_n$ connecting $v_{n-1}$ and $v_n = v$.
> 
> * **Simple Path:** A path is **simple** if it does not contain the same edge more than once.
> * **Circuit:** A path is a **circuit** if it begins and ends at the same vertex (i.e., $u = v$) and has length $>0$.
> * **Simple Circuit:** A circuit is **simple** if it does not contain the same edge more than once.

*(Note: In directed graphs, paths and circuits must follow the direction of the directed edges, i.e., $e_i$ is a directed edge from $v_{i-1}$ to $v_i$).*

---

### 2. Connectedness in Undirected Graphs

An undirected graph is considered connected if we can get from any vertex to any other.

> **Definition 2:** An undirected graph $G$ is called **connected** if there is a path between every pair of distinct vertices in $G$.

* **Connected Component:** A maximal connected subgraph of $G$. A graph that is not connected (disconnected) can be partitioned into two or more disjoint connected components.
* **Theorem 1:** There is a simple path between any two distinct vertices of a connected undirected graph.

#### Articulation Points and Bridges:
* **Cut Vertex (Articulation Point):** A vertex whose removal (and the removal of all edges incident with it) increases the number of connected components in the graph.
* **Bridge (Cut Edge):** An edge whose removal increases the number of connected components in the graph.

---

### 3. Connectedness in Directed Graphs

Because directed edges are one-way, directed graphs have two levels of connectedness:

> **Definition 3:**
> * A directed graph is **strongly connected** if there is a directed path from $a$ to $b$ and from $b$ to $a$ for all vertices $a, b$ in the graph.
> * A directed graph is **weakly connected** if there is a path between every pair of vertices in its underlying undirected graph (the graph obtained by ignoring the direction of the edges).

* **Strongly Connected Components:** The maximal strongly connected subgraphs of a directed graph.

---

### 4. Paths and Isomorphism

Paths and cycles are structurally invariant. If two graphs $G_1$ and $G_2$ are isomorphic, the existence and lengths of paths and cycles must be identical:

* **Isomorphic Invariant:** If $G_1 \cong G_2$, then:
  1. If $G_1$ has a simple path of length $k$, then $G_2$ must have a simple path of length $k$.
  2. If $G_1$ has a simple circuit of length $k$, then $G_2$ must have a simple circuit of length $k$.
  3. The length of the shortest simple circuit (girth) must be the same in both.

This is a powerful tool to prove that two graphs are not isomorphic. If one graph has a 3-cycle ($C_3$) and the other does not, they cannot be isomorphic.

---

### 5. Counting Paths Between Vertices

We can determine the exact number of paths of a specific length between any two vertices using the powers of the graph's adjacency matrix.

> **Theorem 2 (Path Counting Theorem):** Let $G$ be a graph with adjacency matrix $A$ (with respect to the ordering $v_1, v_2, \dots, v_n$). The number of different paths of length $r$ from $v_i$ to $v_j$, where $r$ is a positive integer, is equal to the $(i, j)$th entry of $A^r$.

#### Proof (By Mathematical Induction):
1. **Base Case ($r = 1$):** The number of paths of length 1 from $v_i$ to $v_j$ is simply the number of edges between them, which is the $(i, j)$th entry of the adjacency matrix $A^1 = A$. This holds by definition of the adjacency matrix.
2. **Inductive Step:** Assume the theorem holds for paths of length $r$. We want to show it holds for paths of length $r+1$.
   * A path of length $r+1$ from $v_i$ to $v_j$ consists of a path of length $r$ from $v_i$ to some intermediate vertex $v_k$, followed by an edge (path of length 1) from $v_k$ to $v_j$.
   * By the inductive hypothesis, the number of paths of length $r$ from $v_i$ to $v_k$ is the $(i, k)$th entry of $A^r$, denoted as $a_{ik}^{(r)}$.
   * The number of edges from $v_k$ to $v_j$ is $a_{kj}$.
   * By the product rule, the number of such paths passing through $v_k$ is $a_{ik}^{(r)} \cdot a_{kj}$.
   * Since these intermediate vertices are mutually exclusive, we sum over all possible intermediate vertices $v_k$:
     $$\text{Total paths of length } r+1 = \sum_{k=1}^n a_{ik}^{(r)} a_{kj}$$
   * By definition of matrix multiplication, this sum is precisely the $(i, j)$th entry of the product matrix $A^r \cdot A = A^{r+1}$.
   * Thus, the theorem holds for $r+1$. By induction, the theorem is true for all positive integers $r$. $\blacksquare$

### 🧠 Check for Understanding

1. Consider a graph $G$ with vertices $\{v_1, v_2, v_3\}$ and adjacency matrix:
   $$A = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$
   Find the number of paths of length 2 from $v_1$ to $v_2$.

2. Find the cut vertices and bridges of the graph with $V = \{a, b, c, d\}$, $E = \{\{a, b\}, \{b, c\}, \{c, a\}, \{c, d\}\}$.

---

### 💡 Solutions & Explanation

> [!NOTE]
> Here are the step-by-step solutions:
> 
> 1. **Path Counting with Matrix Powers:**
>    * To find the number of paths of length 2, we calculate $A^2$:
>      $$A^2 = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1\cdot1+1\cdot1 & 1\cdot1 & 1\cdot1 \\ 1\cdot1 & 1\cdot1+1\cdot1 & 1\cdot1 \\ 1\cdot1 & 1\cdot1 & 1\cdot1+1\cdot1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$$
>    * The entry in the 1st row and 2nd column of $A^2$ is $1$.
>    * Therefore, there is exactly **1 path** of length 2 from $v_1$ to $v_2$.
>    * *Verification:* The path is $v_1 - v_3 - v_2$.
> 
> 2. **Cut Vertices & Bridges:**
>    * Removing vertex $c$ increases the number of connected components from 1 to 2 (one component containing $\{a, b\}$, and one containing $\{d\}$). Thus, $c$ is a **cut vertex**.
>    * Removing the edge $\{c, d\}$ disconnects the graph into $\{a, b, c\}$ and $\{d\}$. Thus, $\{c, d\}$ is a **bridge**.
